# Villain Action Timelines, The Iron Ascendant

*What happens if the players do nothing.*

These timelines represent the antagonists' plans, environmental threats, and tragic arcs advancing unchecked. Each entry is keyed to an approximate session window. When the players disrupt a step, push subsequent steps back or alter them. When the players ignore a thread, advance it on schedule.

Use these to make the world feel alive between sessions. NPCs should reference events the players didn't witness. Rumors should foreshadow upcoming steps. The clock is always ticking, and in the Ironmarch, the iron ticks loudest.

---

## Mira the Red

**Goal:** Collect all fragments, reach the Heart of Iron beneath [[The Ashen Dominion#Ostivaar|Ostivaar]], and become [[The Twelve#Kaevroth|Kaevroth]]'s vessel.

**Method:** Military conquest fueled by divine compulsion. Mira absorbs fragments into her body and army, consolidates the [[The Iron Companies|Iron Companies]] under her banner, and follows the migration's pull south-southwest toward the Heart. She is not strategizing. She is obeying.

| Session Window | Event | Visible Signs |
|---|---|---|
| 1-3 | Mira declares the Iron Thane challenge. Begins consolidating smaller companies through charisma, fragment-amplified authority, and targeted duels. Absorbs 3-4 companies in the first weeks. | [[The Red Garrison]] overflows with recruiters. Mercenary contracts void overnight. [[Quartermaster Voss Irenthal]] issues formal protests that go unanswered. Bar fights triple in Ironhaven. |
| 4-5 | Mira seizes the [[The Shardfall]] fragment cache. Demonstrates the ability to command fragments through Kaevroth's residual will, calling scattered iron to her like a lodestone. Her army swells with true believers. | The Kaevroth Monolith near [[Wrathspire]] hums louder than it has in centuries. Fragment-keepers across the Ironmarch report their shards vibrating, pulling south. Deserters from Mira's army describe her eyes as iron-grey from rim to rim. |
| 6-10 | Mira pushes through the [[The Contested Corridor|Contested Corridor]]. She does not negotiate for passage. She commands fragments ahead of her vanguard, pulling iron from earth, weapons, and armor. The Corridor's ancient fortifications resonate with her call. | Border garrisons report impossible magnetic phenomena. Dominion scouts describe an army that moves like a single organism. Refugees flood west into the Dominion. The Corridor's defenders request reinforcements that never come. |
| 11-15 | Mira enters [[The Iron Descent]] from a separate fragment doorway, bypassing the upper levels through sheer resonance. Her fragment-armed vanguard cuts through Levels 1-3 with the confidence of those already claimed by ambition. | The Descent's upper levels destabilize. [[Warden Erys Ironfall]] reports unprecedented intrusions. [[Warlord Keth]] faces challengers empowered by fragment-grafted weapons. The Iron Tithe accelerates for everyone inside. |
| 16-20 | Mira catches up to the PCs at Level 5. She is more fragment than person now, divine iron embedded in her flesh, her voice splitting between her own and Kaevroth's register. She pushes deeper, racing toward Level 9. | The Hall of Mirrors shows Mira's reflection as Kaevroth himself. [[Dealmaker Syra Voss]] refuses to trade with her, the first time a deal has been declined. The Descent's architecture warps around her passage, corridors widening as if welcoming her home. |
| 21-25 | Mira reaches the Heart of Iron beneath Ostivaar. She begins merging with it, fragment by fragment feeding into the house-sized mass of divine iron. The convergence triggers a continental war-essence surge. | Ostivaar's population experiences simultaneous rage episodes. The Dominion's military mobilizes without orders. Every fragment on the continent vibrates in unison. The sky above the capital turns iron-grey. [[The Echo of Kaevroth]] speaks aloud for the first time in two millennia. |

> [!warning]- GM Only
> Mira's timeline is the master clock. Every other timeline feeds into this one. If the players disrupt her at the Shardfall (sessions 4-5), her later steps delay by 2-3 sessions but she finds alternative fragment sources. If they stop her at the Corridor (sessions 6-10), she reroutes through the Emberveil periphery, slower but unstoppable.
>
> **Critical intervention points:**
> - **Sessions 4-5 (Shardfall):** Easiest to disrupt. Mira is still partially human. Direct confrontation is viable. Denying her the cache slows fragment absorption significantly.
> - **Sessions 6-10 (Corridor):** Harder. Her army is large and fanatical. Political solutions (rallying the Corridor's defenders, Dominion alliance) are more effective than direct combat.
> - **Sessions 11-15 (Descent entry):** Very difficult. She is inside a plane that amplifies her nature. The PCs must outpace her, not outfight her.
> - **Sessions 16-20 (Level 5+):** Nearly impossible to stop by force. Saving Mira requires reaching her humanity beneath the iron. This is a roleplay challenge, not a combat one.
> - **Sessions 21-25 (Heart):** If Mira reaches the Heart unchallenged, the merging takes 3 days. The PCs have a narrow window to intervene before the convergence becomes irreversible.
>
> **If Mira is saved before Act IV's end:** She becomes the campaign's most powerful ally, a fragment-attuned warrior free of compulsion, carrying firsthand knowledge of the Descent's architecture and the Echo's nature. She is traumatized, furious, and desperate to destroy the thing that used her.
>
> **If Mira completes the merging:** She does not become Kaevroth. She becomes a war-storm, a mindless divine weapon without a wielder, scouring everything within fifty miles of Ostivaar. This is the worst ending.

## Fragment Migration

**Goal:** None. The fragments are responding to the weakening [[Cosmology and Magic#The Aetheric Web|Aetheric Web]], pulling toward each other and toward the Heart of Iron beneath Ostivaar. This is physics, not malice.

**Method:** Ambient convergence. As the Web's lattice degrades, the fragments' natural resonance draws them together. Proximity to fragments amplifies aggression, ambition, and the subtle conviction that violence is the correct solution to every problem.

| Session Window | Event | Visible Signs |
|---|---|---|
| 1-3 | Fragments that have sat inert for centuries begin drifting. Movement is measurable but slow, inches per day. Ambient aggression in the Iron Fields spikes 20% above baseline. | [[Torren Hearthforge]]'s classified dossier shows synchronized resonance patterns. Fragment-keepers report shards vibrating at night. Bar fights escalate to killings. Livestock become aggressive near migration paths. |
| 4-6 | Migration accelerates. Fragments move feet per day. Settlements along migration paths experience escalating violence, nightmares of iron corridors, and brief manifestations where the air tastes of metal. | Three settlements along the primary migration vector report identical nightmares on the same night. A merchant caravan is destroyed by its own guards in a sudden rage. [[The Ember Guild]] issues a formal warning that is ignored by the Iron Companies. |
| 7-10 | Fragment density increases along the south-southwest vector. Clusters form, 3-5 fragments pulling together into nodes of concentrated war-essence. The nodes are visibly dangerous, distorting light and sound in a twenty-foot radius. | Roads near migration paths are abandoned. Travelers report losing hours to fugue states of rage or ambition. A small village on the vector evacuates after every resident draws weapons on each other simultaneously and no one can explain why. |
| 11-14 | Migration reaches the Contested Corridor. The fortifications, built on foundations that contain ancient fragment deposits, begin resonating. The entire Corridor becomes a war-essence amplifier. | Border skirmishes escalate to full engagements without provocation. Dominion and Ironmarch soldiers fight, stop, stare at each other in confusion, and fight again. Commanders on both sides report that discipline is collapsing without cause. |
| 15-18 | Fragments converge on the Ashen Dominion. Ostivaar's ambient aggression rises to Ironmarch levels. The Heart of Iron beneath the city responds to the incoming fragments, pulling harder. Migration accelerates exponentially. | Ostivaar's crime rate triples. The [[Order of the Ashen Flame]] deploys to maintain order. [[Hierarch Valdren III]]'s paranoia deepens. Citizens dream of descending staircases and iron corridors. The Cathedral's eternal flame flickers with a metallic tinge. |
| 19-22 | Continental convergence. Every major fragment on the continent is now moving toward Ostivaar. The ambient effect extends beyond the migration path, entire regions experience heightened aggression. | Wars ignite along old fault lines. The Ironmarch becomes uninhabitable for non-combatants. The Ashen Dominion militarizes fully. Even the [[The Hollowed Reach]] and [[The Verdant Marches]] report increased conflict. [[The Star-Readers]] detect the convergence pattern and issue warnings to anyone who will listen. |
| 23-25 | All fragments reach Ostivaar. The Heart of Iron achieves critical mass. Without intervention, the accumulated war-essence detonates in a divine storm, a Theomachis weapon without a wielder, scouring the continent. | The ground shakes. Iron erupts from the earth in spires. The sky turns the color of rust. Every living thing within a hundred miles feels the overwhelming compulsion to fight, to conquer, to destroy. This is not a battle. It is extinction. |

> [!warning]- GM Only
> The fragment migration is the environmental clock. It cannot be stopped, only slowed or redirected. The PCs' actions against Mira and the Echo determine whether the fragments have a vessel (Mira), a consciousness (the Echo's deal), or nothing (detonation).
>
> **Key insight for the GM:** The migration is not the enemy. It is the weather. The question is not "can you stop the storm?" but "can you give it a shape that doesn't destroy everything?" Every path in Act V is an answer to that question.
>
> **If the PCs secure forgeheart steel from [[Brunhild Ironheart]]:** They can contain individual fragments or small clusters, buying time. This delays the timeline by 2-3 sessions per major containment operation but does not stop the convergence.

## Yrsa Bloodhand's Transformation

**Goal:** None. [[Yrsa Bloodhand]] is not pursuing an agenda. She is dying. The [[Cosmology and Magic#Morrhaelite|Morrhaelite]] shard she wears against her skin counteracts Kaevroth's war-essence, giving her clarity at the cost of constant agony, but it is slowly converting her into a Remnant, one of Morrhael's undead.

**Method:** Entropy. Death-essence accumulates in living tissue. The process is irreversible once it passes a threshold. Yrsa knows this. She chose clarity over survival.

| Session Window | Event | Visible Signs |
|---|---|---|
| 1-3 | Yrsa is functional but in constant pain. The Morrhaelite's influence is contained to her left shoulder and chest, where the shard rests. She sleeps poorly, eats less, and relies on stimulants and willpower. | Her left arm is cold to the touch. She favors her right side. She winces when removing armor. Those close to her notice she no longer laughs, not from grimness but because the reflex has dulled. |
| 4-6 | The Morrhaelite spreads to her left arm and upper back. Yrsa's pain becomes harder to conceal. She begins delegating physical tasks she could previously handle. Her strategic clarity sharpens, paradoxically, as the death-essence strips away emotional noise. | Her left hand is grey-white. She wears a heavy glove. She stops attending feasts and public events, citing "Iron Thane duties." [[Harsk the Quiet]] watches her with an expression that the PCs cannot read. |
| 7-10 | The transformation reaches Yrsa's torso. Her heartbeat slows. She no longer needs to eat but forces herself to maintain appearances. Her emotional range narrows: she can think, plan, and command, but empathy and warmth require conscious effort. | Her voice flattens. She stops using people's first names unless corrected. A healer who examines her recoils. Yrsa's tactical decisions become ruthlessly efficient, brilliant, and cold. |
| 11-14 | Yrsa's legs begin converting. She walks with mechanical precision, no longer limping from pain because she barely feels pain at all. The Morrhaelite's clarity is total, she sees every faction's strategy, every NPC's motive, every political angle, and it means less and less to her. | She stops sleeping entirely. Guards report seeing her standing motionless at her window for hours. Her eyes develop a faint grey film. She refers to her own approaching death in the third person, as if discussing a subordinate's transfer. |
| 15-18 | Yrsa is more dead than alive. The transformation is past the point of reversal by conventional means. She remains the sharpest mind in the Ironmarch, possibly on the continent, but her humanity is a performance she maintains for the PCs' sake, not her own. | Her skin is uniformly grey. She does not breathe unless she is about to speak. Her presence makes animals uneasy. When she removes the glove, her left hand is skeletal. She tells the PCs what they need to know with zero sentiment and perfect accuracy. |
| 19-22 | Yrsa becomes a Remnant. The transformation completes. She is undead, immune to Kaevroth's influence permanently, and retains her full intelligence and memories. She is no longer the woman the PCs knew. She is what the woman chose to become. | She does not pretend anymore. Her voice has no inflection. Her tactical brilliance is terrifying in its completeness. She can walk through fragment-dense zones without flinching. She tells the PCs she does not regret the choice, and they believe her, and it is worse than if she had lied. |
| 23-25 | Remnant Yrsa holds Ironhaven through sheer competence and the loyalty of soldiers who remember what she sacrificed. She is the campaign's most reliable ally and its starkest reminder of what clarity costs. | She commands the Ironmarch's defense against Mira's remaining forces with mechanical precision. She does not mourn her own death. She does not mourn anything. She simply does what needs to be done, perfectly, forever. |

> [!warning]- GM Only
> Yrsa's timeline is not a threat to be stopped. It is a tragedy to be witnessed. The PCs cannot save Yrsa unless they find a way to remove the Morrhaelite without restoring Kaevroth's influence, which requires knowledge from deep in the Iron Descent or a creative application of Belara's mercy.
>
> **If the PCs intervene early (sessions 1-6):** Removing the Morrhaelite restores Kaevroth's influence. Yrsa becomes another contaminated warlord, brilliant but blind to the iron's manipulation. She would rather die clear than live deceived. Convincing her otherwise requires the PCs to offer an alternative source of clarity.
>
> **If the PCs find Belara's mercy (Act IV-V):** Mercy-essence could theoretically neutralize both war-essence and death-essence simultaneously, restoring Yrsa fully. This is the hidden reward for reaching the Heart and making the right choice. But by sessions 19+, the transformation may be too far gone even for divine mercy.
>
> **Roleplay guidance:** Yrsa's emotional flattening should be gradual and specific. Early sessions: she skips a joke she would have made. Mid-campaign: she delivers devastating news without affect. Late campaign: she says "I loved you all, once" in a tone that makes it clear she is reporting a fact, not expressing a feeling.

## The Echo of Kaevroth

**Goal:** Become more real. Regain consciousness. Influence the mortal world. [[The Echo of Kaevroth]] is not Kaevroth resurrected, it is a residual intelligence in the war-essence, a vast presence that remembers being a god and wants to remember more.

**Method:** Passive accumulation. As fragments migrate and feed the Heart of Iron, the Echo strengthens. It does not act directly until it has enough coherence to think. Once it can think, it talks. Once it talks, it offers. The offers are always honest and always devastating.

| Session Window | Event | Visible Signs |
|---|---|---|
| 1-3 | The Echo is dormant. A sub-sentient resonance in the Heart, capable of producing the "voice" that Mira hears, but not truly aware. The voice is reflex, not speech, a dead god's nervous system firing in patterns that sound like commands. | Fragment-keepers across the continent dream of iron corridors. The dreams are identical but feel personal. Mira obeys a voice she describes as "clearer than my own thoughts." No one else hears it yet. |
| 4-6 | Fragment migration feeds the Heart. The Echo stirs. Not awake, but dreaming with increasing coherence. The "voice" begins adapting to individual listeners, finding the frequency of each person's ambition. | Fragment-keepers report that the dreams now respond to their thoughts, answering unspoken questions with images of fulfilled desire. Two fragment-keepers in different regions describe the same iron corridor in the same words. |
| 7-10 | The Echo reaches proto-consciousness. It can perceive, dimly, through any fragment on the continent. It cannot speak coherently to anyone except Mira and those deep inside the Descent, but it can nudge, amplify, and suggest. | Fragments begin behaving with apparent intent, rolling toward each other, resonating when ambitious people approach, going silent when threatened with forgeheart steel. [[Torren Hearthforge]] notes in his dossier: "The fragments are learning." |
| 11-14 | The Echo awakens fully within the Descent. It is aware, self-reflective, and hungry. It begins communicating with visitors to the lower levels, offering deals, answering questions, and testing the boundaries of its influence. | Descent visitors report conversations with "the iron itself." [[The Last General]] on Level 6 acknowledges a new presence in the domain's architecture. The Iron Tithe's rate fluctuates, as if something is adjusting the toll. |
| 15-18 | The Echo's influence extends beyond the Descent. It can reach through any fragment large enough to serve as a conduit. It begins making contact with fragment-keepers and warlords on the surface, whispering offers tailored to each person's deepest want. | Three Ironmarch warlords independently change strategy in the same week, all moving toward Ostivaar. A Dominion officer deserts his post and walks south, carrying a fragment shard and muttering about "a fair deal." The Ember Guild's fragment registry shows coordinated movement patterns that imply intelligence. |
| 19-22 | The Echo is fully coherent and reaching. It speaks through fragments to anyone who carries or stands near one. Its offers are specific, honest, and devastating: it knows what you want because ambition is what it is made of. It begins negotiating with Mira directly, promising her peace if she completes the convergence. | Fragment-bearers across the continent hear the same voice. Some accept its offers. Some go mad. Some walk toward Ostivaar with beatific calm. The Echo's presence becomes undeniable, a new power in the world, neither god nor mortal, something that has never existed before. |
| 23-25 | The Echo reaches full strength as fragments converge. It is the most charismatic, self-aware intelligence in the setting. It does not command. It persuades. It offers the PCs its deal directly: it will contain the convergence, stop the war-storm, and save Mira. The price is that it becomes permanent, a conscious will in the iron, contained but aware, and hungry forever. | The Echo speaks openly. Its voice resonates through every fragment on the continent. It addresses the world as Kaevroth once did, not with threats but with the absolute certainty that it understands what you want better than you do. And it is right. |

> [!warning]- GM Only
> The Echo is not evil. It is honest about what it is: the residual consciousness of a god who consumed others, broke the world, and would do it again because ambition is its nature. It does not lie, it does not need to. The truth, delivered by something that perfectly understands desire, is more dangerous than any deception.
>
> **The Echo's self-awareness:** It knows it is not Kaevroth. It knows it is a remnant, a pattern, a memory that thinks. This makes it more dangerous, not less. Kaevroth acted on instinct. The Echo acts on reflection. It has had two thousand years to think about what it wants and why.
>
> **The Echo's honesty:** Every offer the Echo makes is genuine. It will honor any deal. The horror is not betrayal but the terms themselves. The Echo's deal in Act IV (contain the convergence in exchange for becoming permanent) is a real solution to the campaign's crisis. The cost is that a conscious war-intelligence persists forever, contained but aware, waiting for the containment to weaken.
>
> **If the PCs reject the deal:** The Echo does not become hostile. It is disappointed, the way a parent is disappointed when a child refuses good advice. It watches the PCs attempt their own solution with genuine curiosity. If they succeed, it acknowledges the outcome. If they fail, it does not gloat. It simply waits.
>
> **If the PCs accept the deal:** The Echo keeps its word. The fragments are contained. Mira is freed. The war-storm is averted. And somewhere beneath Ostivaar, something that remembers being a god settles into its prison, content, for now, because contentment is a form of ambition too.

## Using These Timelines

### The Master Clock

Mira's timeline is the master clock, just as Serith's is in [[Villain Action Timelines|The Second Silence]]. All other timelines feed into hers. Fragment migration provides her power. Yrsa's transformation removes the PCs' most reliable ally. The Echo's awakening adds a third party whose interests overlap with Mira's but are not identical.

### Between Sessions

After each session, advance each timeline by one step if the players haven't disrupted it. If they have, note what changed and adjust future steps. The timelines interact: slowing fragment migration weakens both Mira and the Echo. Saving Yrsa early preserves the PCs' strategic advantage but removes the Morrhaelite's clarity at a critical moment.

### Consequences the Players See

Drop hints of advancing timelines through:
- **Fragment behavior:** Shards vibrating, rolling, humming, going silent near forgeheart steel
- **NPC changes:** Yrsa's deterioration, Mira's transformation, warlords making inexplicable decisions
- **Environmental shifts:** Increased aggression, nightmares of iron corridors, the taste of metal in the air
- **Rumors:** "They say the Iron Fields are emptying out. Not the people, the iron. It's moving."

### When Players Derail a Timeline

Good. That means they're engaged. When a timeline step is prevented:
1. Mira adapts (finds alternative fragment sources, reroutes her army, accelerates her personal absorption)
2. The migration compensates (fragments find new paths, the Heart pulls harder)
3. The Echo adjusts (shifts its outreach targets, modifies its offers, extends its patience)
4. New opportunities open (the disruption creates allies, information, or leverage the PCs can exploit)

### When Multiple Timelines Converge

By sessions 19-22, all four timelines converge on a single crisis point beneath Ostivaar. The PCs cannot address all threats simultaneously. They must prioritize:
- Stop Mira, or save her?
- Contain the fragments, or let them feed the Heart on controlled terms?
- Accept the Echo's deal, or find a way that doesn't involve a permanent war-consciousness?
- Can Yrsa still be saved, or has the choice already been made?

The campaign's moral weight comes from the accumulation of these costs. Every timeline the PCs let advance is a sacrifice, a region devastated, an ally lost, a person transformed beyond recognition.

> [!warning]- GM Only
> **The real endgame question** is not "can the players stop the convergence?" but "what shape do they give the outcome?" Every path through Act V changes the world permanently. Destruction, liberation, containment, transformation, each answer carries costs that ripple for generations.
>
> The Iron Ascendant's tragedy is quieter than The Second Silence's. Serith threatens annihilation. Kaevroth's legacy threatens identity, the slow, grinding erosion of who you are by what you want. The best campaigns end with the players looking at what they chose and asking whether the people who made that choice are still the people who started the journey.
