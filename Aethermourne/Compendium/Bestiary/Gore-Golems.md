## Description

Gore-Golems are what happens after the fighting stops. On the Proving Grounds, nothing is wasted. When a battle ends, whether between [[Iron Wraiths]], wandering souls, or unlucky travelers, the dead begin to move. Not as undead, not as spirits, but as raw material. Bones, armor, flesh, weapons, all of it slides across the iron ground as though dragged by invisible hands, converging into a central mass that compresses, folds, and reshapes itself into something new.

The result is a Gore-Golem: a hulking construct assembled from the detritus of combat. They stand eight to twelve feet tall, their bodies a compacted mass of broken weapons, shattered armor, and organic matter fused together by residual war-essence. They are not undead. There is no animating soul, no necromantic spark, no ghost trapped inside. They are recycled material given crude purpose by the Proving Grounds' self-maintaining architecture, [[The Twelve#Kaevroth|Kaevroth]]'s domain cleaning up after itself and turning the waste into something useful.

They smell of hot iron and old blood. Their surfaces shift as components settle into place, a sword hilt protruding from a shoulder, a ribcage forming a crude breastplate, a jawbone serving as a knee joint. They are grotesque, not because they are malicious, but because they are efficient.

## Behavior & Tactics

Gore-Golems serve a single function: maintain the Proving Grounds as a battlefield. They patrol in slow, ponderous circuits, clearing debris, smoothing terrain, and eliminating anything that is not fighting. A creature engaged in combat is ignored, even assisted, as the Golem herds fleeing opponents back into the engagement zone. A creature at rest is a problem. Gore-Golems interpret peace as malfunction and will attack non-combatants to restart the cycle.

In combat, they are slow but devastating. Their strikes carry the combined mass of dozens of bodies compressed into a single fist. They do not feel pain, do not tire, and do not stop unless physically destroyed, at which point their remains begin the reassembly process, joining the next Golem to form in the area. Destroying a Gore-Golem permanently requires scattering its components widely enough that the reassembly mechanism cannot gather them, or burning the organic components to ash.

Their greatest tactical asset is inevitability. They are not fast enough to catch a running target, but they do not need to be. They know the Proving Grounds. They know where the paths narrow, where the terrain forces travelers into killboxes, where the Iron Wraith patrols will drive fleeing prey back toward them. They are patient in the way that machinery is patient.

> [!warning]- GM Only
> Gore-Golems raise an uncomfortable question: if the Proving Grounds recycles the dead, what happens to PCs who die here? The answer is yes, a fallen PC's body will be incorporated into the next Gore-Golem. This creates a ticking clock for resurrection or retrieval, and a deeply unsettling moment if the party later encounters a Golem wearing their friend's armor.
>
> **Tactical Note.** Gore-Golems are best used as environmental pressure rather than direct combat encounters. They force the party to keep moving, to avoid prolonged rest, and to think twice about leaving bodies behind.

## Encounter Notes

> [!example] Sample Encounter
> The party has just survived a fight with Iron Wraiths. They are wounded and want to rest. Within minutes, the fallen Wraiths' remains begin sliding across the ground toward a central point thirty feet away. The components stack, compress, and rise. A Gore-Golem assembles itself from the very enemies the party just killed, incorporating their weapons and armor into its body. It turns toward the party. They are not fighting. That is a problem.
>
> **Twist:** The Golem is wearing a distinctive piece of equipment the party recognizes, something from a previous encounter or an NPC they knew. The Proving Grounds have been recycling for a long time.
