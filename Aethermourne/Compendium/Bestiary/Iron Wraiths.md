## Description

Iron Wraiths are soldiers who never received the order to stand down. They march the Proving Grounds in formations that have not broken in two thousand years, executing battle plans from the Theomachis with mechanical precision. They wear the armor of the Age of Ash, blackened plate scored with war-glyphs that still faintly glow with [[The Twelve#Kaevroth|Kaevroth]]'s residual power. Their bodies beneath the armor are not flesh but compacted war-essence, a dense, iron-grey substance that holds shape through sheer force of expired command.

They do not speak. They do not negotiate. They do not recognize that the war is over. To an Iron Wraith, the Theomachis is still happening. Every creature they encounter is either an ally following the same battle plan or an enemy to be destroyed. Since no living creature knows the formations they are executing, everyone defaults to enemy. Their movements are eerily coordinated: an entire column will turn in unison, raise weapons in unison, charge in unison, as though a commander none can see is still issuing orders.

## Behavior & Tactics

Iron Wraiths fight as trained soldiers, not as monsters. They hold shield walls. They rotate wounded members to the rear. They exploit terrain, flank, and use combined arms. A formation of Iron Wraiths is more dangerous than an equivalent number of individual foes because they fight as a unit with two millennia of muscle memory.

Their war-glyphs pulse brighter during combat, and proximity to multiple active Wraiths creates a cumulative effect: a low, subsonic vibration that induces panic in the untrained and battle-fury in those with martial experience. Veterans and fighters feel the pull to join the formation, to fall into step, to follow the phantom orders. Resisting this compulsion requires recognizing it for what it is, the echo of a dead god's charisma.

Iron Wraiths cannot be reasoned with, but they can be outmaneuvered. Their battle plans are fixed. Once committed to a tactical pattern, they execute it regardless of whether it still makes sense. A formation that begins a flanking maneuver will complete it even if the enemy has moved. A shield wall will hold position against a threat that has already withdrawn. Predictability is their greatest weakness, provided you survive long enough to identify the pattern.

> [!warning]- GM Only
> Iron Wraiths are the backbone of the Proving Grounds' danger. They should feel like fighting a disciplined military force, not a swarm of undead. Use real tactical language when describing their movements. The subsonic battle-fury effect is a good mechanical hook: martial PCs must resist the urge to join the formation, while non-martial PCs experience fear.
>
> **Escalation.** Wraiths that "win" a battle (destroy all opposition) absorb the defeated's equipment and war-essence, growing slightly stronger. A Wraith formation that has been winning for centuries is significantly more dangerous than a fresh one.

## Encounter Notes

> [!example] Sample Encounter
> The party crests a ridge on the Proving Grounds and sees a column of forty Iron Wraiths marching in perfect formation across the iron plain below. They are executing a flanking maneuver on nothing, wheeling around an enemy position that no longer exists. The party can avoid them by staying still, as the formation's battle plan does not include this ridge. But between the party and their destination, the Wraiths' patrol route crosses the only viable path.
>
> **Complication:** A detachment of six Wraiths has broken from the main column and is executing a scouting pattern that will bring them directly to the party's position within minutes. The party must decide: fight six now, or wait and risk forty.
