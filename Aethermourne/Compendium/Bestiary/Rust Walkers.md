# Rust Walkers

> [!info]
> | | |

> | **Origin** | Former mortals transformed by prolonged exposure to the Descent's war-essence |
> | **Threat Level** | Variable (minor to serious, depending on stage) |
> | **Associated God** | [[The Twelve#Kaevroth|Kaevroth]] |

## Description

Rust Walkers are the Descent's slow work made visible. They are former mortals, visitors, prisoners, the lost, whose bodies have been gradually replaced by iron through prolonged exposure to the domain's saturating war-essence. The transformation begins at the extremities: fingertips darken to rust-brown, joints stiffen, skin takes on a metallic sheen that catches the light wrong. In these early stages, the Rust Walker is still a person. They can speak. They can remember their name, their home, the moment they entered the Descent. They can feel the iron creeping inward with every passing day.

In middle stages, the transformation reaches the torso and face. Movement becomes labored, accompanied by the grinding sound of metal on metal where joints once flexed smoothly. Speech slurs as the jaw stiffens. Expressions freeze into iron masks that approximate the face the person once wore but cannot replicate its warmth. In late stages, the Rust Walker is an iron statue that moves, grinding and groaning with every step, recognizably human in shape but wrong in every detail. The proportions are slightly off, the fingers too long, the stance too rigid, the gait a mechanical approximation of walking that no living body would produce. They do not speak. Whether they still think is unknown, and asking the question is one of the Descent's quieter cruelties.

## Behavior & Tactics

Early-stage Rust Walkers behave as desperate mortals. They seek help, companionship, and escape, all of which accelerate their transformation because each of these is a form of ambition. They are heartbreaking to encounter: lucid enough to describe what is happening to them, aware enough to feel the iron replacing their bones, and helpless to stop it. Some have developed strategies to slow the transformation, meditating to reduce desire, avoiding strong emotions, refusing to want anything at all. These individuals are recognizable by their deliberate stillness and the careful, measured way they speak, as if each word is rationed.

Late-stage Rust Walkers are essentially automatons. They patrol the Descent's corridors in repetitive patterns, their movements synchronized with the domain's rhythms. They are not hostile unless provoked, but their bodies are now iron, and a swing from a Rust Walker's arm carries the weight and hardness of a warhammer. They respond to stimuli with a delay, as if processing input through a mechanism rather than a mind. Groups of late-stage Rust Walkers sometimes move in formation, unconsciously echoing the military patterns that Kaevroth's war-essence imprints on everything it touches.

> [!warning]- GM Only
> **Campaign Use.** Rust Walkers are the Descent's most effective horror: they show PCs their own possible future. Every session spent in the Descent should include at least one Rust Walker encounter, calibrated to the party's current depth. On Level 1, they meet an early-stage Walker who can tell them what is happening and beg for help. On Level 5, they meet a mid-stage Walker who can barely speak but tries. On Level 8, they meet a late-stage Walker who might have been a person they spoke to earlier.
>
> **Mechanical Tie-In.** The Rust Walker transformation is the physical manifestation of the [[The Iron Descent#The Descent's Hunger|Iron Tithe]]. PCs who spend extended time in the Descent should notice early symptoms: a metallic taste, stiffness in the fingers, dreams of iron. This gives the Tithe a visible, tangible consequence that escalates with each session.

## Encounter Notes

> [!example] Sample Encounter
> The party finds a figure sitting against a wall on Level 3, legs extended, head bowed. Her hands are iron from the wrists down, dark and pitted with rust. She looks up when addressed. "How long have you been here?" she asks, and when the party answers, she nods slowly. "I said the same thing when I arrived. 'Just passing through.' That was, I think, four months ago. Maybe longer. I've stopped counting because counting means I still care about time, and caring about anything accelerates it." She holds up her iron hands. "I was a scribe. My hands were everything." She flexes her fingers, and the sound of grinding metal fills the corridor.
