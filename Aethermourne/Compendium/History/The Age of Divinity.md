# The Age of Divinity

*No mortal dates. No reliable calendar. Only myth, fragment, and divine echo.*

Before the silence, the gods walked the world.

There were **Twelve**, not born, not made, but *present*, as though they had always been and simply chose to act. Together they shaped Aethermourne: its continents and seas, its skies and depths, the bones of the earth and the breath of the wind. Creation was a shared labour, and for an age beyond counting, [[The Twelve]] maintained their work in concert.

---

### The World Under the Gods

The gods were not abstract forces. They were *entities*, vast, alien, and present. Mortals lived in their shadows, built temples at their feet, and sometimes spoke with them face to face. The young civilizations of humans, elves, dwarves, and a dozen other ancestries grew under divine stewardship, guided and at times ruled by avatars that walked among them.

This was not worship from afar. A farmer brought a boundary dispute to [[The Twelve#Solvaen|Solvaen]]'s court and received a ruling in the god's own voice. A hunter planted seeds blessed by [[The Twelve#Thyrea|Thyrea]]'s hand and watched them take root overnight. A grandmother dying in winter knew that [[The Twelve#Morrhael|Morrhael]] would come for her gently, lantern in hand, to guide her through the passage between worlds. Faith was not belief. It was observation.

[[The Twelve#Kaevroth|Kaevroth]] inspired mortals to push beyond their limits, to build, to strive, to refuse mediocrity. He was the reason civilizations rose. His domain in the Empyrean, the [[The Iron Descent#Origins of the Iron Descent|Crucible Ascendant]], was a paradise of fair struggle, its challenges calibrated to each visitor's potential. A hundred mortal civilizations offered tribute to the God of Ambition, and in return he gave them the fire to become more than they were.

But there was a cost to paradise that no one spoke of. Mortals did not govern themselves because they did not need to. They did not innovate because the gods provided. They did not grieve long because Morrhael made death gentle. They did not struggle with uncertainty because [[The Twelve#Orenthas|Orenthas]] read the Loom of Fate and prophecy settled every question worth asking. The Age of Divinity was a world of children tended by parents who would live forever.

Or so everyone believed.

This matters, because when the gods fell, mortals were not prepared to live without them. They had never needed to be. The despair of the [[The Age of Ash|Age of Ash]] was not only grief for dead gods. It was the terror of children abandoned in a house they had never learned to maintain.

### The Gods Among Themselves

The Twelve were not colleagues. They were a family, with all that the word implies: love, jealousy, admiration, resentment, and the particular tenderness that comes from knowing someone across uncountable millennia.

[[The Twelve#Solvaen|Solvaen]] and [[The Twelve#Thyrea|Thyrea]] shared the deepest friendship among the Twelve. Order and Nature, seemingly opposed, but Solvaen understood that law needed wildness to prevent stagnation, and Thyrea understood that growth needed structure to prevent chaos. They disagreed constantly and respected each other absolutely. Of all the gods, Solvaen loved mortals the most. Of all the gods, Thyrea understood them best. Together they shaped the conditions under which mortal civilization could flourish without losing its connection to the wild. They died on opposite sides of the continent during the Theomachis, unable to reach each other. No detail of the war is crueler.

[[The Twelve#Morrhael|Morrhael]] and [[The Twelve#Belara|Belara]] worked in quiet tandem, rarely speaking but always coordinating, like two hands performing a single task. Belara eased suffering in life. Morrhael eased the passage out of it. Between them, they tended the mortal experience from cradle to grave with a gentleness that the other gods, occupied with grander concerns, often overlooked.

What theologians have forgotten, and what the [[The Cyclekeepers|Cyclekeepers]] of the Thornwild are only now rediscovering, is that [[The Twelve#Thyrea|Thyrea]] and [[The Twelve#Morrhael|Morrhael]] were collaborators. Not opposed forces, but complementary ones, tending the same world from different sides. Growth and passage. Bloom and rest. Life needed death to make room, and death needed life to have meaning. They understood this as a natural cycle. The Theomachis shattered that understanding along with everything else, and the modern world treats their domains as enemies. The truth is older and gentler.

[[The Twelve#Kaevroth|Kaevroth]] and [[The Twelve#Thalvor|Thalvor]] were both gods of making, and the tension between them foreshadowed everything. Thalvor built things that lasted. Kaevroth built things that consumed. For millennia the difference was creative friction, the forge and the battlefield, patience and hunger, each keeping the other honest. Thalvor was the first to sense Kaevroth's hunger shifting from ambition to predation. He said nothing, because he could not believe a fellow god would steal from his own family. That silence cost him his life.

[[The Twelve#Serith|Serith]] and [[The Twelve#Orenthas|Orenthas]] were the most dangerous pairing among the Twelve. Both saw patterns the others missed. Orenthas saw the future in the Loom. Serith saw the shape of hidden things through [[The Shattered Mirror|her Mirror]]. They circled the same truth from different angles, two vast intelligences whose conversations, when they had them, left the other gods uneasy. What passed between them in the final years before the war is the subject of every theological debate in Aethermourne, and none of those debates have reached a conclusion.

[[The Twelve#Vorrhyn|Vorrhyn]] was the family's disruptor, playful and mischievous, the god who ensured nothing calcified into permanence. He kept the Twelve from stagnation. He kept the world from growing too still. His nature chafed against any compact, any boundary, any rule that said *this far and no further*. The other gods loved him and found him exhausting. His complaint about the Empyrean Accord was legitimate: the Accord *was* stagnating. But the solution he chose destroyed everything.

[[The Twelve#Yvenne|Yvenne]] was proud, vast, and mercurial. The sea was not her domain. The sea was *her*. She did not govern the ocean; she inhabited it, and the tides were her moods, and the storms were her temper. She was the least interested in mortal affairs and the most dangerous when roused.

[[The Twelve#Aelindra|Aelindra]] was distant even then. She loved knowledge more than she loved people, and she knew this about herself, and it made her sorrowful. She watched the patterns of the world from the stars and recorded what she saw with meticulous care, because someone had to.

Somewhere beyond the Boundary, in [[Kavothei's Collection]], there exist songs from the last night before the Theomachis. Music, conversation, laughter. The emotional record of a divine family on the eve of its destruction. No mortal has heard them. Perhaps that is mercy.

### Serith Before the Fall

Before the betrayal, [[The Twelve#Serith|Serith]] was necessary.

She was the Goddess of Secrets, and the distinction between secrets and lies mattered to her more than anything. Lies corrupt the truth. Secrets *protect* it. Serith guarded the hidden knowledge that the world could not bear to see unveiled. She kept the confidences of gods. She watched the shadows where threats gathered before they had names. She saw through [[The Shattered Mirror|her Mirror]] every hidden thing in creation, and she bore the weight of that sight without complaint.

The other gods trusted her. They brought her their doubts, their fears, the things they could not say to one another face to face. She held all of it in the dark, because the dark was where dangerous truths were safest.

Her turn was not predatory. It was born of seeing too much. The Mirror showed her everything, including what [[The Twelve#Orenthas|Orenthas]] would not say aloud: that the Empyrean Accord had an expiration date woven into the Loom's deepest threads. That the divine cycle, for all its beauty, was not sustainable. That catastrophe was not a possibility but a certainty, the only question being when and how much would be left afterward.

She chose to act rather than wait. Whether this makes her a traitor or a prophet is the question the campaign exists to answer.

### The Empyrean Accord

To govern their interactions and prevent divine conflict from scouring the mortal world, the Twelve established the **Empyrean Accord**, a compact of divine law, unwritten but absolute. It defined the boundaries of each god's domain, governed interactions between their portfolios, and forbade the theft or consumption of another's essence. The Accord was built upon [[The Primordials#Achren|Achren]]'s primordial principle of separation, the metaphysical law that boundaries make existence possible, elevated to divine statute.

The Accord was not merely political. It was metaphysical. [[The Twelve#Orenthas|Orenthas]] wove it into the **Loom of Fate** itself, threading the compact through the destiny of every living thing. To break it required either the unanimous consent of all Twelve or an act of such magnitude that the Loom itself would need to be rewoven, an event that would ripple across every timeline and destiny simultaneously.

For uncounted millennia, it held.

### The Aetheric Web and the Loom of Fate

The two great collaborative works of the Age.

[[The Twelve#Nethys|Nethys]], Goddess of Magic, wove the **Aetheric Web**, a continent-spanning network of ley lines that channelled raw magical energy through the world like blood through veins. Every spell, every enchantment, every whisper of arcane power drew from the Web. It was her masterwork, and she wove it with the contribution of all Twelve, each god threading their domain's energy into the lattice. The Web was not Nethys alone. It was the Twelve's shared breath, their combined will made into infrastructure.

[[The Twelve#Orenthas|Orenthas]], God of Time, maintained the **Loom of Fate**, a metaphysical construct beyond mortal comprehension, said to weave the destiny of all living things. The Loom was Orenthas's alone, and the other gods accepted this because they trusted him. He spoke rarely, but when he did, his words were prophecy.

Both are now broken or unattended. The Web is wild, its channels fraying, its flow erratic. The Loom still turns, but its keeper is silent. The campaign of the [[Campaign Overview|Second Silence]] is, at its deepest level, about the consequences of a world whose infrastructure has lost its maintainers.

### The Erosion

The Accord did not fail all at once. It was *eroded*.

[[The Twelve#Kaevroth|Kaevroth]], God of War, grew restless within his domain. War alone was not enough, he hungered for dominion itself. In secret, he whispered to [[The Twelve#Vorrhyn|Vorrhyn]], God of Chaos, whose nature chafed against any compact, and to [[The Twelve#Serith|Serith]], Goddess of Shadow, who had seen the Accord's inevitable end in the patterns of her Mirror. Together, they formed the **Triumvirate of Ruin**, an alliance hidden behind millennia of smiles and silence.

The first violation was quiet. Kaevroth consumed a fragment of [[The Twelve#Thalvor|Thalvor]]'s divine fire, not enough to weaken the Forge-God noticeably, but enough to prove it could be done. Serith hid the theft in shadow. Vorrhyn kept the other gods' attention elsewhere with a minor crisis of his own engineering. It was a test. It worked. The Empyrean Accord was broken, and the other gods did not yet know.

But the test changed Kaevroth. Once he tasted stolen divinity, ambition became appetite. He began planning larger thefts, not because the Triumvirate needed more power, but because *he* did. The hunger was no longer strategic. It was biological. Serith noticed the shift and was troubled, but she had already committed to the path. She told herself the plan would hold. She told herself Kaevroth could be managed.

She was wrong.

[[The Twelve#Thyrea|Thyrea]] felt the wrongness first, a note missing from a chord she had heard since the beginning of creation. She brought her concern to [[The Twelve#Solvaen|Solvaen]], who investigated and found nothing. Serith had covered the tracks perfectly. Solvaen dismissed Thyrea's unease as a seasonal fluctuation in the natural order. He was wrong, and this failure of trust between the two closest gods haunted Thyrea until the day she died shielding the forests he would never see again.

> [!warning]- GM Only
> At some point before the open war, Serith went to [[The Twelve#Orenthas|Orenthas]]. Not to confess. To ask. She stood before the Loom of Fate and asked the God of Time what he saw in its threads. What end was written for the Twelve, for the Accord, for the world they had built together.
>
> Orenthas answered with silence.
>
> Serith interpreted the silence as confirmation that the cataclysm was indeed inevitable. That the Accord's end was woven into the Loom's deepest structure. That her choice to act, to control the destruction rather than wait for it, was not betrayal but mercy.
>
> In hindsight, the silence may have been grief. Or it may have been complicity. Evidence suggests Orenthas could have stopped the Theomachis. He chose not to. Whether he chose this because the Loom required it, because he agreed with Serith's diagnosis, or because even a god of fate cannot outrun his own nature, is the deepest unanswered question in Aethermourne's theology.
>
> Orenthas's final words, spoken as the war ended and the world fell silent, were: *"The silence comes, and in silence, the loom weaves what must be."* Then he went mute. He has not spoken since.

The last night before the war, the Twelve gathered. Whether by tradition or premonition, the accounts do not agree. [[The Twelve#Belara|Belara]] reportedly wept that morning, though she smiled through the evening. [[The Twelve#Morrhael|Morrhael]] prepared his lantern with particular care. [[The Twelve#Thalvor|Thalvor]] checked his forge one final time, adjusting tolerances that had not needed adjusting in millennia. [[The Twelve#Aelindra|Aelindra]] watched the stars that night and saw them flicker, just once, as if the sky itself had flinched. Whether these were premonitions or later mythologizing, no mortal can say.

By the time they understood, it was too late.

The Age of Divinity did not end when the first blow fell. It ended the moment Kaevroth realized that stolen divinity tasted good, and Serith decided that an ending could be a mercy.

> [!warning]- GM Only
> **Serith's True Motivation.** Serith did not join the Triumvirate out of ambition. She joined because she foresaw what Orenthas was hiding: the divine cycle was always destined to end in catastrophe. The Empyrean Accord was a delay, not a solution. The Theomachis, or something like it, was inevitable. Serith believed that acting was better than waiting, that a controlled destruction was preferable to an uncontrolled one. She was wrong about the "controlled" part. She was right about the inevitability.
>
> What she wants now, from her prison beneath the sea, is to finish what she started. She has spent two thousand years studying the threads of fate, and she has discovered how to unravel the [[Cosmology and Magic#The Loom of Fate|Loom of Fate]] entirely, to restart the divine cycle. New gods. New mortals. New everything. The old world, with all its accumulated ruin and sorrow, would be unmade.
>
> She genuinely believes this is necessary and merciful. The world is dying, slowly, but inevitably. The gods are dead or broken. Magic is failing. The mechanisms of reality are running down without maintenance. In a few more centuries, everything will simply stop.
>
> She is offering a mercy killing. An ending that is also a beginning.
>
> She is the campaign's primary antagonist, but she is not a simple villain. She may, in fact, be right. The players will need to decide.
