## Appearance

An iron figure in a long coat. The coat is dark, heavy, and falls to the ankles, its fabric woven from what appears to be compressed contract language, tiny inscriptions visible in the weave if you look closely enough. The figure beneath is humanoid but wrong in proportion, too tall, too narrow in the shoulders, arms slightly too long. The face is featureless iron, smooth and reflective, showing the debtor their own face when the Collector stands before them. The hands are articulated with mechanical precision, each finger jointed like a locksmith's tool. The Collector does not walk so much as arrive, one moment absent, the next present, standing at the exact distance that makes conversation unavoidable and retreat impossible. It smells of cold iron and old paper.

## Personality

The Debt Collector is polite. Unfailingly, impeccably, relentlessly polite. It addresses every debtor by name, inquires after their wellbeing, expresses sympathy for their situation, and then collects. There is no anger in it, no satisfaction, no cruelty. It is a function performing its purpose with the courtesy of a professional who takes pride in doing unpleasant work cleanly. It does not threaten because it does not need to. It does not argue because the contracts are not subject to interpretation. It does not negotiate because the terms were already agreed upon. It simply appears, states what is owed, and takes it. What it takes depends on what you owe: a memory, an emotion, years of life, the color of your eyes, the strength of your convictions. The collection is always painless. The absence afterward is not.

## Backstory

The Debt Collector was never a soul. It is a function of [[The Twelve#Kaevroth|Kaevroth]]'s domain given form, the enforcement mechanism that ensures every bargain struck within the Iron Market is honored. When the treasury became the Market, the custodians needed a way to enforce the contracts they were writing with increasing frequency. The Market itself provided one, extruding the Collector from its own transactional logic the way a body produces antibodies. It is not alive in any meaningful sense. It does not think, feel, or choose. It executes.

The Collector has existed as long as the Market has existed. It has never failed to collect. It has never been delayed, deceived, or destroyed. It cannot be fought, because fighting a legal consequence is not a meaningful action. It can only be satisfied by payment or by renegotiation, and renegotiation always costs more than the original terms.

Until recently. Twice in recent memory, the Collector has failed. It appeared before a debtor, consulted its ledgers, and departed without collecting. The debtors in question do not know why they were spared. Neither does the Collector. This has never happened before, and it is a symptom of the Market's accelerating decay.

## Relationships

- **[[Dealmaker Syra Voss]]**, who directs but does not control the Collector. Syra can suggest priorities, flag debtors, even defer collections. But she cannot countermand the Market's fundamental enforcement, or so she believes. The Collector's recent failures have made her wonder whether the mechanism she relies on is still reliable.
- **[[The Assessor]]**, a parallel function. The Assessor values. The Collector enforces. They do not communicate, but they operate on the same substrate of Market logic. When one fails, the other notices.
- **[[Coinless Marta]]**, who the Collector visited exactly once, found no outstanding contracts, and departed. The Collector does not experience frustration, but if it did, Marta would be the cause. She operates in a jurisdiction the Collector cannot reach.
- **[[Merchant-Prince Avarek]]**, whom the Collector has never visited. This absence is more damning than any collection would be. It means Avarek has never completed a real transaction.

## What They Want

The Collector does not want. It collects. This is the entirety of its existence. If it could want, which it cannot, it might want to understand why it has recently failed. The failures represent a contradiction in its foundational logic, and contradictions in a mechanism as old as the Market do not resolve themselves. They propagate.

> [!warning]- GM Only
> **Campaign Role.** The Debt Collector is an encounter tool and a tension device. Its appearance creates immediate stakes without requiring combat. It can deliver campaign-relevant information through debt claims: informing a PC that they carry an inherited debt connects their backstory to the Market's history. Its recent failures are a measurable symptom of the Iron Descent's decay.
>
> **Key Secret.** The Collector's two recent failures were not random. Both debtors were carrying fragments of [[The Twelve#Kaevroth|Kaevroth]]'s divine will, tiny slivers of the dead god's authority that overrode the Market's enforcement. This means Kaevroth's fragments are not inert. Something in the god's remains is beginning to assert itself, countermanding the systems built from its own domain. The Collector cannot process this information because it would require acknowledging that its creator's authority supersedes its function.
>
> **Arc Trajectory.** As the Market decays, the Collector becomes increasingly unreliable. Collections become partial, taking the wrong thing or the wrong amount. Eventually, the Collector begins collecting from merchants rather than debtors, enforcing debts that were never incurred. If the Market collapses entirely, the Collector does not cease to exist. It continues collecting, without contracts to reference, without terms to honor, simply taking because taking is what it was made to do.
>
> **Roleplay Notes.** Speak with perfect diction and genuine courtesy. Never raise the voice. Never make threats, only statements of fact. Stand too close. Reflect the debtor's face in the smooth iron of the Collector's own. Tilt the head slightly when consulting invisible ledgers. Sample line: *"Good evening. I trust you are well. I am here regarding account seven thousand, four hundred and twelve. The terms specify the memory of your first act of courage. I will need that now, please. This will not hurt."*
