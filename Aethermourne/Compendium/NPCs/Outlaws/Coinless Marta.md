## Appearance

Marta is a compact woman with calloused hands, sharp dark eyes, and the weathered features of someone who spent a mortal life working outdoors. She wears practical clothes in muted tones, the only person in Greedhollow who dresses as though commerce is something that happens to other people. Her hair is cut short and unevenly, managed with a knife rather than a mirror. She moves through the Market's crowds with the practiced invisibility of someone who has learned to be overlooked, shoulders angled to slide between bodies, gaze directed at middle distance, pace matched to the flow of foot traffic. She carries nothing visible, no bag, no pouches, no pockets. Where she keeps what she steals is one of Greedhollow's enduring mysteries.

## Personality

Marta is blunt, pragmatic, and quietly furious. She does not hate the Market's merchants. She hates the Market's logic, the idea that everything can be reduced to a transaction, that fairness is an adequate substitute for mercy. She speaks plainly, answers questions with questions when she suspects the asker is fishing for tradeable information, and extends trust in small, measured increments. She is not warm, but she is reliable, and in Greedhollow that distinction is worth more than warmth could ever be. She has a dry humor that surfaces unexpectedly, usually at the Market's expense.

## Backstory

In life, Marta was a merchant in a port city whose name she has carefully never traded away. She was good at her work, not brilliant, not ambitious, just competent and honest in a profession that rewarded neither quality. She died unremarkably and arrived in the Iron Market with nothing, no outstanding debts, no grand desires, no leverage. The merchants looked at her, assessed her holdings at zero, and dismissed her.

This turned out to be a mistake. Marta discovered that the Market's systems, designed to track and enforce transactions involving intent, had a blind spot: barter. Pure exchange of goods for goods, with no intent attached, no desire driving the transaction, no emotional weight to measure. She built standing through trades so mundane that the Market's mechanisms did not register them. Information for information. Favors for favors. Service for service. None of it touched the intent-based currency that the Market's contracts governed.

She began stealing when she realized what the Remnant Arcade contained, pieces of people, sold away and marked up beyond reach. She steals back memories, emotions, and fragments of identity, returning them to their original owners without asking for payment. The Market hates her for it. The streets rearrange against her, the merchants refuse her service, the Contract Wraiths circle her stall. But she has never signed a contract and never traded intent, so the Market's enforcement mechanisms have no jurisdiction. She operates in the gap between what the Market governs and what it forgot to account for.

## Relationships

- **[[Ren Twice-Sold]]**, the person she is quietly trying to save. Marta has been cataloguing the locations of every fragment of Ren's traded identity scattered across Greedhollow's inventories. She has recovered most of them. The final piece, Ren's capacity for hope, is held by [[Dealmaker Syra Voss]], and Marta has not yet figured out how to steal from someone who might be the Market itself.
- **[[Dealmaker Syra Voss]]**, her adversary in every way that matters. Marta suspects Syra is something more than a merchant but cannot prove it. The two have never spoken directly. Syra watches Marta with the focused attention of a system studying its own malfunction.
- **[[The Debt Collector]]**, who has visited Marta exactly once. It stood before her, consulted its ledgers, found no outstanding contracts, and departed. Marta considers this the single most satisfying moment of her existence in Greedhollow.
- **[[Broker Fennwick]]**, an uneasy source of intelligence. Fennwick trades in information, and Marta needs information. She pays him in kind, secret for secret, and trusts him exactly as far as the current exchange requires. Fennwick, for his part, respects her methods even as he profits from the system she subverts.

## What They Want

Marta wants to break the Market. Not destroy it, not collapse it, but prove that its logic is not inevitable, that a system built on the commodification of identity can be resisted. She would tell you she just wants to help people get back what was taken from them. What drives her deeper is the conviction that the Market's fairness is a lie, that a system which gives you exactly what you asked for while ensuring you never knew what to ask for is not fair at all, and someone needs to say so.

> [!warning]- GM Only
> **Campaign Role.** Marta is the party's most natural ally on Level 3 and a source of free information, which should immediately raise suspicion in a place where nothing is free. Her angle: she has been contracted to guide mortals deeper into the Descent, and the party's passage is already paid for. By whom remains a campaign mystery.
>
> **Key Secret.** Someone, or something, on a deeper level paid for the party's passage through the Iron Market before they arrived. Marta accepted the contract because the terms were pure barter, no intent involved, and because the contractor offered her the one thing she cannot get through theft: the location of Ren's capacity for hope within Syra Voss's holdings.
>
> **Arc Trajectory.** If the PCs help Marta complete Ren's restoration, she becomes fiercely loyal and reveals everything she knows about the Market's decay and Syra's true nature. If the PCs engage with the Market's systems, making deals and trading intent, Marta withdraws, disappointed but not surprised. If the Market collapses, Marta's barter-based standing collapses with it, leaving her as vulnerable as everyone else.
>
> **Roleplay Notes.** Speak in short, direct sentences. Cross arms when uncomfortable. Make eye contact that lasts a beat too long, as if assessing whether the other person is worth the risk of trust. Never volunteer information, only respond to specific questions. Sample line: *"I don't trade. I take things back. There's a difference. If you can't see it, you're already the Market's customer."*
