# Laws of the Descent

> [!quote]
> *"It has rules. They are not fair. They are not negotiable. They are not survivable. But they are rules, and knowing them is the difference between dying in an hour and dying in a week."*
> , Arch-Reader Thessaly Vane, transcribed account

---

## Overview

The Iron Descent is not chaos. It is a divine domain, built by [[The Twelve#Kaevroth|Kaevroth]], the God of War and Ambition, and it operates according to principles as rigid and unyielding as its creator's iron body. These principles were once the fair, transparent rules of the Crucible Ascendant, where challenges were honest and rewards were earned. The Theomachis broke them into something darker, but the underlying structure remains.

Understanding these laws is the difference between a doomed visitor and a merely imperiled one. None of them can be circumvented. Some of them can be exploited.

---

## The Iron Tithe

For every day spent in the Descent, you lose something.

The Tithe begins with peripheral memories: the name of a childhood friend, the color of your mother's eyes, what your hometown smelled like in spring. These losses are subtle. Most visitors do not notice them until they try to recall something specific and find only a smooth, iron-grey blank where the memory should be.

As exposure continues, the Tithe escalates. After the memories go, it takes desires: hobbies you once enjoyed become meaningless, preferences dissolve, tastes flatten. Food in the Descent all tastes the same, not because the food lacks flavor, but because the Tithe has consumed your capacity to distinguish one flavor from another.

At the deepest levels, the Tithe consumes identity itself. Your name. Your purpose. Your face. Long-term inhabitants of the Descent do not forget who they are, the domain *takes* who they are, replacing selfhood with function. This is why the Descent's native inhabitants have titles instead of names, roles instead of personalities, positions instead of lives.

The Tithe accelerates with depth. A week on Level 1 might cost a handful of childhood details. A week on Level 5 might cost your sense of humor, your capacity for nostalgia, and the sound of your own name spoken in your mother's voice. A week on Level 9 might cost everything that makes you recognizable as yourself.

> [!example] Mechanical Suggestion: The Iron Tithe
> For each full day a PC spends in the Descent, the GM selects or randomly determines a memory, preference, or personality trait that the Tithe consumes. On Levels 1-3, these should be minor and peripheral (a childhood memory, a food preference, the name of a distant acquaintance). On Levels 4-6, the losses become significant (a relationship memory, a core hobby, a formative experience). On Levels 7-9, the Tithe targets identity (the PC's name, their motivation for adventuring, their emotional connection to another PC).
>
> **Recovery:** Tithe losses are not permanent if the PC leaves the Descent. Lost memories return over the course of weeks, fading back in like a bruise healing. But if a PC spends long enough that the Tithe reaches identity-level losses, recovery is uncertain. Some things, once taken, leave scars even when returned.

## The Gravity of Ambition

Movement between levels is governed by desire.

Wanting something pulls you deeper. The more specific and intense the want, the faster you descend. A vague wish to explore might shift you gradually over days. A burning need to find a specific person, recover a specific artifact, or achieve a specific goal can drag you down multiple levels in hours. The Descent reads desire like a compass needle, and it always points down.

Going up requires surrendering desire. Not suppressing it, not ignoring it, but genuinely releasing the want that brought you deeper. For most mortals, this is nearly impossible. The very act of wanting to leave is a form of desire, and the Descent uses it to hold you in place. The visitors who ascend most easily are those who have stopped caring about escape, which is its own form of defeat.

This is the Descent's most elegant trap: the motivation that drives you to enter is the same force that prevents you from leaving.

> [!example] Mechanical Suggestion: Ambition Checks
> When a PC takes an action driven by personal desire (pursuing a goal, seeking a reward, trying to find someone), the GM may call for an **Ambition Check**. On a failure, the PC is pulled one level deeper. On a success, they resist the pull but feel the domain's gravity intensify.
>
> **Ascending:** To move up one level, a PC must voluntarily surrender something they genuinely want. This cannot be faked. The domain knows. The sacrifice must be real: abandoning a quest goal, releasing a grudge, giving up an item of personal significance. The GM should make ascending feel costly and meaningful.

## Time Dilation

Time in the Descent is inconsistent, and deliberately so.

A day on Level 1 might correspond to a week in the mortal world. A year on Level 9 might be an hour outside. Or the reverse. There is no mathematical formula, no predictable ratio, no pattern that can be mapped or exploited. The domain adjusts its temporal flow according to principles that appear arbitrary but are, in fact, strategic.

The Descent manipulates time to maximize the Iron Tithe. When a visitor is accumulating useful ambition, time slows inside, giving the domain more opportunities to feed. When a visitor is close to escaping, time accelerates, burning through the Tithe faster. When a visitor has been fully consumed and no longer generates meaningful desire, time stops mattering entirely, which is why some inhabitants of the deeper levels have existed for what feels like millennia while only centuries have passed outside.

The practical effect for visitors is disorienting. You cannot know how long you have been inside. You cannot know how long you have been gone from the mortal world. Every assumption about duration is unreliable. Some expeditions that felt like weeks discovered they had been gone for hours. Others that felt like days discovered years had passed.

## The Resonance

When the Descent is active, Kaevroth's fragments in the mortal world pulse.

The pulse is detectable by anyone touching a fragment directly: a vibration, a warmth, a sudden spike of aggression or ambition that fades when contact is broken. [[The Ember Guild]] has developed instruments that can detect resonance at a distance, monitoring fragments without direct contact, and they use these instruments to track the Descent's activity.

A standard resonance event, the background hum of the Descent existing, is constant and low-level. A feeding event, when the Descent is actively consuming a visitor's memories or desires, produces a sharper pulse that the Guild's instruments can distinguish from background noise. A major event, something significant happening inside the domain, produces resonance strong enough to be felt by anyone within a hundred yards of any fragment on the continent.

Since the onset of the Second Silence, resonance events have increased dramatically. The Guild's monitoring stations are recording feeding pulses at ten times their historical frequency. Major events, which once occurred perhaps once a decade, are now happening monthly.

## The Immunity Paradox

Only those without ambition can pass freely through the Descent. Its laws do not apply to beings who genuinely want nothing: the Iron Tithe has nothing to take, the gravity of ambition has nothing to pull, and the time dilation has no strategic value against a visitor who generates no desire to feed upon.

But those without ambition never enter, because entering requires wanting something. Even curiosity is a form of want. Even duty is a form of desire. The only beings exempt from this paradox are the dead. Souls from [[The Twelve#Morrhael|Morrhael]]'s broken domain occasionally drift through Level 6, where the Conquest overlaps with the Hollowdeep's deepest reaches. They pass through untouched, grey and purposeless, and the Descent ignores them the way a predator ignores prey that has already been consumed by something else.

This immunity has been noted by [[The Star-Readers]], who have theorized that a mortal could achieve temporary immunity by entering a state of genuine desirelessness. No one has successfully tested this theory. The Star-Readers acknowledge that a person capable of achieving true desirelessness would, by definition, have no motivation to test it.

## Iron Logic

Within the Descent, Kaevroth's Iron, the divine material that comprises his scattered fragments, functions differently than it does in the mortal world. Outside, Kaevroth fragments radiate war-essence: aggression, paranoia, the urge to dominate. Inside, the same material amplifies rather than corrupts.

A mortal carrying a Kaevroth fragment into the Descent gains authority. The domain's inhabitants recognize the iron as a piece of their creator and respond with deference that ranges from wary respect to outright obedience, depending on the fragment's size and the inhabitant's position in the hierarchy. Doors open. Merchants offer better terms. Soldiers hesitate before attacking.

The cost is proportional. Carrying iron makes you more visible to everything in the Descent. The Tithe accelerates. The gravity of ambition intensifies. And the domain itself becomes aware of you in a way it does not notice ordinary visitors, not as prey, but as a piece of itself that has been returned. The Descent does not want to consume someone carrying its creator's iron. It wants to keep them.

## The Mercy Exception

On Level 9, the Heart, a fragment of [[The Twelve#Belara|Belara]]'s stolen essence remains imprisoned within Kaevroth's concentrated core. Belara's capacity for hope, consumed during the Theomachis, was not destroyed when Kaevroth shattered. It persists, trapped, radiating a faint influence that contradicts everything the Descent stands for.

This creates a narrow exception to the domain's rules. Those who genuinely seek to help someone else, not themselves, not as a means to a personal end, but out of authentic selflessness, find the Iron Tithe slowed. Their memories hold longer. Their desires remain intact. The gravity of ambition loosens its grip, because selfless action is not ambition, and the domain's mechanisms were not designed to process it.

Pure selflessness is the closest thing to armor this place allows. It is also extraordinarily difficult to maintain, because the Descent is designed to convert every noble impulse into self-interest. The moment a selfless actor begins to feel pride in their selflessness, or to desire recognition for their sacrifice, the exception collapses and the domain's full weight reasserts itself.

> [!warning]- GM Only
> **The Mercy Exception is the key to the Descent.** If the PCs ever need to reach Level 9, whether to recover Belara's stolen essence or to confront whatever remains of Kaevroth, the Mercy Exception is how they survive the journey. But it requires genuine selflessness, which means the player must commit to acting against their character's interests without expectation of reward. This should feel like a sacrifice, not a strategy. If a player treats it as a gameplay optimization ("I'll just be selfless to get the buff"), the domain sees through it instantly.
>
> **Belara's imprisoned essence** is the reason the Dominion feels so hopeless. The Heart of Iron, buried beneath [[Ostivaar]], contains both Kaevroth's war-essence and Belara's stolen hope. The entire region sits atop a concentration of divine ambition that has imprisoned the divine embodiment of hope within itself. Freeing Belara's essence from the Heart could shift the Dominion's emotional landscape dramatically, but it would require entering the Descent, reaching Level 9, and performing an act of selflessness profound enough to crack open the prison of a god.
