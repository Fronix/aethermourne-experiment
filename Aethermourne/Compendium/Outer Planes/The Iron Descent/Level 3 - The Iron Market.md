## Description

The noise hits first, a roar of commerce so dense it registers as texture rather than sound. Thousands of voices haggling, wheedling, promising, threatening, all layered over the clink of something that is not coin and the scratch of contracts being signed on surfaces that are not paper. The Iron Market is a bazaar that has consumed an entire level of [[The Twelve#Kaevroth|Kaevroth]]'s domain, a sprawling, vertical, impossible marketplace where every surface is a stall and every shadow hides a merchant.

The architecture defies consistent geometry. Iron walkways crisscross at impossible angles. Stalls are stacked three, four, five levels deep, connected by ladders that seem to lengthen as you climb them. The light comes from lanterns that burn with pale, colorless flame, casting everything in a flat illumination that makes it difficult to judge distance or time. The air smells of hot metal, old parchment, and something sweet that you can never quite identify, a scent designed to make you hungry for something you cannot name.

Everything is for sale. Weapons from wars that never happened. Memories preserved in iron filigree. Emotions bottled in black glass. Services rendered by creatures that should not exist. Information about the deeper levels, about the mortal world, about secrets buried in the ash above. The merchants are unfailingly polite, endlessly patient, and completely without mercy. They will give you exactly what you ask for, at a price that seems reasonable until you realize what you've paid.

The currency of the Iron Market is not gold, not souls, not blood. It is intent. You pay with pieces of yourself: a childhood memory, the capacity for a specific emotion, years of life compressed into iron tokens, the color of your eyes, the sound of your laughter, the knowledge of your mother's face. Every transaction is fair by the letter. Every contract is binding by mechanisms older than mortal law. And every deal, without exception, leaves the buyer diminished in ways they did not anticipate, not because the merchants cheat, but because mortals consistently undervalue what they are willing to trade away.

## The City: Greedhollow

Greedhollow is less a city than a condition. It occupies the entirety of the Iron Market's habitable space, which is to say it occupies the Iron Market itself. There are no residential districts separate from the commercial ones. People live in their stalls, behind their counters, beneath their display cases. The concept of a space that is not transactional does not exist here. Even sleep is commodified, you can buy a night of dreamless rest, but it costs the memory of your most recent nightmare, and without nightmares, the fears they processed begin to accumulate in the waking mind.

The streets rearrange themselves. This is not metaphor. Greedhollow's layout shifts according to the desires of whoever walks it, guiding them toward merchants who sell what they want most. The effect is subtle enough to mistake for coincidence the first few times, compelling enough to follow once recognized, and impossible to resist after prolonged exposure. You will always find what you are looking for in Greedhollow. The city will make certain of it. The only defense is to stop wanting, and the Iron Market's ambient magic ensures that no one who enters ever truly stops.

The social hierarchy is measured in debt and credit. Those who owe nothing are either newcomers or so thoroughly stripped of tradeable qualities that the Market has lost interest in them. The wealthiest merchants are those who hold the most contracts, the most owed intent, the most fragments of surrendered identity. Dealmaker Syra Voss holds more than anyone, which makes her the closest thing Greedhollow has to a sovereign, though she would never use that word. Sovereigns command. Syra negotiates. The distinction matters to her.

### Notable Residents

- **Dealmaker Syra Voss**, Greedhollow's de facto ruler, a figure of sharp beauty and sharper intellect whose collection of contracts gives her influence over thousands of souls. She speaks softly, smiles often, and has never once offered a deal that was not precisely fair. The fact that fair and kind are not synonyms is the foundation of her power.
- **The Assessor**, a blind figure who sits at Greedhollow's central crossroads and evaluates the worth of anything brought before them. Their assessments are infallible. They can tell you exactly what your happiest memory is worth in Market currency, down to the fraction. They do this as a public service. They have never explained why.
- **Ren Twice-Sold**, a soul who has traded away so much of themselves that they exist as a kind of outline, present but barely, defined more by what they have lost than what remains. Ren serves as a cautionary tale and, paradoxically, as one of the Market's most effective guides, because they know every merchant's methods from the buyer's side.
- **Merchant-Prince Avarek**, who deals exclusively in emotions. His stall sells bottled joy, compressed grief, refined rage, and distilled serenity. His prices are high. His product is genuine. His return policy is nonexistent.
- **Coinless Marta**, a former mortal merchant who arrived with nothing and has somehow accumulated significant standing through pure barter skill. She trades in information rather than intent, which makes her both invaluable and deeply suspect.
- **The Debt Collector**, an entity that enforces the Market's contracts. It is not clear whether the Collector is a person, a creature, or a function of the Market itself. It appears when debts come due. It is always polite. It is never unsuccessful.
- **Broker Fennwick**, who specializes in brokering trades between parties who cannot or will not deal directly. He takes a percentage of every transaction, payable in years of life. He is, by the Market's accounting, the oldest living thing on this level, sustained entirely by the years he has skimmed.

## Notable Locations

**The Ledger Spire**, Greedhollow's tallest structure, a tower of iron plates inscribed with every contract ever executed in the Market. The inscriptions are too small to read with mortal eyes, but the Spire hums with the weight of accumulated obligation. Syra Voss maintains an office at its peak. The Spire is said to lean toward whoever in Greedhollow currently holds the most debt, though the lean is too subtle to confirm without instruments that the Market does not sell.

**The Hollow Exchange**, a sunken plaza where the Market's most significant transactions take place. Here, entire identities change hands. Souls trade faces, histories, even species. The Exchange is open to all but used only by those who have already traded away enough to consider such exchanges reasonable. The air in the Exchange smells of nothing at all, which is deeply unsettling in a place where everything else has a scent.

**The Remnant Arcade**, a quiet gallery where the discarded fragments of traded-away selves are displayed. Jars of laughter. Frames holding the color of eyes that are now grey. Boxes that, when opened, release the smell of someone's childhood home. The Arcade is technically a shop, you can buy these fragments, but the prices are astronomical, and the merchants who run it take a particular pleasure in selling someone back a piece of themselves at a thousand times what they were paid for it.

**The Descent Throat**, the passage to the fourth level. Unlike the gates above, the Throat requires payment. The price is always the same: something you do not know you have. The Throat assesses, names its price, and opens or remains shut. Those who pass through arrive on Level 4 missing something they cannot identify, a gap in themselves that they can feel but not describe.

## Native Creatures

**Desire Leeches**, small, translucent parasites that attach to the wanting. They are invisible to anyone who is content, which means they are visible to everyone in Greedhollow. They feed on unfulfilled desire, growing larger and more opaque as their host's wants intensify. A fully fed Desire Leech detaches and crystallizes into a small iron token worth exactly one minor transaction. The merchants consider them a natural resource.

**Contract Wraiths**, the enforcement mechanism for broken deals. When a contract is violated, a Wraith manifests from the text itself, a figure made of compressed language and binding obligation. It pursues the contract-breaker with patient, inexorable purpose. It cannot be fought, because it is not a creature but a legal consequence given form. It can only be satisfied by fulfilling the original terms or negotiating new ones, which always cost more.

**Fragment Hounds**, the same species found on [[Level 1 - The Ashfield|The Ashfield]], but changed by the Market's influence. Here, they hunt not through scent but through desire, tracking the wanting of souls who have traded away too much and begun to regret. Their bite does not inflame aggression as it does above. Here, it inflames *need*, a sudden, overwhelming compulsion to acquire, to trade, to buy something, anything, immediately.

## History

The Iron Market was not originally a marketplace. It was [[The Twelve#Kaevroth|Kaevroth]]'s treasury, the level where the spoils of divine conquest were catalogued, stored, and displayed. In the age before the Theomachis, this level held the tributes of a hundred mortal civilizations: weapons forged in worship, banners taken in wars fought in Kaevroth's name, the compressed ambitions of entire nations offered up to their god. It was a monument to what ambition could accumulate.

After Kaevroth's fall, the treasury's custodians, souls bound to maintain the collection, found themselves without a master and without purpose. The collection began to deteriorate. Custodians began trading pieces of it among themselves, at first to preserve what mattered most, then out of habit, then out of something that looked increasingly like compulsion. The trading expanded. New arrivals brought new goods. The custodial function evolved into commerce. Over centuries, the treasury consumed itself and became the Market, a place where everything is for sale because nothing belongs to anyone anymore, least of all the god who accumulated it. Syra Voss, who arrived three mortal centuries ago, recognized the Market's logic and simply positioned herself at its center, becoming its most successful participant and, eventually, its de facto administrator.

## Encounters & Hooks

1. **The Perfect Offer.** A merchant approaches a PC and offers them exactly what they need most, something tied to their personal arc or backstory. The price is a memory, specifically the memory of why they need the thing being offered. If they accept, they gain the object but lose the context that makes it meaningful. If they refuse, the merchant smiles and says they will ask again later. They always do.
2. **Ren's Warning.** Ren Twice-Sold seeks out the party and warns them about a specific merchant who is collecting something unusual: names from the Crucible above. Someone is buying combat-earned names and stockpiling them. Ren does not know why, but they know the buyer is accumulating enough identity-weight to do something that should not be possible on this level.
3. **The Assessor's Request.** The Assessor, who never asks for anything, asks the party to retrieve something from the Remnant Arcade: a pair of eyes. Not metaphorical. The Assessor traded away their sight centuries ago and has recently learned that it is for sale. They can pay the Arcade's price, but they cannot navigate the Arcade without assistance. What the Assessor has not mentioned is that their blindness is what makes their assessments infallible, and regaining sight will destroy their gift.
4. **Coinless Marta's Intelligence.** Marta offers the party information about the deeper levels, free of charge. This should be impossible in Greedhollow, where nothing is free. Marta's angle becomes clear only later: she has been contracted to guide mortals deeper into the Descent, and the party's passage is already paid for. The question is, by whom.
5. **The Debt Collector's Audit.** The Debt Collector appears before the party and informs one of them that they carry an outstanding debt, a contract they did not sign, inherited from an ancestor, a past life, or a previous visitor to the Market who wore their face. The debt is small. The interest is not. Resolving it requires either payment, a favor for Syra Voss, or finding the original debtor somewhere deeper in the Descent.

> [!warning]- GM Only
> **Campaign Connection.** The Iron Market is where information about [[The Twelve#Kaevroth|Kaevroth]]'s fragments can be purchased, if the buyer knows to ask and is willing to pay. Syra Voss knows the location of every major fragment in the mortal world, including the Heart of Iron. She considers this information her most valuable holding and will not part with it cheaply. Her price for the Heart's location: the buyer must trade away their ambition, permanently. A mortal without ambition cannot misuse the Heart. This is, by Syra's logic, a kindness.
>
> **The Market's Decay.** Like the levels above, the Iron Market is slowly breaking down. Contracts that should be binding are developing loopholes. The streets' rearrangement is becoming erratic, sometimes trapping visitors in loops. The Debt Collector has failed to collect twice in recent memory, something that has never happened before. Syra Voss is aware of the decay and is quietly preparing contingencies, which look suspiciously like an exit strategy. If the Market collapses, the accumulated intent of thousands of transactions will discharge at once. The effect on the mortal world, particularly in regions near Kaevroth fragments, would be catastrophic.
>
> **Syra Voss's True Nature.** Syra is not a mortal soul. She is a fragment of Kaevroth's cunning, a sliver of divine intellect that survived the Theomachis by embedding itself in the Market's transactional logic. She does not know this consciously, but it explains her preternatural skill at negotiation and her inability to leave the Market even when she has, on three occasions, genuinely tried. She is the god's ambition expressed as commerce, and she will exist as long as the Market does. If the party discovers this, Syra's reaction depends entirely on how it is presented. Told gently, she might become an ally. Told carelessly, she might become the most dangerous adversary in the Iron Descent.
