## Description

The Iron Court is the most civilized level of the Descent, and therefore the most dangerous. Here, [[The Twelve#Kaevroth|Kaevroth]]'s obsession with hierarchy and dominion crystallized into something that resembles governance, a vast administrative apparatus of rank, protocol, and precedent that continues to function centuries after its creator's death. The Court is a government with no nation to govern, an army with no war to fight, a bureaucracy processing nothing. It does not notice this. It does not care.

The air here is cold and tastes of iron filings. Every surface is polished to a military shine. Banners hang in perfect formation from walls that stretch higher than any mortal architecture should allow, bearing insignias of divisions and legions that existed only in Kaevroth's war-dreams. The light comes from somewhere above, filtered through iron lattice until it falls in precise geometric patterns across the floors. Nothing here is accidental. Nothing here is warm.

Sound behaves strangely in the Court. Whispers carry across vast halls while shouted orders die within arm's reach. This is by design: the architecture rewards subtlety and punishes directness. Those who have survived here longest speak in implications, suggestions, and carefully worded silences. A raised eyebrow can condemn. A nod can elevate. The politics of the Iron Court make mortal intrigue look like children arguing over toys.

The most unsettling quality of the Court is its competence. Unlike the lower levels, where suffering is raw and obvious, the Iron Court's cruelty is administrative. Sentences are passed in triplicate. Executions follow due process. The damned here are not tortured, they are *managed*, assigned roles, given quotas, evaluated on performance metrics that measure nothing meaningful. It is ambition distilled to its purest, most hollow form.

## The City: Ironhold

Ironhold is the seat of what passes for governance in the Iron Descent, a massive fortress-city of iron spires, audience chambers, and administrative halls that sprawls across a plateau of hammered metal. The city is clean, orderly, and oppressive in the way that only perfect order can be. Streets intersect at precise angles. Buildings are identical in height. Citizens move in regulated patterns, adhering to schedules posted on iron tablets at every intersection.

At the city's heart stands the Throne of Command, Kaevroth's empty seat of power. It is a chair the size of a cathedral, forged from the same iron that comprises the god's scattered fragments. No one sits in it. No one dares. Lord Imperator Vaelgrim holds court at its base, literally in the shadow of the empty throne, dispensing judgments and issuing decrees that echo through all nine levels but are obeyed only here.

The Court maintains an Embassy to the mortal world, a negotiated passage point where mortals can bargain for exit from the Descent. The Embassy is a small, immaculate building near the Throne, staffed by The Ambassador and a rotating cadre of functionaries. The price of passage is always steep: years of service, memories, a piece of your identity, or worse. The Ambassador is unfailingly polite and will explain every clause of the contract in detail. The contracts are always honored. They are also always worse than they sound.

## Notable Locations

**The Throne of Command.** Kaevroth's empty seat, towering above Ironhold like a monument to purposeless authority. The iron radiates a low hum that those nearby interpret as commands, though no two listeners hear the same order. Vaelgrim claims to hear nothing at all, which is either a lie or the reason he remains in power.

**The Hall of Precedent.** An archive containing every decree, judgment, and regulation ever issued in the Iron Court. The records stretch back to the Theomachis itself, and the archivists, skeletal figures in iron robes, can locate any ruling within moments. Petitioners study here for decades before bringing cases before the Court, because any argument that contradicts established precedent is grounds for immediate sentencing.

**The Embassy.** A deceptively modest building where mortals negotiate their release from the Descent. The interior is comfortable by the Court's standards, with actual chairs and something that approximates tea. The contracts displayed on the walls are written in a script that shifts meaning depending on the reader's deepest ambition.

**The Proving Grounds.** A vast arena where disputes of rank are settled through combat, strategy games, or tests of will. The Grounds are the only place in the Court where violence is sanctioned without paperwork. Spectators attend in formal silence, marking scores on iron tablets. Victory earns promotion. Defeat earns reassignment to the levels below.

**The Cells of Contemplation.** Not a prison in the conventional sense, but a wing of identical iron rooms where those who have violated protocol are sent to "reconsider their position." The rooms are featureless. Time passes differently inside. Some occupants emerge after what feels like hours to find centuries have passed. Others experience years in the span of a single Court session.

## Native Creatures

**War Colossi.** Towering constructs of iron and residual divine will, standing three stories tall. They served as Kaevroth's siege engines during the Theomachis and now patrol Ironhold's perimeter in mechanical loops, executing the last orders they were given. They do not distinguish between intruders and residents who stray outside authorized zones.

**Court Phantoms.** The remnants of souls who served in Kaevroth's bureaucracy so long that they forgot they were ever anything else. They appear as translucent figures in iron-gray robes, endlessly shuffling documents, filing reports, and attending meetings that dissolved centuries ago. Mostly harmless, but a Phantom that mistakes a living creature for an unfiled case will attempt to "process" them, a painful experience involving classification, stamping, and archival.

**Iron Mites.** Tiny metallic parasites that feed on ambition itself. They swarm around anyone harboring strong desires, burrowing into skin and drinking the emotional energy that drives the host's goals. A severe infestation leaves the victim hollow, technically alive but stripped of all want, all drive, all purpose. The Court's hierarchy quietly encourages mild infestations as a means of keeping subordinates compliant.

## History

The Iron Court was not built but *declared*. When Kaevroth established the Descent as his domain, the seventh level organized itself around his nature, his obsession with rank, chain of command, and the architecture of power. After his death in the Theomachis, the Court did not collapse. It simply continued, an engine of authority running without fuel, maintained by servants too disciplined to question whether their duties still had meaning. Lord Imperator Vaelgrim, Kaevroth's first general, assumed stewardship and has held the position through a combination of political acumen, genuine military brilliance, and the simple fact that no one else wants the responsibility of governing nothing.

## Encounters & Hooks

1. **The Petitioner's Plea.** Petitioner Ashwyn, a mortal soul trapped in the Court for decades, begs the party to represent her in a case before Judicator Seren. Her crime: she asked why.
2. **The Ambassador's Offer.** The Ambassador approaches the party with an offer of safe passage, but the contract contains a clause that would bind one party member to return within a year and serve as a Court functionary. The clause is buried in sub-article 7, paragraph 12.
3. **Spymaster's Game.** Spymaster Whisper approaches the party with intelligence about the levels below, offered freely. Nothing Whisper offers is ever free. The information is accurate, but accepting it registers the party as Whisper's assets in Court records, making them targets for Chancellor Dross's counter-intelligence operations.
4. **The Empty Throne Speaks.** During a Court session, the Throne of Command emits a sound that everyone present interprets as a direct order, but each person hears something different. The resulting confusion threatens to collapse the Court's hierarchy entirely. Vaelgrim needs the situation resolved before it undermines his authority.
5. **Executioner's Holiday.** Executioner Gral has not carried out a sentence in eleven years, because no cases have completed the full judicial review process. Gral is restless, bored, and increasingly creative in interpreting which infractions qualify for summary judgment. The party's arrival presents fresh candidates.

> [!warning]- GM Only
> The Iron Court is the safest level of the Descent for players who understand politics and the most lethal for those who don't. Combat here is rare, but a misstep in protocol can result in sentencing to the lower levels, which is functionally a death sentence.
>
> **Vaelgrim's Secret.** The Lord Imperator knows the Throne is not empty. Something of Kaevroth's will persists in the iron, whispering strategies for wars that will never be fought. Vaelgrim has been ignoring the whispers for centuries, but they are getting louder. He suspects the Echo on Level 9 is growing stronger, and he is terrified of what happens when it wakes fully.
>
> **The Embassy's True Purpose.** The Embassy does not actually connect to the mortal world. It connects to Level 9, the Heart. Every soul that "escapes" through the Embassy is fed directly to the Echo. The Ambassador knows this. The Ambassador does not care. The contracts are still technically honored, the souls do leave the Court.
>
> **Campaign Connection.** If the players reach the Iron Court, Vaelgrim can be a reluctant ally. He does not want the Echo to consolidate power, and he has centuries of intelligence about the Descent's structure. His price: help him maintain order in the Court. His fear: that without the Court's rigid hierarchy, the entire Descent collapses into the raw war-essence that Kaevroth embodied.
