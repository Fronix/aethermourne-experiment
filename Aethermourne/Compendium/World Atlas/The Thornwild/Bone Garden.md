## Overview

The name is literal. Bone Garden sits in a grove of trees that died and did not fall. Their trunks are pale as ivory, stripped of bark, smooth as polished stone. Their branches spread wide and bare, and from them, in the life-phase of the Cycle, flowers bloom: petals of bone-white, translucent, faintly luminescent, with a scent like cold water and clean earth. When the death-phase returns, the flowers crumble to dust, the branches go still, and the grove stands in perfect skeletal silence until the next turn.

It is one of the most beautiful places in Aethermourne. It makes your skin crawl.

The settlement exists because the grove sits precisely on the boundary between the Wilt and the Threshold, where the transition between life and death phases is slow enough to observe, and to harvest. Cycle-materials, substances that exist only in the moment of transition, form here in usable quantities: Thornwild amber crystallizing on branches, transition-moss that is simultaneously alive and decaying, sap that runs warm and cold in alternating heartbeats. [[Garret Boneharvest]] built the town's economy on these materials, and harvesters from across the Thornwild bring their finds here for processing and trade.

> [!warning]- GM Only
> The bone-trees are not natural. They are the skeletal remains of a grove that was alive when [[The Twelve#Thyrea|Thyrea]] fell, flash-killed by [[The Twelve#Morrhael|Morrhael]]'s death-pulse, and then locked in the Cycle's loop. They are, in a sense, the oldest living-dead things in the Thornwild, and they contain trace memories of the Theomachis itself. A sufficiently skilled communion, using both growth and death attunement, could experience fragments of the moment two divine legacies collided. This would be revelatory, terrifying, and potentially mind-breaking.

## Layout & Landmarks

**The Bone Grove**, the ring of skeletal trees surrounding the settlement. Harvesters work the grove in shifts timed to the Cycle's transitions, collecting materials during the narrow windows when substances are forming. The grove is also the settlement's first line of defense: during Death Tides, the trees' undead resonance creates a dampening field that slows incoming risen, giving residents time to retreat.

**Boneharvest House**, [[Garret Boneharvest]]'s trading post and the economic heart of the settlement. A squat building of stone and preserved wood, its shelves lined with sealed jars of cycle-materials, each labeled with the exact transition-phase in which it was collected. The quality of Boneharvest's inventory is legendary among alchemists.

**The Crucible**, an open-air alchemical workshop where cycle-materials are processed, stabilized, and prepared for export. Three master alchemists and a dozen apprentices work here, and the air always carries a faint shimmer, a visual distortion caused by the interaction of stabilized life and death energies. Looking at the Crucible from a distance, it appears to breathe.

**The Quiet**, a small shrine maintained by a single Cyclekeeper where the dead are honored and the living are blessed. It serves both Marcher and Wastes traditions, with a living garden on one side and a bone-cairn on the other. Residents visit daily. It is the only place in Bone Garden where the Cycle's effects are deliberately muted.

## People & Culture

Bone Garden is a working town. Its people are harvesters, alchemists, traders, and laborers who came for the materials and stayed because the work gives meaning to a place that otherwise defies it. The culture is practical and surprisingly cheerful, the dark humor of people who handle death and life as raw materials every day. "Death's just inventory," the harvesters say. "Life's just processing. We're the ones who sell the difference."

The population skews younger than Cyclethorn's, attracting those willing to do dangerous physical work for good pay. Injuries are common, treated with Thornwild amber poultices that heal at uncanny speed but leave faintly luminescent scars. A Bone Garden veteran can be identified by the pale tracery of scars on their hands and forearms, marks of the trade.

### Notable Residents

- **[[Garret Boneharvest]]**, founder and chief trader. He runs the town's economy with the cheerful ruthlessness of a man who has found his calling in the impossible. He wants stable trade routes to the wider world.
- **Master Coris**, head alchemist at the Crucible, a quiet woman with luminescent scars covering both arms to the elbow. She wants to understand the fundamental nature of cycle-materials, and she suspects they hold the key to reconciling divine energies.
- **Watcher Fell**, the lone Cyclekeeper assigned to Bone Garden, a young man who reads the Cycle with instinctive accuracy but hates the isolation of his post. He wants a partner, professional or otherwise.

## Tensions & Hooks

1. **Ember Guild interest.** Traders connected to [[The Ember Guild]] have been making offers for exclusive Thornwild amber contracts. [[Garret Boneharvest]] is tempted by the gold but wary of the strings. The civilian council is divided.
2. **Unstable materials.** Recent batches of cycle-materials have been behaving erratically, transitioning unpredictably after collection. Master Coris believes the Cycle's acceleration is affecting material stability. If she's right, Bone Garden's entire economy is at risk.
3. **The grove is changing.** The bone-trees are blooming longer and withering faster. Some are showing growth that wasn't there before: not flowers, but something fungal, dark-veined, reaching. The harvesters are unsettled.
4. **A dead harvester walked home.** Tomas Breck died in a collection accident three days ago. He walked back to his house last night, sat in his chair, and hasn't moved since. He responds to questions with single words. He is not a Remnant. He is something the Cycle made, and no one knows what to do with him.

> [!warning]- GM Only
> Tomas Breck is the first sign that the Cycle is producing something new: not undead, not living, but a third state that mirrors the Bloom-Wraith's permanent transition on a smaller scale. If more people begin transitioning this way, the Thornwild's population will face an existential question about what counts as alive, what counts as dead, and whether the distinction matters anymore. This is the Thornwild's deepest philosophical hook, and it has no clean answer.
