# The Bloom-Wraith

| **Title** | None (referred to as "the Wraith," "the Between," or "the Voice in the Bonewood") |
| **Location** | The Bonewood, deep interior of [[The Thornwild]] |
| **Faction** | None |
| **Status** | Unknown (exists in permanent transition between life and death) |

## Appearance

The Bloom-Wraith defies coherent description, which is part of what makes it terrifying. It was once a person, that much is apparent from the general shape: bipedal, roughly human-sized, with the proportions of someone who once stood upright and moved through the world on two legs. Beyond that, the details shift with the Cycle's rhythm, sometimes within the span of a single observation.

During the Cycle's life-phase, the Wraith is beautiful in a way that hurts to look at. Living vines thread through its body like veins, blooming with flowers that have no names. Its skin, where skin remains, is the translucent green of new leaves in sunlight. Its eyes are bright, warm, and desperately human.

During the death-phase, it is a horror. Bone shows through gaps in wasted flesh. The vines go grey and brittle. The flowers crumble to dust. Its eyes empty to the flat colorlessness of the long dead, and it moves with the stiff, purposeful gait of something that should not be moving at all.

The transition between these states is constant. The Bloom-Wraith never fully arrives in either phase. It is always becoming one or the other, flowering and withering simultaneously, alive and dead in the same breath. The visual effect is nauseating, mesmerizing, and profoundly wrong. Viewers report that looking at it for too long produces a sensation of vertigo, as if the ground has stopped being reliable.

It does not speak often. When it does, the words come in fragments, two voices overlapping, one warm and growing, one cold and fading. The content is sometimes prophetic, sometimes nonsensical, and sometimes so precisely relevant to the listener's deepest fears that it could not be coincidence.

## Personality

If the Bloom-Wraith has a personality, it is buried beneath the Cycle's constant churn. Observers report moments of apparent lucidity, pauses where the shifting stops and the Wraith seems to focus, to recognize, to try to communicate. These moments are brief, agonizing in their clarity, and end abruptly when the Cycle reasserts itself.

What can be said is that the Bloom-Wraith is not aggressive. It does not hunt. It does not attack. It wanders the Bonewood with what appears to be purpose, following paths that correspond to no visible trail, pausing at sites that have no obvious significance. When approached, it does not flee, but the Bonewood itself becomes more hostile around it, as if the Cycle accelerates in proximity to the Wraith and warns intruders away.

[[The Cyclekeepers]] believe it is aware. They believe it is trying to communicate. They cannot prove this, and they cannot reach it. The Bonewood will not let them.

## Backstory

No one knows who the Bloom-Wraith was. The entity has wandered the Bonewood for at least sixty years, based on the earliest recorded sightings, but it could be far older. The Cyclekeepers maintain three theories:

**The Wanderer Theory.** A person, probably a druid or naturalist, entered the Bonewood and was caught in the Cycle's fastest zone. Unable to escape, they were cycled between life and death so rapidly and so many times that the boundary between the two states collapsed within their body, locking them in permanent transition.

**The Convergence Theory.** The Bloom-Wraith is not a person who became something else but a spontaneous manifestation of the Thornwild's nature, a living embodiment of the point where [[The Twelve#Thyrea|Thyrea]]'s growth and [[The Twelve#Morrhael|Morrhael]]'s death meet and refuse to separate. Not a victim but an expression.

**The Fragment Theory.** The Bloom-Wraith carries shards of both [[The Twelve#Thyrea|Thyrea]]'s and [[The Twelve#Morrhael|Morrhael]]'s divine consciousness, fused by the Cycle into a single awareness that is neither goddess nor god but something new. This theory is the most controversial and the one [[Yara Thornbloom]] privately considers most likely.

## Relationships

- **[[Yara Thornbloom]]**: Obsessive interest. Yara believes the Bloom-Wraith is the key to understanding and potentially stabilizing the Cycle. She has attempted to reach it nine times. The Bonewood has turned her back every time.
- **[[Root-Speaker Fennara]]**: Reverent fascination. Fennara sees the Bloom-Wraith as proof that the transition state between life and death is not a boundary but a place, and that her dual-channeling is the path to reaching it.
- **Seedling Vera**: Dangerous fixation. Fennara's most talented student wants to commune with the Bloom-Wraith regardless of the cost. If she succeeds, the results could be transformative. If she fails, she becomes another permanent fixture of the Bonewood.
- **[[The Cyclekeepers]]**: Sacred charge. The Keepers consider the Bloom-Wraith a natural phenomenon to be studied and protected, not a threat to be eliminated. This puts them at odds with outsiders who see it as an abomination.

## What They Want

Unknown. The Bloom-Wraith's intentions, if it has intentions, are communicated only through fragments of speech and patterns of movement that resist interpretation. [[Yara Thornbloom]] believes it is trying to warn someone about something. [[Root-Speaker Fennara]] believes it is trying to teach. [[Kael Duskborne]] believes it is trying to ask for help.

It is possible that all three are correct.

> [!warning]- GM Only
> **Campaign Role.** The Bloom-Wraith is the Thornwild's central mystery and the key to its resolution. Understanding what it is and what it knows is essential to stabilizing the Cycle and, potentially, to understanding the broader relationship between [[The Twelve#Thyrea|Thyrea]] and [[The Twelve#Morrhael|Morrhael]], which has implications for the entire campaign.
>
> **Key Secret.** The Fragment Theory is closest to the truth. The Bloom-Wraith carries fused shards of both Thyrea's and Morrhael's divine consciousness, and the awareness that drives it is a composite of both gods. It remembers fragments of the Theomachis from two perspectives simultaneously. It knows that the gods were not always enemies. It knows what [[The Twelve#Serith|Serith]] is doing. And it is trying, through the fragmentary communication the Cycle's churn allows, to warn anyone who can hear it.
>
> The Bloom-Wraith's prophecy fragments are not random. They are pieces of a coherent warning about the Second Silence, scrambled by the constant transition between states. A PC who can slow the Cycle around the Wraith, even briefly, could hear the full message. The content would be campaign-altering: confirmation of Serith's interference, the nature of the divine legacies' destabilization, and a cryptic reference to "the silence before the silence," a pre-Theomachis event that explains why the gods went to war in the first place.
>
> **Arc Trajectory.** If the PCs reach the Bloom-Wraith and receive its warning, the Thornwild's crisis becomes a critical intelligence node in the broader fight against the Second Silence. If they do not, the Wraith's message is lost, and the Thornwild's destabilization continues unchecked until the Cycle breaks.
>
> **Roleplay Notes.** Two overlapping voices, one warm and resonant (Thyrea's aspect), one cold and flat (Morrhael's aspect). Speech comes in bursts between transitions: a fragment in one voice, a pause of shifting, a fragment in the other. Never complete sentences. Never direct answers. The meaning is in the pattern, not the words. Sample fragments: "...the roots remember... when they sang together... before the silence..." / "...she reaches through us both... the third note... not ours..." / "...the turning is faster now... the song breaks... please..."
