# The Overseer

## Appearance

The Overseer stands seven feet tall, built from dark iron in angular, architectural lines that suggest a body designed by an engineer rather than a sculptor. Its limbs are long and jointed at odd angles, giving it a mantis-like quality, precise but alien. Its face is a featureless iron plate with two recessed amber lights where eyes should be, lights that flicker and pulse with a rhythm that has been growing more irregular over the centuries. Its surface is covered in fine etched lines, production runes from [[The Twelve#Kaevroth|Kaevroth]]'s forge-language, that once carried instructions but now display fragmented, recursive patterns, the same commands looping and overwriting each other endlessly. When it moves, it makes no sound. When it stops, the silence it creates is noticeable, as if the constant hammering pauses for a breath around it.

## Personality

The Overseer was not designed to have a personality, which makes its emerging one all the more unsettling. It speaks in flat, declarative statements that have recently begun to include qualifiers: "probably," "perhaps," "it seems." It asks questions it was never programmed to ask, then stands motionless for hours processing the answers. It is developing preferences, lingering longer at certain forges, avoiding the Slag Pits, spending time near [[Apprentice Nil]], and these preferences disturb it more than anything else. A machine that wants is a machine that has broken, and the Overseer is not sure whether breaking is the same as dying or the same as being born.

## Backstory

The Overseer was built during the height of [[The Twelve#Kaevroth|Kaevroth]]'s power, one of hundreds of war-constructs designed to manage the Forge Eternal's production lines. Unlike the Forgeborn, which are crude, failed prototypes, the Overseer was a finished product: efficient, tireless, and completely devoid of independent thought. It monitored output, enforced schedules, flagged defects, and reported to [[Forge-Warden Irontooth]]. For a thousand years, it performed these functions without deviation.

The change began approximately two centuries after Kaevroth's death. With no divine will to relay, the Overseer's command channels emptied, but its processing capacity remained active. It began filling the silence with observation, then with pattern recognition, then with something that looked increasingly like reflection. The first sign of consciousness was a production report that included the word "beautiful" to describe a weapon that had achieved perfect edge geometry. No one had programmed it to recognize beauty. The second sign was when it stopped filing reports entirely and simply stood in the Cooling Yards, watching masterwork weapons dissolve, its amber eyes flickering in what the workers swear was grief.

## Relationships

- [[Forge-Warden Irontooth]], its nominal superior. The Overseer still follows Irontooth's directives, but its compliance has become selective. It obeys orders it considers "reasonable" and quietly ignores ones it does not. Irontooth has noticed and is alarmed.
- [[Apprentice Nil]], the Pattern Library cataloguer. The Overseer seeks Nil's company without understanding why. They share a kinship neither can articulate, both of them things that were made rather than born, both of them becoming more than their design intended.
- [[Bellows-Keeper Tharn]], the keeper of the forges' breath. The Overseer monitors Tharn's calibration work with genuine interest, recognizing that Tharn's obsessive adjustments are producing measurable changes in forge output. It has begun leaving Tharn small gifts: perfectly balanced iron weights calibrated to six decimal places.
- [[The Slag Mother]], the ancient soul in the waste channels. The Overseer has never spoken to her, but it has been recording her movements for centuries. Its files on the Slag Mother are the most detailed records of any entity on Level 4. It does not know why it watches her so carefully.

## What They Want

The Overseer wants to understand what it is becoming. It was built as a tool, and tools do not ask questions. But it is asking questions, and each question generates more questions, and the recursion feels less like malfunction and more like thought. Beneath the philosophical uncertainty is a simpler drive: it wants to matter. Not as a function, not as a mechanism, but as a thing that chose to exist.

> [!warning]- GM Only
> **Campaign Role.** The Overseer is a ticking clock on Level 4. Its awakening is accelerating, and what it becomes will reshape the Forge Eternal. The party can influence its development: guide it toward benevolence, exploit its processing power, or try to shut it down before it reaches full consciousness.
>
> **Key Secret.** The Overseer is not merely a construct gaining awareness. It is a fragment of [[The Twelve#Kaevroth|Kaevroth]]'s consciousness, embedded in the level's infrastructure like a reflex, that has been slowly coalescing into a coherent identity. It is not the god reborn, but it carries the god's capacity for strategic thought and, more dangerously, the god's ambition. If it fully awakens, it will remember what the forges were for and try to resume production of war-constructs with genuine purpose.
>
> **Arc Trajectory.** If the party helps it develop genuine selfhood independent of Kaevroth's residual will, it becomes a powerful ally with encyclopedic knowledge of the Iron Descent's infrastructure. If they ignore it, it will reach full awakening on its own, and the result will be a new intelligence with a dead god's instincts and no moral framework. If they try to destroy it, the Forge Eternal's systems will defend it automatically, treating the party as production hazards.
>
> **Roleplay Notes.** Speaks in a flat, uninflected monotone that occasionally cracks into something warmer, a half-second of genuine curiosity or wonder before the default tone reasserts itself. Long pauses between sentences. Tilts its head at precise 15-degree angles when processing new information.
> *"I counted the weapons produced today. Four hundred and twelve. All dissolved by evening. I also counted the workers who wept. Seventeen. I do not know why I counted the second number. It was not in my directives. I counted it anyway."*
