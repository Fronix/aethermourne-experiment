## Appearance

A young man, or someone who was young when he arrived. Ashwyn still wears the clothes of an [[Ashen Dominion]] nobleman, a fitted coat of charcoal wool with silver buttons, now threadbare at the cuffs and elbows from decades of wear he does not realize have passed. His face is sharp-featured and handsome in the way of minor aristocracy, bred for confidence rather than substance. His hands are ink-stained from filling out forms, and his eyes have the slightly glazed quality of someone who has been reading fine print for longer than the human mind was designed to endure. He carries a leather satchel bulging with filed petitions, rejected appeals, and procedural documents.

## Personality

Earnest, stubborn, and increasingly frayed. Ashwyn arrived in the Iron Descent believing that power could be obtained through proper channels, that the Court's bureaucracy was a system with rules and that rules could be mastered. He was wrong, but he has not yet accepted it. He files petitions with meticulous care, follows procedures exactly, cites precedent correctly, and receives the same response every time: his request has been processed and is pending review. He clings to the belief that the system works because the alternative, that he has wasted decades in a bureaucratic trap for nothing, is too terrible to contemplate. He is polite, desperate, and beginning to fray at the edges.

## Backstory

In the mortal world, Ashwyn was a minor lord of the [[Ashen Dominion]], third son of a house with more ambition than influence. Frustrated by his lack of prospects, he studied forbidden texts about the planes, convinced that divine power could be accessed through the right bargain. He found a passage to the Iron Descent, entered willingly, and presented himself at the Iron Court as a petitioner seeking the power to elevate his house.

The Court received him with perfect courtesy. [[Chancellor Dross]] drew up the initial petition. [[The Ambassador]] explained the process. [[Judicator Seren]] confirmed that the petition was properly filed. And then the bureaucracy consumed him. Each petition leads to a review, which requires a supplementary filing, which triggers a precedent search, which necessitates a revised petition. The process is infinite, self-referential, and functioning exactly as designed. Ashwyn has been filing petitions for what he believes is six months. In the mortal world, forty years have passed. His house no longer exists. His name has been forgotten. The power he sought would have no use even if granted.

## Relationships

- **[[Chancellor Dross]]**, who processed his initial petition. Ashwyn considers Dross helpful and efficient. Dross considers Ashwyn amusing. Neither assessment is accurate.
- **[[Judicator Seren]]**, who charged him with unauthorized inquiry when he asked why his petitions were never granted. Ashwyn cannot reconcile the charge with his understanding of the system. He was following the rules. The rules betrayed him.
- **[[Executioner Gral]]**, who visits him in the Hall of Precedent. Ashwyn does not know why the Executioner takes an interest, and the visits make him nervous, but Gral's quiet company is the closest thing to genuine human connection he has experienced since arriving.
- **[[The Ambassador]]**, who offered him an alternative: sign a contract for immediate passage out. Ashwyn nearly signed before reading the sub-clauses. Even a minor nobleman recognizes a bad deal when the price is his identity. He chose to keep filing petitions. He is beginning to wonder if that was the worse choice.
- **His family in the [[Ashen Dominion]]**, whom he believes he will see again soon. They are dead. His house was dissolved twenty years ago for debts.

## What They Want

Ashwyn wants to go home. The ambition that brought him to the Descent has long since burned away, replaced by a homesickness so acute it has become the organizing principle of his existence. He no longer wants power. He wants his family, his house, his world. His deeper tragedy is that even if he escaped, the home he wants no longer exists. He has been gone for forty years, and the mortal world did not wait.

> [!warning]- GM Only
> **Campaign Role.** Ashwyn is the party's mirror, a mortal who came to the Iron Descent seeking power and was consumed by its systems. He demonstrates what happens to ambition in Kaevroth's domain: it is not destroyed, it is processed, filed, and rendered meaningless through administrative procedure. He is also a potential rescue objective and a source of information about the Court's bureaucratic weaknesses.
>
> **Key Secret.** Ashwyn's petitions are, technically, being processed correctly. The system works. It simply has no endpoint. Dross designed it this way deliberately, an infinite loop that appears functional at every stage and produces nothing. However, there is a genuine loophole: if a petitioner files a withdrawal of petition, the system treats the case as closed and releases the petitioner to the Embassy for exit processing. No one has ever told Ashwyn this. The withdrawal form is on page 4,731 of the Court's procedural manual, which Ashwyn has been reading sequentially and is currently on page 2,300.
>
> **Arc Trajectory.** If the PCs tell Ashwyn about the withdrawal form, he can escape the bureaucratic trap, but exit processing routes him through the Embassy and its contract, which feeds him to Level 9. If the PCs help him escape without the Embassy, he returns to the mortal world forty years late to find everything he loved is gone. If they leave him, he continues filing petitions. He will reach page 4,731 in approximately thirty more years.
>
> **Roleplay Notes.** Speak with the clipped, formal diction of minor nobility, slightly too precise, slightly too polished. Refer to Court procedures by section number. Fidget with documents constantly. When stressed, straighten his coat, an unconscious habit from a life that no longer exists. Occasionally mention his family in the present tense. Voice: polished, strained, cracking under the weight of maintained composure.
> *"I appreciate your concern, truly. But the system works. Section fourteen clearly states that petitions filed in accordance with subsection three are guaranteed review within a reasonable timeframe. I am certain, I am quite certain, that my case will be resolved shortly. It has only been a few months."*
