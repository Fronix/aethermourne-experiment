# The Fathom

> [!quote]
> *"I have spent forty years reading the stars. I can tell you when a king will die, when a harvest will fail, when a storm will break a fleet in half. But there was one night, working the great lens at Ashveil, when the glass turned past the last star and I saw what lies beyond it. I have not slept well since. Not because it was terrible. Because it was there, and it was vast, and it did not care that I was looking."*
> , Astronomer-Lector Haelynn of the [[The Star-Readers|Star-Readers]], private correspondence, never published

---

## Overview

The Fathom is the expanse that exists outside the boundaries of creation. It is not a void. It is not emptiness. It is a fundamentally different kind of existence, one that operates according to rules completely unrelated to the reality [[The Twelve]] built. It has its own structures, its own inhabitants, its own nature, but all of it is alien to mortal comprehension.

If [[World Overview|Aethermourne]] is an island, The Fathom is the ocean it sits in.

The name carries double meaning: fathom as a unit of depth, immeasurable, and fathom as comprehension, impossible. It is the unfathomable. It predates creation. It will outlast creation. And it is largely indifferent to creation, the way an ocean is indifferent to a single island.

The Fathom is not evil. Its nature is simply incompatible with creation's rules in the same way that deep water is incompatible with air-breathing. Not hostile. Just different. And in that difference, a quiet, immeasurable terror.

> [!info] Cosmological Position
> The layers of existence, from outermost to innermost:
>
> - **The Fathom**, the vast alien beyond, surrounding everything
>   - **The Boundary**, the celestial sphere, the stars, the edge of creation
>     - **The Shattered Empyrean**, the divine realm, now collapsed
>       - **Aethermourne**, the mortal world
>         - **[[The Gyre]]**, the raw substrate within and below creation
> - **The Loom of Fate** threads through all layers
>
> The Fathom is the outermost layer. It presses against the boundary of creation the way deep water presses against a diving bell.

## The Boundary

The stars are not merely lights in the sky. They are structural elements, anchor points that maintain the separation between inside and outside, between creation and The Fathom. The celestial sphere is the skin of the world in a cosmological sense, the membrane that keeps the alien nature of The Fathom from flooding inward.

[[The Twelve#Aelindra|Aelindra]], Goddess of Knowledge and Stars, retreated to this boundary after the Theomachis. She sits upon the edge of creation and can see in both directions: inward, toward the broken world she could not save, and outward, toward the vast alien reality pressing against the membrane she now inhabits. This dual perspective is part of why she despairs. She can see what is pressing in, and she understands, with the clarity of a goddess devoted to truth, how fragile the separation really is.

### The Stars Going Dark

The stars are going dark. One by one, over the last eighteen months, they have been vanishing, as if snuffed. This is not merely an astronomical phenomenon. Each star that goes dark is an anchor point failing, a note in the harmonic that holds the boundary weakening. Where a star once burned, the membrane between creation and The Fathom thins. The Fathom's alien nature begins to seep through, not in a flood, but in whispers. Subtle wrongness. Dreams that do not belong to the dreamer. Geometries that the eye follows but the mind cannot parse.

The [[The Star-Readers|Star-Readers]] have documented the pattern of disappearances. They cannot explain it. They are terrified by it. And they are right to be.

> [!warning]- GM Only
> The stars going dark and the weakening of Serith's prison may not be unrelated phenomena, but the exact causal relationship is unclear even to the gods. Is Serith's activity destabilizing the boundary? Is the boundary's natural decay weakening Serith's prison along with everything else? Or is there a third cause that neither the gods nor mortals have identified?
>
> This ambiguity is intentional. The Fathom adds a layer of cosmic context to the campaign's central crisis: even if the players solve the Serith problem, the stars are still going dark. The boundary is still thinning. The world's problems may be deeper than any single antagonist.

## The Entities of The Fathom

Mortal scholarship has identified, through fragmentary evidence accumulated over millennia, a handful of intelligences that seem to exist within or upon The Fathom. These are not like the forces of [[The Gyre]], which are vast, formless processes, the raw substrate of pre-creation. Fathom entities are more recognizably beings, possessing will, intelligence, and purpose. But their nature is fundamentally alien because they evolved and exist in a reality with completely different rules.

What follows are the three most documented. There are certainly more. The Fathom could contain entire alien realities, vast domains, uncountable intelligences. These three are merely the shapes that mortal perception has, briefly and imperfectly, managed to identify.

### Orvhal, The Witness

A vast, ancient intelligence that has observed creation from outside since its inception. It does not intervene. It does not judge. It does not desire. It watches. It remembers. It knows things about [[The Twelve]] that The Twelve themselves may not have known.

Orvhal appears in fragmentary references across multiple cultures and eras. The [[The Star-Readers|Star-Readers]] know it as "The Eye Beyond." Coastal mystics in [[The Hollowed Reach]] call it "The Patient One." Ancient texts from the pre-Dominion era reference simply "That Which Sees." The consistency across unconnected sources, spanning centuries and continents, suggests that what they describe is real.

Some scholars believe the rare "true visions" that prophets experience, visions that are accurate and detailed beyond what the fragmented Loom of Fate can provide, come from accidentally touching Orvhal's awareness. The entity does not send these visions deliberately. A mortal mind, reaching outward in a moment of extreme perception, simply brushes against a consciousness that has been watching for longer than creation has existed, and receives, for one searing instant, a fragment of what it has seen.

Orvhal is not a threat. It is a witness. And there is something profoundly unsettling about the knowledge that everything, every triumph, every atrocity, every quiet moment of grace, has been observed by something that will never look away and will never care.

### Ithael, The Threshold Singer

An entity that exists at the exact boundary between creation and The Fathom. Its song IS the boundary, a harmonic vibration that maintains the separation between inside and outside. Ithael is not a guardian. It is the surface tension of a bubble, a phenomenon as much as an entity.

The stars may be resonance points of Ithael's song, crystallized notes that anchor the barrier in place. When stars go dark, notes are fading. The harmonic is losing coherence. If Ithael's song falters entirely, the boundary dissolves, and creation is flooded by The Fathom's alien nature, not as an invasion, but as a mixing. Oil poured into water. Two incompatible realities occupying the same space.

[[The Twelve#Aelindra|Aelindra]] can hear the song. She has listened to it for two thousand years from her place on the celestial sphere. She has heard it weaken. She has heard individual notes fail. She has felt the silence where a star used to sing. And she cannot stop it. She is a goddess of knowledge, not of creation. She can catalogue the dying. She cannot prevent it.

> [!info] A Note on Ithael's Nature
> Whether Ithael is truly an entity with consciousness, or a natural phenomenon that mortal minds interpret as an entity, is an open question. The distinction may not be meaningful. A river does not "choose" to flow, but it shapes everything it touches. Ithael's song maintains the boundary whether or not there is a singer behind it.

### The Unremembered

Not a single entity but a category, or perhaps a collective. The Unremembered are what creation excluded.

Every act of creation is also an act of exclusion. When [[The Twelve]] shaped reality from [[The Gyre]]'s raw potential, they decided what things would be. In doing so, they also decided what things would not be. The Unremembered are the possibilities that were rejected, the forms that were not chosen, the laws that were not enacted. They are the "isn'ts," the options that were set aside when the gods selected the architecture of reality.

They press against the boundary not from malice but from a kind of existential yearning, the desire to BE that was denied when creation chose otherwise. They are not hostile. They are incomplete. They are the sketches that were never finished, the words that were never spoken, the shapes that were never given form. And they want, with a longing that predates mortal emotion, to exist.

If the boundary were to weaken enough for The Unremembered to seep into creation, the result would not be destruction. It would be something stranger: the intrusion of forms and principles that have no place in reality as it was designed. Colors that do not exist on any spectrum. Geometries that violate spatial logic. Creatures that follow biological rules never enacted. Not wrong, exactly. Just profoundly, fundamentally other.

> [!warning]- GM Only
> The Unremembered are the most useful Fathom entity for table play because they explain the wrongness that seeps in where the boundary thins. Deep-sea creatures with too many eyes, geometric patterns on shells that hurt to look at, the "something older" that [[The Twelve#Yvenne|Yvenne]]'s ocean becomes at its deepest points, these are all touches of The Unremembered leaking through.
>
> They also raise a philosophical question that could become campaign-relevant: if Serith unmakes creation and starts over, what happens to The Unremembered? Are they given their chance to exist in the new creation? Or are they excluded again, a fresh set of rejected possibilities joining the old ones in the dark?

## Mortal Knowledge

Almost no one in [[World Overview|Aethermourne]] knows about The Fathom. The concept exists only in the most esoteric scholarly circles, and even there it is treated as speculation rather than established cosmology.

**The [[The Star-Readers|Star-Readers]]** come closest. Their astronomical observations have documented the stars going dark, and their most advanced theorists have begun to ask what lies beyond the celestial sphere. A handful of Star-Reader texts, heavily annotated and fiercely debated, describe "the outer dark" as something more than absence. These texts are not widely circulated. The scholars who wrote them are regarded as brilliant but unstable.

**Tidal Sorcerers** in [[The Hollowed Reach]] occasionally report, during deep attunement at extreme low tide, a sensation they describe as "touching the floor of the world." In those moments, they perceive something beyond the floor, something vast, something that is not water and is not emptiness. Most dismiss the experience as hallucination brought on by over-attunement. A few do not dismiss it. Those few tend to stop practicing tidal sorcery.

**The [[The Lantern-Keepers|Lantern-Keepers]]' Divers**, who descend to investigate Trench phenomena, have brought back reports of encounters at extreme depth where the water "stops being water." The Lantern-Keeper archives contain sealed accounts from Divers who describe the deep ocean as a transition zone, a gradient between mortal reality and something that is neither ocean nor void nor anything they have a name for. These accounts are classified and available only to senior Keepers.

**Ancient pre-Theomachis texts** reference entities "beyond the firmament" in language that suggests awareness of The Fathom, or something like it. The Drowning Bridge in the [[The Greymere Fenlands|Greymere Fenlands]], which may predate [[The Twelve]] themselves, was built during an age when the boundary between creation and The Fathom was not yet fully formed. Its builders may have known what lay outside, because in their time, there was no clear "outside" yet.

## Points of Contact

The boundary between creation and The Fathom is not uniformly thick. There are places where it thins, where the membrane stretches taut, where the alien nature of the beyond presses close enough to leave marks on reality.

### The Celestial Sphere

The most obvious point of contact. The stars are the boundary, and [[The Twelve#Aelindra|Aelindra]] inhabits it. Where stars go dark, the boundary thins. The celestial sphere is vast, and the darkened patches are still few, but they are growing. Aelindra watches them spread the way a sailor watches leaks in a hull.

### The Deep Ocean

At its absolute deepest points, the ocean reaches the boundary from below. Existing lore describes [[The Twelve#Yvenne|Yvenne]]'s Deep as a place "where the water becomes something else, where Yvenne's divine essence makes the sea a borderland between mortal reality and something older." That "something older" is The Fathom, leaking in through the thinnest parts of the boundary at the ocean floor.

This explains the wrongness of deep-sea life in the Reach: creatures with too many eyes, geometric patterns on shells that seem to shift when observed, bioluminescence in colors that have no name. They are not merely adapted to deep water. They are adapted to water that is partially contaminated by an alien reality.

### The Abyssal Trench

[[The Twelve#Serith|Serith]]'s prison was placed at the bottom of the Abyssal Trench for a reason. The Trench approaches the boundary of creation at its deepest point, as close to "outside" as it is possible to get without actually breaching the membrane. The surviving gods who imprisoned Serith placed her at the very edge of existence, a cage on the rim of the world.

This proximity is cosmologically significant. Serith, trapped for two thousand years at the edge of creation, has had more contact with the boundary than any other being in existence. What she has perceived, what she has learned, what she has become as a result, is unknown. But a goddess of secrets, imprisoned for millennia at the point where creation meets the unknowable, will not have spent that time idle.

### The Black Tides

The supernatural storms battering [[The Hollowed Reach]]'s coast may be more than Serith's prison weakening. They may be moments when The Fathom's alien nature seeps through the boundary into the ocean, corrupting the water. The two causes, Serith's influence and Fathom seepage, would be almost impossible to distinguish from the mortal world. Both produce the same symptoms: black water, alien creatures, madness, wrongness.

This raises a disturbing possibility. The Lantern-Keepers, the Concord, the Star-Readers, everyone investigating the Black Tides is looking for a single cause. There may be two, layered on top of each other, indistinguishable and compounding.

## The Fathom and The Second Silence

The [[Cosmology and Magic#The Second Silence|Second Silence]], the slow decline of magic, the fading of divine materials, the dimming of ley lines, is understood as the consequence of dead gods finally decaying. And it is. But The Fathom adds a dimension that no mortal scholar has yet grasped.

The boundary between creation and The Fathom was built by [[The Twelve]] at the height of their power. It was maintained by their collective will. Now most of them are dead, one is shattered, one is imprisoned, one is silent, one is erratic, and one is paralyzed by despair. The boundary has no maintainers. Like the [[Cosmology and Magic#Ley Lines and the Aetheric Web|Aetheric Web]], like the death-cycle, like the Loom of Fate, it is a divine mechanism running without its operators.

It is running down.

The stars going dark are the first visible sign. The deep-ocean contamination is the second. The increasing wrongness of Black Tide events, the reports of impossible geometries in Trench creatures, the dreams that coastal populations share without explanation, these are all symptoms of a boundary losing coherence.

If the boundary fails entirely, The Fathom will not invade. It will simply be present, the way water is present when a dam breaks. And creation, built on rules that are incompatible with The Fathom's nature, will not survive the mixing.

> [!warning]- GM Only
> The Fathom is not a problem the players need to solve in this campaign. It is the context that makes everything else feel more urgent. The Second Silence is not just about Serith, or the dead gods, or the failing magic. It is about the slow dissolution of the barrier between creation and an alien reality that dwarfs it in every dimension.
>
> Use The Fathom sparingly. It should be felt, not confronted. A Star-Reader who looks too long through her telescope and cannot describe what she saw. A Diver who surfaces from the Trench with a calm, distant expression and never speaks of what he found at the bottom. A Black Tide that leaves behind sea-glass in shapes that correspond to no known geometry.
>
> The Fathom should make creation feel small, fragile, and precious. It is the darkness outside the campfire's light. It is the depth beneath the hull. It is the silence between the stars.

## GM Toolkit

### Campaign Hooks

| Hook | Connection | Suggested Act |
|---|---|---|
| Stars going dark over a specific region | The boundary is thinning locally, creating a zone of subtle wrongness | Act I-II |
| A Diver returns from the Trench changed, speaking of "the floor of the world" | Introduction to the boundary concept, leads to Lantern-Keeper investigation | Act I-II |
| Star-Reader research connects the Black Tides to stellar disappearances | Links the Hollowed Reach crisis to the cosmological crisis, Ashgrove's data | Act II-III |
| Aelindra sends a vision of what she sees beyond the boundary | First direct contact with Fathom awareness, deeply unsettling | Act III-IV |
| Serith reveals knowledge gained from her proximity to the boundary | Reframes Serith's motivations, she knows how fragile creation is | Act IV-V |
| The Unremembered begin manifesting in a region where stars have gone dark | Physical intrusion of alien forms into the mortal world | Act IV-V |

### Using Fathom Entities

- **Orvhal** is a source of knowledge, never a source of help. If players somehow contact it, it can show them truths, but it will not act on their behalf. It watches. It does not participate.
- **Ithael** is a structural concern. Its song can be perceived by characters with extreme magical sensitivity. Hearing it fade is a horror of the subtlest kind: the sound of the world's walls thinning.
- **The Unremembered** are the most versatile for encounters. Any creature, object, or phenomenon that is just slightly wrong, that follows rules that don't quite match reality, could be a touch of The Unremembered seeping through.

### Tone Guidelines

The Fathom is not a dungeon to be explored or a villain to be fought. It is a condition of existence. It should evoke:

- **Cosmic loneliness**: creation is very small, surrounded by something very large that does not know or care that it exists
- **Fragility**: everything mortals take for granted, physics, biology, perception, is a local phenomenon maintained by a failing boundary
- **Awe and unease in equal measure**: The Fathom is not ugly or hostile. It is vast and alien and profoundly, quietly terrifying
