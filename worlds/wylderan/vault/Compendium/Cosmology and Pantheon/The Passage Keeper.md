## Overview

The Passage Keeper was the god of rivers, crossings, commerce, and change. In mortal memory, they were the patron of travelers, traders, ferrymen, and all who lived by moving goods and people from place to place. The Keeper taught that water does not resist obstacles, it flows around them. That connection is power. That borders and boundaries exist only as long as mortals agree to respect them. Those who followed the Keeper's teachings valued adaptability, negotiation, and the dignity of honest trade. The god's symbols were the river and the coin, one representing flow, the other exchange.

The Passage Keeper fell during the Theomachis, the cataclysmic war that ended the age of living gods. Their death was unlike [[The Twelve#The Stone Warden|The Stone Warden]]'s petrification. Where the Warden turned inward and became stone, the Keeper dissolved. The god's body broke apart, fragmented into pieces that scattered along the Millrun River from headwaters to delta. The corpse did not coalesce. It spread. And now, seventy-five years later, the fragments are stirring, trying to reassemble, and the river is changing.

## Domain and Influence

**Passage and Crossings:** The Keeper embodied movement, the act of crossing from one place to another, one state to another. Fords, bridges, ferries, and river crossings were sacred to the god. In life, the Keeper's presence made crossings safer, currents predictable, and floodwaters less destructive. Travelers who prayed to the Keeper before crossing a river found the water calm and the footing solid. To cross without acknowledging the god was considered arrogant and dangerous.

**Rivers and Flow:** The Keeper was the god of moving water. Not the ocean's vastness or the mountain spring's purity, but the river's journey from source to sea. The god taught that rivers connect disparate places, that flow is inevitable, that change cannot be stopped only guided. River folk who worshipped the Keeper understood that the river's course might shift, but the river itself would endure. Dams and weirs were acceptable if they guided flow, unforgivable if they tried to stop it entirely.

**Commerce and Exchange:** The Keeper represented trade, particularly river trade. Barge captains, merchants, toll collectors, and market brokers invoked the god's name when negotiating deals or launching shipments. The Keeper taught that trade is sacred because it connects people who would otherwise be strangers, that fair exchange builds trust, and that a merchant who cheats will find the river turns against them. To swear a deal "by the Keeper's current" was binding. Breaking such an oath meant the river would refuse you passage.

**Change and Adaptation:** The Keeper valued flexibility over rigidity. "Yield, but do not break. Flow around obstacles. What resists too hard will shatter." This philosophy created tension with [[The Twelve#The Stone Warden|The Stone Warden]]'s teachings of endurance and unyielding strength. Highlanders who followed the Warden saw river folk as weak and opportunistic. River folk saw highlanders as brittle and doomed to break when the world changed. Both gods were right, and both were wrong.

## Death and Theomachis

Seventy-five years ago, the gods went to war. The Passage Keeper fell during the same cataclysm that killed [[The Twelve#The Stone Warden|The Stone Warden]], though the circumstances of their deaths differed.

Where the Warden's body crashed into the highlands and petrified in a single location, the Keeper's corpse broke apart. Whether the god was struck by a killing blow that shattered their divine form, or whether the Keeper chose dissolution over death, no one knows. What is known: the body fragmented into three major pieces and countless smaller shards, and these fragments fell along the length of the Millrun River.

The god's skull landed in the northern highlands where three streams converge, the Cold Run, Ashwater, and Piney Flow. The impact created the confluence that would later become [[Ashcrag]]'s founding site. The skull dissolved into the streambed, seeping into the rock and water, making the convergence point sacred geography even if mortals no longer recognize it.

The ribcage and spine fell at the point where the river slows and spreads wide, creating the ford. The divine bones did not petrify like the Warden's remains. They dissolved into the riverbed, creating the cobbled shallows that mortals now call [[Millford]]. The bones stabilize the current, hold the river's course, and prevent the ford from shifting. As long as the bones remain, the crossing is passable. If they decay further, the ford will fail.

The heart, the largest fragment, plunged into the delta where the Millrun splits into three channels before meeting the sea. It landed beneath the marshes, deep in the silt and salt water, and there it remains. The heart did not stop beating when the god died. It pulses, faintly, trying to pump divine essence back through the fragmented body. This is the source of "the pull."

## Divine Remains

The Passage Keeper's fragmented corpse defines the geography and supernatural behavior of the Millrun River:

**The Confluence (The Skull):**
Where the three highland streams meet at [[Ashcrag]], the riverbed holds the dissolved remains of the god's skull. The water here is clearer than it should be, the current smoother. Barge captains launching from the confluence report feeling a "blessing" as they enter the Millrun proper, a sense of safety that lasts until they reach the ford. Highlanders do not know the skull is there, but they sense the place is significant. Offerings are left at the confluence before long journeys, though the reasons have been forgotten.

**The Ford (The Ribcage and Spine):**
At [[Millford]], the divine bones create the cobbled shallows that allow safe crossing. The stones are not mortar-laid by mortal hands, they are fragments of divine bone that have partially mineralized over seventy-five years. The ford is stable because the bones hold the current in place, preventing the river from shifting course or deepening. But the bones are dissolving. Slower than the Warden's petrified remains, but still decaying. In another generation, the ford may wash away entirely.

**The Delta (The Heart):**
Beneath [[Saltmere]]'s marshes, deep in the silt where the river splits into the Westrun, Centerflow, and Saltcurrent channels, the god's heart rests. It is the size of a house, partially submerged, covered in barnacles and river weeds. And it still beats. Once per hour, a slow, grinding pulse that sends ripples through the delta. This is the source of "the pull," the supernatural downstream current that has begun dragging barges off course and into the delta's deep channels.

**Scattered Fragments:**
Smaller pieces of the god, ribs, vertebrae, fingers, are scattered along the riverbed between the major sites. Some have dissolved entirely. Others remain, creating localized phenomena: calm pools where the current should be fast, eddies that spin against the flow, sections of riverbank that never flood. River folk know these spots and navigate accordingly, but they attribute them to "the river's moods," not divine remains.

## Supernatural Influence

The Keeper's death was not clean, and divine decay continues seventy-five years later. The fragmented corpse is behaving strangely, creating supernatural phenomena along the Millrun:

**The Pull:**
The god's heart is trying to reassemble the body. It pulses, sending out divine will through the river, a command to "return, flow back, come together." This manifests as a supernatural downstream current that appears unpredictably, usually during autumn low water when the river's natural flow weakens. Barges caught in the pull are dragged toward the delta regardless of how hard the crew rows or poles against it. The pull is strengthening. What was once a rare nuisance is now a deadly threat. Three barges have been lost in the past year, dragged into the delta's deep channels and wrecked.

**Changing River Behavior:**
The Millrun's flow is becoming erratic. Spring floods arrive earlier and hit harder. Autumn low water is weaker than it was a generation ago. The river's course shifts slightly year by year, wearing away banks that used to be stable. Millers and barge captains attribute this to weather or logging upstream, but the truth is simpler: the god's bones are decaying, and the divine stabilization that held the river's course for millennia is failing.

**Ford Instability:**
The cobbled shallows at [[Millford]] are sinking. Slowly, imperceptibly to those who cross daily, but barge captains who have worked the ford for decades notice the water is deeper now than it was when they started. The divine bones are dissolving into silt. Within a generation, the ford may become impassable, cutting off highland-lowland trade entirely.

**Whispers in the Current:**
Some barge captains report hearing voices in the river at night, especially near the ford or in the delta. The voices do not speak words, they sound like water flowing over stones, like grinding silt, like something vast and broken trying to remember its name. Ferrymen dismiss this as exhaustion or drink, but the whispers are real. They are the god's fragmented consciousness, scattered across the river, trying to communicate or reassemble or simply understand why it is broken.

## Cultural Legacy

The Passage Keeper is dead, but the god's teachings survive in river culture, particularly among barge captains, ferrymen, and traders:

**Ford Blessings:**
Before crossing the ford at [[Millford]], some travelers still touch the water and whisper, "Keeper, grant safe passage." It is a rote gesture now, more superstition than prayer, but it descends from the god's teachings. The words have shortened over generations. The original prayer was longer, a formal request for the god's blessing and a promise to respect the river. Now it is three words, mumbled quickly, but the intent remains.

**Barge Oaths:**
Serious deals struck on barges or at riverside markets are sworn "by the current." To swear by the current is to invoke the Keeper's authority, to say, "If I lie, may the river refuse me passage." Breaking such an oath is believed to bring divine retribution: capsized barges, failed crossings, cargo lost to flood. [[Dace Torrn]] and [[Lira Fenn]], both veteran river traders, have sworn by the current and keep their oaths scrupulously.

**Toll Origins:**
The tolls collected at [[Millford]]'s ford descend from the god's teachings, though their meaning has been corrupted. Originally, travelers paid offerings to the Keeper, not to mortals. The offerings were symbolic, a coin, a piece of bread, a carved token, left at shrines on both sides of the crossing. After the god's death, enterprising toll collectors claimed the offerings as revenue. The Crown formalized this as a tax. Now [[Lyman Corvish]] collects crossing fees on behalf of the Crown, and no one remembers the money once belonged to a god.

**River Prayers:**
Barge captains launching from the confluence at [[Ashcrag]] sometimes pour a cup of wine into the water and say, "Flow steady, return safe." It is not a prayer to the Keeper, it is a prayer to the river itself, but the river and the god are the same thing. The ritual acknowledges that the river is alive, that it has will, and that respect must be paid.

## GM Notes

> [!warning]- Campaign Hooks & Secrets

**Divine Reassembly Accelerating:**
The Passage Keeper's heart is pulsing faster. What was once an hourly beat is now every forty minutes. The pull is strengthening, and the god's fragments are responding. If this continues, the river will become a one-way current. Nothing will be able to travel upstream. Highland-lowland trade will collapse. [[Millford]] and [[Saltmere]] will starve. The god is not choosing this. The divine will to reassemble is instinctive, like a dying animal crawling toward water.

**The Ford Will Fail:**
The cobbled crossing at [[Millford]] is held in place by divine bones that are dissolving. Within 10-20 years, the ford will wash away. The crossing will deepen to the point where wagons cannot pass. Highland-lowland trade will require a bridge, which no faction has the resources to build, or a ferry monopoly, which will spark economic war. PCs who investigate the ford's instability will find the bones, and realize the entire regional economy is built on a foundation that is literally dissolving.

**What Happens If the God Reassembles:**
If the fragments are drawn together, if the heart pulls the skull and spine back into the delta, what emerges will not be the Passage Keeper. It will be a broken, incomplete, insane echo of a god. A thing that wants to be whole but cannot remember how. The river will become a living nightmare, currents that change hourly, whirlpools that appear and vanish, the entire Millrun thrashing like a wounded animal.

**Campaign-Scale Threats:**
- PCs discover a cult attempting to "help" the god reassemble by dragging the skull from the confluence to the delta
- The heart's pulse reaches critical frequency, triggering a supernatural flood that wipes out [[Saltmere]]
- The ford collapses mid-crossing, drowning a caravan and sparking a crisis as highland and lowland factions blame each other
- A fragment of the god, a rib or vertebrae, is discovered by miners and sold as valuable ore, destabilizing the river when it is removed

**Faction Implications:**
- [[Saltmere Merchant Assembly]] knows about the pull but is covering it up to prevent economic panic
- [[Millford]] millers are unaware the ford is failing, but [[Brenna Cray]] has noticed the water deepening and suspects something is wrong
- Highland factions dismiss the pull as lowland superstition, unaware the god's skull is beneath their river source
- If the god's remains are revealed as sacred, who controls them? Who has the right to "fix" the river or let it fail?
