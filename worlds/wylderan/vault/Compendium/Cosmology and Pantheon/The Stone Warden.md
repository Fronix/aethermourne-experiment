## Overview

The Stone Warden was the god of mountains, stone, metalworking, and endurance. In mortal memory, they were the patron of miners, smiths, and all who labored in stone or worked metal. The Warden taught that strength is not yielding, that mountains endure while rivers change course, that what is forged in fire and shaped by hammer outlasts what is grown or woven. Those who followed the Warden's teachings valued unyielding resolve, self-sufficiency, and the dignity of hard labor. The god's symbols were the hammer and the mountain, tools of creation and symbols of permanence.

The Stone Warden fell during the Theomachis, the cataclysmic war that ended the age of living gods. Their body crashed into the highlands, and upon impact, the divine flesh petrified, turning to stone and bone and cold iron. The corpse became the land itself, the ridgeline and bedrock of what mortals now call The Stone Heights. The god's death did not end their influence. The divine remains continue to shape the land, the climate, and the people who live atop the god's grave.

## Domain and Influence

**Stone and Mountains:** The Stone Warden embodied permanence, the slow-grinding patience of stone shaped by millennia, the weight of mountains that cannot be moved. In life, the god's presence made stone easier to work, iron ore richer, and mountain passes safer. Miners who prayed to the Warden found veins where others saw only barren rock. Masons who invoked the god's name built walls that stood for centuries.

**Metalworking:** The Warden was patron to smiths and metalworkers, particularly those who worked iron and steel. The god taught that metal is stone's blood, that ore is a gift from the earth, and that the forge is sacred ground. Guild traditions of apprenticeship, mastery, and forge blessing ceremonies descend from the Warden's teachings. A smith who forges in the Warden's name swears to honor the metal, waste nothing, and take pride in their craft.

**Endurance:** The Warden represented survival through unyielding strength. Endure the cold. Survive the winter. Outlast the storm. Do not break, do not yield, do not compromise. This philosophy shaped the cultures that worshipped the Warden, particularly highland communities where harsh winters and short growing seasons demanded resilience. To be called "stone-hearted" in the Warden's tradition was a compliment, a recognition of one's ability to endure hardship without complaint.

**Labor:** The Warden valued work, particularly physical labor that left marks on the body: calloused hands, scarred forearms, backs bent from lifting stone. The god taught that work defines worth, that a life of labor is honorable, and that those who create with their hands are closer to the divine than those who profit from the work of others. This created a cultural divide between laborers (miners, smiths, farmers) and merchants or creditors, who were seen as parasites feeding on others' labor.

## Death and Theomachis

Seventy-five years ago, the gods went to war. The reasons are lost, or were never known to mortals. Scholars call this event the Theomachis, the war of gods, though few alive today remember it firsthand. What is known: the gods killed each other, and when they fell, their bodies did not decay like mortal flesh. They petrified, burned, drowned, or shattered into fragments that scarred the world.

The Stone Warden fell in the highlands, far from the coastal battlefields where other gods clashed. Whether the Warden was fleeing, pursuing, or simply in the wrong place when the killing blow landed, no one knows. The god's body crashed into the highland ridgeline with such force that the impact reshaped the terrain. Mountains buckled. Valleys split. Rivers changed course. And when the dust settled, the Warden's corpse had turned to stone.

The god's spine became the exposed rocky peaks north of [[Ironhollow]], jagged and cold and lifeless. The ribcage and torso became the bedrock beneath the highland valleys, close enough to the surface that mortal plows strike stone after inches of soil. The divine blood seeped into the earth and mineralized, creating the iron ore veins that miners now extract. And the god's coldness, the divine essence of endurance and unyielding strength, did not die. It radiated outward from the remains, making the winters harsher, the soil thinner, and the land harder than it should be.

In the immediate aftermath of the Theomachis, the Crown's authority collapsed. Kingdoms fractured. Trade routes broke. Highland regions like [[The Stone Heights]] were abandoned by central governance, left to survive on their own. The highlanders did survive, because they followed the Warden's teachings even in death: endure, do not yield, outlast the chaos. They built forges atop the god's remains and called the place [[Ashcrag]]. They dug mines into the god's bones and called it [[Ironhollow]]. They learned to live on stone.

## Divine Remains

The Stone Warden's petrified corpse defines the geography of [[The Stone Heights]]:

**Northern Peaks (The Spine):**
The exposed rocky peaks north of [[Ironhollow]] are the Warden's spine, vertebrae turned to stone and thrust upward by the impact of the god's fall. These peaks are barren, windswept, and cold. No trees grow on them. Snow lingers into late spring. Miners avoid the highest peaks, claiming the stone there is cursed, that tools break against it, and that those who camp overnight hear grinding sounds like bones shifting beneath the earth.

**Highland Bedrock (The Flesh):**
The thin soil of the highland valleys sits atop the Warden's petrified flesh. Farmers in [[Ashcrag]] know that plowing deeper than a foot risks striking bedrock. The god's body is just beneath the surface, too close to allow deep-rooted crops or forests. This is why the highlands produce short-season grain and thin timber, why the soil is poor compared to the lowlands. The land itself is stone pretending to be earth.

**Iron Ore Veins (The Blood):**
The iron ore extracted from [[Ironhollow]]'s mines is the Warden's mineralized blood, or perhaps bone marrow, depending on which miner's tale you believe. The ore is rich but finite. Surface veins that were easy to extract forty years ago are now nearly exhausted. Miners must dig deeper, and the deeper they go, the stranger the ore becomes. Some veins pulse faintly when struck. Some bleed rust-red water. Some resist tools as if the metal itself does not want to be taken.

**The Cold (The Will):**
The Warden's divine essence, the god's will to endure and never yield, persists in the stone. It manifests as cold. Winters in [[The Stone Heights]] are harsher than they should be for the latitude. Blizzards appear without warning. Frost comes early and lingers late. The cold is not natural, it is divine, radiating from the god's remains like heat from a forge, except inverted. Highlanders who camp on bare stone in winter report feeling the cold seep upward through their bedrolls, as if the earth itself is trying to freeze them.

## Supernatural Influence

The Warden's death was not clean. Divine decay is slow, and seventy-five years is only a moment in the lifespan of a god's corpse. The remains are still dying, still leaking divine essence into the land, and this creates supernatural phenomena:

**Stone-Cursed Creatures:**
Wildlife exposed to the god's remains over generations develops petrifying mutations. Deer with stone-encrusted antlers that cannot shed them. Wolves with stone hides resistant to blades. Boars with tusks that grow inward, piercing their own skulls, turning them mad with pain. These creatures are rare, found only in the deep forests near the northern peaks or in abandoned mine shafts where divine decay concentrates. Highlanders call them "the Warden's children" and consider them omens of bad luck.

**Ore Madness:**
Miners who work deep in the [[Ironhollow]] escarpment, cutting ore from veins close to the god's bones, sometimes develop obsessive behaviors. They hear whispers in the rock, voices that sound like grinding stone. They become convinced that one more strike, one more foot deeper, will reveal a vein of pure divine ore that will make them rich. They neglect food, sleep, and safety, chasing the whispers until they collapse from exhaustion or die in cave-ins. The [[Highland Merchant Cartel]] calls it exhaustion psychosis. Miners call it the Warden's Curse, punishment for taking too much from the god's body.

**Cold Sickness:**
In certain places, pockets where the god's coldness concentrates, the supernatural chill is deadly. Travelers who camp in these zones develop frostbite in minutes, even in summer. The cold bypasses clothing, sinks into bones, and refuses to leave. Victims report feeling as if their blood is turning to ice, their joints stiffening like stone. If not moved to warmth immediately, they die, and their bodies are found frozen solid with expressions of terror. Highlanders mark these zones with cairns and avoid them. The [[Halfway Burn Site]] is rumored to be one such place, which is why the charcoal kilns were abandoned.

**Living Ore Veins:**
Some of the deepest ore veins in [[Ironhollow]] are not inert metal. They shift when miners approach, retreating into the rock face. They bleed rust-red water when struck, as if the stone itself feels pain. Miners have reported veins that "close" after being mined, the ore healing like wounded flesh. The cartel dismisses these reports as superstition, but experienced miners know which veins to avoid. Cutting into a living vein brings cave-ins, equipment failure, and bad luck that follows the crew for months.

## Cultural Legacy

The Stone Warden is dead, but the god's teachings and worship survive in highland culture, particularly in [[Ashcrag]] and among smiths and miners:

**Stone Shrine at The Confluence:**
At the point where the three highland streams converge in [[Ashcrag]], a small stone shrine sits on the south bank. It is older than the town, built by the first settlers who recognized the Warden's presence in the bedrock. Offerings are left here before barge launches, before forge blessings, and before dangerous mine work. The offerings are simple: a nail, a piece of ore, a stone taken from the god's peaks and returned with thanks. The shrine is maintained by no one and everyone. To damage it would be unthinkable.

**Forge Blessing Rituals:**
Guild masters in [[Ashcrag]] invoke the Warden's name when lighting a new forge or beginning a major commission. The ritual is brief: a piece of iron ore is heated until it glows, then quenched in water while the smith recites, "Stone yields to fire, fire yields to water, water yields to stone. We forge in your name, Warden, and honor the metal." The quenched ore is kept in the forge as a charm against bad luck. [[Myra Cadros]] and [[Hal Brennan]] both maintain this tradition, though they argue about the proper wording.

**Miner Prayers:**
Before entering the [[Ironhollow]] mines, some miners touch the rock face and whisper, "Warden, grant passage. We take only what we need." It is not a prayer for safety, it is a request for permission, an acknowledgment that the ore belongs to the god and miners are borrowing it. Miners who skip this ritual are considered reckless. When a cave-in kills someone, other miners will quietly ask, "Did they pray?" The implication: the Warden punished them for taking without asking.

**Stone Oaths:**
Serious vows in [[The Stone Heights]] are sworn "on stone" or "on the Warden's bones." To swear on stone is to invoke the god's unyielding nature, to say, "I will not break this promise, as stone does not break." Breaking a stone oath is believed to bring divine curse: bad luck, failed ventures, early death. [[Orrin Pike]] has sworn his coalition oath on stone. [[Aldric Harth]] swore his council oath on stone. Both men are now trapped by their promises, unable to compromise without invoking the Warden's wrath.

## GM Notes

> [!warning]- Campaign Hooks & Secrets

**Divine Decay Accelerating:**
The Stone Warden's remains are not stable. Over the past seventy-five years, the divine coldness has intensified. Winters are longer now than they were a generation ago. The ore veins are behaving strangely. Stone-cursed creatures are appearing more frequently. Something is wrong with the god's corpse, and it is getting worse. If this continues, [[The Stone Heights]] may become uninhabitable within a generation.

**Potential Resurrection Attempts:**
If the god is truly dead, why does the divine essence persist? Some scholars theorize that gods do not die the way mortals do, that divine death is a state, not an ending. If divine death can be reversed, the Warden's corpse is a resurrection site waiting for someone desperate or insane enough to attempt it. A cult, a desperate smith, or a faction seeking divine power might try to "awaken" the Warden. The consequences would be catastrophic.

**The Warden's Will:**
What if the god is not fully dead? What if the coldness, the ore madness, the stone-cursed creatures are not decay but the god's dying will asserting itself? The Warden valued endurance. Perhaps the god is enduring death itself, refusing to let go, clinging to existence through the stone. If true, the Stone Heights is not a corpse, it is a prison, and the god is the prisoner.

**Campaign-Scale Threats:**
- PCs discover a cult attempting to resurrect the Warden beneath [[Ironhollow]]
- Divine decay reaches critical point, triggering supernatural blizzard that lasts months
- Living ore veins begin to spread, "infecting" other metal in the region, turning tools and weapons into semi-living constructs
- The Warden's skull is discovered in the northern peaks, and something inside it is still awake

**Faction Implications:**
- [[Forge Council]] claims legitimacy through stone oaths, but if the Warden "wakes," who does the god support?
- [[Highland Workers Coalition]] swears oaths on the Warden's bones, invoking divine authority against the council, a theological civil war
- [[Highland Merchant Cartel]] is mining the god's remains for profit, unaware they are destabilizing the corpse
- Lowland factions see the Warden's remains as resources to exploit, not as sacred, creating cultural conflict
