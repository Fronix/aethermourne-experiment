# Corwin Marsh

## Appearance

Corwin is a well-fed man in his late fifties with silver hair and a trimmed beard that frames a face used to smiling without meaning it. He dresses like old money: fine wool in muted colors, a gold signet ring on his right hand, boots that never see mud. His hands are soft, unmarked by labor. He carries himself with the ease of someone who knows his power is structural, not physical. He smells like beeswax and expensive tobacco. His voice is smooth, measured, and never raised, which somehow makes it more commanding.

## Personality

Corwin is patient, calculating, and utterly unsentimental about business. He does not gloat or threaten, he simply states facts and lets others draw their own conclusions. When someone owes him money, he does not demand payment, he inquires about their progress with the tone of a concerned mentor. This makes him more terrifying than any bully. He believes in the long game: a desperate debtor is a liability, but a profitable debtor is an asset. He will forgive interest, extend terms, even advance more capital if it means securing a more valuable position later. Beneath this, he is deeply lonely. His children scattered to distant cities, his wife dead for a decade. He has wealth, influence, and no one to share it with. He tells himself he prefers it this way.

## Backstory

Corwin was born to a middling merchant family in Saltmere. His father dealt in cloth and salt, profitable but unremarkable. Corwin saw early that the real wealth was not in goods, but in debts. He began lending to farmers, then to millers, then to other merchants. He foreclosed on the desperate, bought low, sold high. He was ruthless in his twenties, compassionate in his forties, and calculating always. By fifty, he owned or held debt on half the grain trade along the Millrun River. He could have retired wealthy. Instead, he kept building, because what else was there?

The formative event: Fifteen years ago, Corwin lent a significant sum to a highland miller named Aldric Cray to rebuild after a flood. Cray's pride refused Corwin's terms, and Cray eventually sold the wreckage to [[Garrick Thane]] at a loss. Corwin learned that pride was a liability in debtors. When Garrick came to him ten years ago asking for capital to build Southgrain Mill, Corwin gave generous terms with one condition: Garrick would owe him, indefinitely, until Corwin decided otherwise. Garrick agreed. Garrick still owes.

## Relationships

- [[Garrick Thane]] — Garrick is Corwin's most valuable asset. Corwin holds the debt that could destroy Garrick, but he will not call it in as long as Garrick remains profitable. Garrick knows this and resents it. Corwin finds Garrick's resentment amusing and predictable. Their relationship is cordial on the surface, tense beneath. Corwin sends occasional letters "checking in" on Garrick's business. Garrick hates those letters.
- [[Ossian Greave]] — A lowland trader who operates in Corwin's sphere. Ossian is smaller, cruder, more desperate. Corwin views him as a useful tool for collecting smaller debts that are not worth Corwin's personal attention. Ossian resents being dismissed but knows Corwin could crush him if provoked.
- [[Dace Torrn]] — River trader. Dace hauls grain for merchants who sell to Corwin's network. Corwin has never met Dace personally but knows his reputation: reliable, slippery, worth keeping an eye on. If Dace ever needed capital, Corwin would be interested.
- [[Brenna Cray]] — Daughter of Aldric Cray. Corwin watched her rebuild her father's mill with borrowed money from everyone but him. He respects her tenacity and thinks she is wasting it on revenge. If she ever approached him, he would offer her a deal that would save her and bind her. She will never approach him.

## What They Want

Corwin wants legacy. He wants to be remembered as the architect of the Riverlands' prosperity, the man who financed the mills and markets that fed the region. He wants monuments, not gold.

What he needs is connection. He has spent decades building a network of debts and dependencies, and it has left him isolated. He needs to let go of control and trust someone, but he does not know how.

> [!warning]- GM Only
> **Campaign Role.** Corwin is the economic power behind the throne. He does not appear in Millford often, but his influence is everywhere. If the PCs investigate Garrick's vulnerability or the grain trade, they will eventually trace it back to Corwin. He is not an enemy unless they make him one, but he is dangerous because his power is structural. Taking him down would destabilize the entire region's economy.
>
> **Key Secret.** Corwin's network is starting to crack. Several of his debtors have defaulted recently, including the owner of a missing barge that disappeared three weeks ago. That barge was carrying Garrick's grain, insured by Corwin. If the barge is not found, Corwin will have to pay out the insurance, and he cannot afford another loss. He is quietly investigating the disappearance and will pay well for information.
>
> **Arc Trajectory.** If unchallenged, Corwin will continue to consolidate power, eventually calling in Garrick's debt when it suits him. If the PCs destabilize his network (expose his debtors, disrupt trade, find evidence of his insurance fraud), he will become an active antagonist. If they approach him honestly and offer valuable information (like the location of the missing barge), he will become a powerful, if morally ambiguous, ally.
>
> **Roleplay Notes.** Corwin speaks in complete, measured sentences. He pauses before answering, as if weighing every word. He never interrupts. He asks clarifying questions that reveal he already knows the answer. When he is angry, he gets quieter, not louder. Sample line: "Garrick is a capable man. I have every confidence he will meet his obligations. But I appreciate your concern. It is rare to meet someone who understands the fragility of prosperity."
