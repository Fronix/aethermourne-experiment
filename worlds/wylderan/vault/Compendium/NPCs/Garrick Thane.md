# Garrick Thane

## Appearance

Garrick is a broad-shouldered man in his late forties with flour-dusted forearms and calloused hands that betray years of physical labor, though he no longer works the stones himself. His graying beard is meticulously trimmed, a sign of the prosperity that came after decades of grinding work. He wears well-made but practical wool coats, dyed a deep charcoal that doesn't show the dust. His boots are good leather, resoled twice. He moves deliberately, with the confidence of someone who controls the flow of grain, and therefore food, through the entire community.

## Personality

Garrick projects competence and control. He speaks in measured tones, weighing his words like he weighs grain measures. He's not cruel, but he's unsentimental about business. When farmers complain about his rates, he gestures at his account books and explains margins with the patience of a teacher correcting a slow student. He believes in the fairness of leverage: he worked for everything he has, and if others want what he has, they should work as hard. Beneath this, there's a deep anxiety that someone will take it all away, that his son won't be able to hold it, that the river will shift and leave his mills stranded. He doesn't sleep well.

## Backstory

Garrick was born the third son of a tenant farmer. No inheritance, no prospects. At fifteen, he apprenticed to Old Tam, the original miller, and worked the stones for twelve years. When Tam died without heirs, Garrick married Tam's widow, Marda, though she was fifteen years his senior. She died three winters later, and Garrick inherited the mill. He built the second mill ten years ago using loans from a merchant in the lowlands, loans he's still repaying. That debt is a constant weight.

The formative event: Fifteen years ago, a spring flood destroyed the upstream mill owned by the Cray family. Garrick offered to buy the wreckage and rebuild it, but on his terms. The Crays refused out of pride, and Garrick waited. When their debts mounted, he bought it for a quarter of what he'd originally offered. The Crays never forgave him. Neither did [[Brenna Cray]], who rebuilt her family's mill with borrowed money and raw determination.

## Relationships

- [[Brenna Cray]] — Rival miller, upstream. He respects her tenacity but sees her as a threat. She hates him for taking advantage of her family's ruin. The feud is personal for her, business for him, which makes it worse.
- [[Osmund Vey]] — Senior farmer and community voice. Osmund negotiates on behalf of the smaller farmers. Garrick finds him exhausting but knows he's necessary to prevent open revolt. They have an unspoken agreement: Osmund keeps the farmers in line, Garrick doesn't raise rates more than once a year.
- [[Dace Torrn]] — River trader. Garrick sells surplus grain through Dace, bypassing the toll collector when possible. They're not friends, but they have a profitable understanding.
- [[Halford Quick]] — Dock master. Garrick pays Halford a yearly "consideration" to prioritize barges carrying his grain. It's not quite a bribe, but it's not quite legitimate either.

## What They Want

Garrick wants security: enough wealth that his son, Edric, won't have to scrape and beg and marry for inheritance like he did. He wants the respect that comes with power, the kind where people defer to him not out of fear but because they recognize his competence.

What he needs is to let go. He's so afraid of losing what he's built that he can't see he's choking the community, breeding resentment that will eventually turn on him or Edric. He needs to trust that the mills will run without his constant, grinding control.

> [!warning]- GM Only
> **Campaign Role.** Garrick is the economic anchor of Millford. He's not a villain, but his control creates the scarcity and tension that drives other conflicts. If PCs investigate trade disruptions or grain shortages, they'll encounter him. He's pragmatic and will work with PCs if it serves his interests.
>
> **Key Secret.** Garrick is still deeply in debt to a lowland merchant named Corwin Marsh. If Marsh called in the debt, Garrick would lose both mills. Marsh hasn't, yet, because Garrick's dominance of Millford's grain market is more profitable to Marsh than foreclosure. But this means Marsh has enormous leverage over Garrick.
>
> **Arc Trajectory.** If unchallenged, Garrick will consolidate further, possibly squeezing out Brenna entirely. If the PCs intervene (or if external events like war or famine disrupt trade), his debt will come due and he'll face ruin. He might become desperate, making poor decisions. Or he might finally delegate to Edric and step back, finding unexpected relief.
>
> **Roleplay Notes.** Garrick speaks in short, declarative sentences. He doesn't waste words. When he's stressed, he rubs flour dust between his thumb and forefinger absently, checking the texture. Sample line: "I don't set the rates to be liked, Osmund. I set them to keep the stones turning. You want cheaper flour, go talk to the river about raising less silt."
