## Appearance

Halven is a lean man in his late 60s with gray hair pulled back in a tight knot and a face like parchment, thin, pale, and covered in fine wrinkles. He wears wire-rimmed spectacles perched on his nose and is never seen without his leather apron and a quill tucked behind his ear. His hands are ink-stained and steady, moving with practiced precision as he records weights and calculates debts. He walks with a cane, not from injury but from age, and he moves slowly, deliberately, as if every step is measured. He smells like lamp oil, old paper, and the metallic tang of iron dust. His expression is neutral by default, revealing nothing, a mask worn so long it has become his face.

## Personality

Halven is meticulous, calculating, and utterly devoid of empathy. He does not see miners as people, he sees them as entries in a ledger: names, debts, production numbers. He claims neutrality, insisting he is merely recording transactions and enforcing contracts, but this neutrality is a lie he tells himself. He knows the scales are rigged. He knows the weights are off. He knows every time he underrecords a miner's ore delivery, he is stealing from people who have nothing. He does it anyway because the [[Highland Merchant Cartel]] pays him well, protects him from retribution, and gives him the one thing he values above all else: order. Halven hates chaos. He hates uncertainty. The ledger is truth, the scales are law, and as long as he maintains them, the system functions. That the system crushes people is irrelevant to him. Systems do not care, and neither does he.

## Backstory

Halven was born in [[Saltmere]], the son of a clerk who worked for the Merchant Assembly. He grew up surrounded by ledgers, contracts, and trade tallies, learning to read and write before he learned to fish. He was good with numbers, exceptional even, and by twenty he was working as an accountant for a grain merchant. He spent thirty years in [[Saltmere]], living a quiet, comfortable life, until a business dispute went bad. Halven was accused of embezzling funds, falsifying records to skim coin from his employer. The accusation was true. He was caught, fired, and blacklisted from the Merchant Assembly.

He fled north to [[Ashcrag]], looking for work, and was hired by the [[Highland Merchant Cartel]] as a debt clerk. He spent ten years tracking loans and foreclosures before [[Tallow Breck]] offered him a position in [[Ironhollow]] as weighmaster. Halven accepted because the job was isolated, paid well, and asked no questions about his past. He has been in [[Ironhollow]] for fifteen years, running the scale house, rigging weights, and recording transactions that keep miners in permanent debt. He tells himself this is just accounting, and that he is doing his job. He does not think about the families broken by his ledger. He does not think about anything except the numbers.

The formative event: Twenty-five years ago, Halven embezzled money from his employer in [[Saltmere]] and got caught. He expected punishment, exile, maybe prison. Instead, his employer offered him a choice: return the money and disappear quietly, or face public trial and ruin. Halven returned the money. He learned two things that day: the system protects those who serve it, and morality is negotiable. Since then, he has served every system that paid him, and he has never questioned what the system does.

## Relationships

- [[Caden Wroth]] — Employer and protector. Halven works for Caden, rigging the scales to ensure miners stay in debt and the cartel maximizes profit. Caden protects Halven from miner retribution because Halven is useful, and Halven is loyal because Caden keeps him safe. They have a professional relationship built on mutual exploitation. Halven does not like Caden, he finds him crude and violent, but he respects his efficiency.
- [[Senna Kade]] — Threat. Halven knows Senna is organizing miners to strike, and he sees her as a threat to the system he maintains. If miners stop working, the scale house stops functioning, and Halven loses his purpose. He has reported Senna's activities to Caden and suggested she be dealt with before the strike can materialize. He does not hate her, he simply sees her as a problem that needs to be removed.
- [[Marna Tess]] — Colleague. Marna runs the company store, tracking debt through purchases, while Halven tracks debt through ore production. They compare ledgers regularly to ensure no miner escapes the system. They have a professional respect for each other's work, but no personal connection. Halven appreciates Marna's ruthlessness and her lack of sentimentality. She understands that the ledger is what matters, not the people behind the names.
- [[Jorin Velk]] — Anomaly. Jorin works outside the cartel system, which offends Halven's sense of order. Halven has refused to weigh Jorin's ore, forcing him to haul it to [[Ashcrag]] and sell it through [[Grett Voss]]. Halven sees Jorin as a disruption, a loose thread in the system that should be cut. He has told Caden that Jorin's independence sets a bad example and should be crushed.
- [[Tallow Breck]] — Distant superior. Halven reports to Breck quarterly, sending ledgers of ore production, debt accumulation, and miner turnover. Breck trusts Halven to maintain the system and rarely intervenes. Halven respects Breck's detachment and professionalism. Breck does not ask Halven to justify his methods, and Halven does not volunteer information. They understand each other.

## What They Want

Halven wants the system to function smoothly. He wants miners to bring ore, the scales to weigh it, the ledgers to record it, and the cartel to profit. He wants no disruptions, no strikes, no questions. He wants to keep his job, collect his pay, and maintain the order that gives his life meaning. He does not want to think about the human cost, because thinking about it would force him to confront what he has become.

What he needs is to face the fact that the system he serves is built on suffering, and that his neutrality is complicity. He tells himself he is just following orders, just doing his job, but the miners he cheats are not numbers, they are people, and they die because of what he does. He needs to choose, either acknowledge his guilt and change, or accept that he is a willing participant in exploitation. He is terrified of both options.

> [!warning]- GM Only
> **Campaign Role.** Halven is the bureaucratic face of cartel oppression, the ledger as weapon. PCs investigating ore theft, miner debt, or cartel operations will encounter Halven as an obstacle. He is not a fighter, he is a functionary who uses paperwork and rules to enforce control. If the PCs confront him, he will cite contracts and regulations. If they threaten him, he will call for [[Caden Wroth]]. If they try to bribe him, he will consider it, because his loyalty is to the system, not to morality.
>
> **Key Secret.** Halven has been rigging the scales for fifteen years, systematically undercounting miner ore deliveries by 5-10% per load. The stolen ore is recorded as "waste" and resold by the cartel at full price. Halven receives a percentage of the profit. If this is exposed, the miners will riot, and the cartel will sacrifice Halven to protect [[Caden Wroth]] and [[Tallow Breck]]. Halven knows this and is terrified of being caught, but he cannot stop because he needs the money to maintain his lifestyle.
>
> **Arc Trajectory.** If unchallenged, Halven will continue rigging scales, tracking debts, and enabling cartel exploitation. If the PCs expose his fraud, he will be forced to flee [[Ironhollow]] or face miner justice. If [[Senna Kade]]'s strike succeeds and the system collapses, Halven will lose his purpose and spiral into despair, because the ledger was all he had. If the PCs offer him a way out, redemption or protection, he might turn on the cartel and provide evidence of their operations.
>
> **Roleplay Notes.** Halven speaks in short, precise sentences, citing exact weights and figures. He refers to his ledger constantly, flipping to specific pages and reading entries aloud. He does not make eye contact unless recording information. When challenged, he does not defend himself, he cites regulations. He never apologizes, never explains, never justifies. Sample line: "Your ore weighed four hundred twelve pounds. At current rates, that is twenty-one silver credit. Your outstanding debt is three hundred forty-seven silver. You remain in deficit. Next miner, step forward."
