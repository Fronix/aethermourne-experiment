# Lyman Corvish

## Appearance

Lyman is a thin, sharp-featured man in his early forties with pale skin and dark hair slicked back with oil. He dresses like someone who wants to be taken seriously: a long coat with brass buttons, good boots, and a leather satchel for his ledgers and official documents. He wears a silver chain with the crown's seal, which he touches when he wants to remind people of his authority. His hands are soft, uncalloused, and he moves stiffly, like someone uncomfortable in his own skin. He smells faintly of ink and saddle leather.

## Personality

Lyman is officious, petty, and deeply insecure. He clings to the rules because they're the only source of power he has. He takes his role as toll collector seriously, not because he believes in it but because it's the only thing that makes him matter. He's condescending to the locals, whom he sees as provincial and beneath him, but he's also terrified of them. He knows they hate him, and he doesn't know how to make them not hate him, so he leans harder into his authority. He's meticulous, paranoid, and lonely. He writes long reports to his superiors in the capital, reports that are probably never read.

## Backstory

Lyman was born in the capital, the youngest son of a minor clerk. He was educated enough to read and write but not connected enough to secure a position of real power. Toll collector was the best he could get: a posting on the edge of civilization, collecting taxes from people who despise him. He's been in Millford for five years and hates every moment of it. He lives in a small, rented room above the general store and eats his meals alone.

What Lyman doesn't tell anyone: the Corvish family were ford-keepers at Millford generations ago, before the role was secularized by the Crown. His great-grandfather kept the shrine to the Passage Keeper and collected offerings from travelers, not taxes. Lyman found his great-grandfather's ledger in the family's archives, a faded book listing "offerings" rather than "tolls," with prayers and blessings written in the margins. He brought it with him to Millford, though he's never shown it to anyone. He resents that what was once a sacred duty has been reduced to tax collection, and he resents even more that no one here remembers or cares.

The formative event: Three years ago, Lyman caught a group of farmers smuggling grain past the toll point. He confiscated the grain and fined them heavily, following the letter of the law. One of the farmers, an old man named Harver, couldn't pay the fine and lost his land. Harver's family blamed Lyman, and someone painted "Crown's Thief" on his door. Lyman requested a transfer. It was denied. He stays because he has nowhere else to go, and because transferring would be admitting failure.

## Relationships

- [[Dace Torrn]] — Lyman has a complicated, adversarial relationship with Dace, who smuggles regularly. Lyman knows Dace is feeding him false information, Dace knows Lyman knows, and they're locked in a mutual game. Lyman finds it exhausting and humiliating.
- [[Osmund Vey]] — Lyman sees Osmund as the leader of the farmers and treats him with thinly veiled contempt. Osmund avoids Lyman when possible and refuses to engage when Lyman tries to provoke him. This frustrates Lyman immensely.
- [[Halford Quick]] — Halford is professionally polite to Lyman and cooperates with inspections, but Lyman suspects (correctly) that Halford tips off smugglers. Lyman can't prove it and doesn't have the authority to investigate Halford, which drives him mad.
- [[Garrick Thane]] — Lyman respects Garrick because Garrick keeps clean records, pays his taxes on time, and doesn't argue. Lyman wishes everyone were like Garrick. He doesn't realize Garrick achieves this by smuggling a significant portion of his grain through Dace.

## What They Want

Lyman wants respect. He wants people to acknowledge that he's doing an important job, that the crown's laws matter, that he matters. He wants a transfer to a better posting, somewhere he's not surrounded by people who hate him. He wants to prove to his family (who barely write) that he's not a failure.

What he needs is to let go of the impossible standard he's holding himself to. He's trying to enforce laws that are unjust, in a system that doesn't care about him, for people who will never appreciate him. He needs to choose: be a human being and bend the rules, or accept that he's the villain in everyone else's story.

> [!warning]- GM Only
> **Campaign Role.** Lyman is the outsider, the representative of distant authority that extracts wealth and provides nothing. He's a natural antagonist for PCs involved in smuggling or local resistance, but he's also pitiable. He's not cruel or corrupt, he's just trying to do his job in an impossible situation. He could become an ally if the PCs show him kindness or a way out.
>
> **Key Secret.** Lyman has been skimming a tiny amount from the tolls he collects, not for profit but to pay for the bribes and "considerations" he needs to survive in Millford. If his superiors audited him, he'd be arrested. He's trapped in a cycle of minor corruption to maintain his position, which makes him more paranoid and desperate.
>
> **Arc Trajectory.** If unchallenged, Lyman will continue grinding through his duties, becoming more bitter and isolated. A major smuggling operation or a direct confrontation with the locals could push him to either crack down (making things worse) or break entirely. If the PCs befriend him or show him a way to escape his role with dignity, he might become an unlikely ally.
>
> **Roleplay Notes.** Lyman speaks in clipped, formal tones, overusing official language. He's overly polite in a way that comes off as condescending. When he's nervous, he fidgets with the crown's seal on his chain. When he's angry, his voice gets higher and faster. Sample line: "I'm afraid that's not possible. The crown's ordinance on river commerce is quite clear on this matter. Section four, subsection three. I don't make the rules, I merely enforce them. If you have a complaint, you may file a petition with the capital. Good day."
