## Overview

The Riverlands is not a place so much as a passage, a river corridor that connects the cold highlands to the salt marshes, the isolated producers to the coastal markets. It is defined by the Millrun River and the communities that depend on its flow. The land here is narrow, a ribbon of fertile floodplain and riverbank forest squeezed between higher ground to the east and west. The region has no capital, no single culture, no unifying identity beyond the river itself. It is what it connects, not what it contains.

The Millrun flows south for fifty miles from Millrun Falls, where it exits the Stone Heights, to the delta marshes, where it splinters into channels and becomes The Saltfen. Along that stretch, the river carries highland grain, lowland trade goods, news, debts, and occasionally corpses. The people who live here, millers and barge captains, toll collectors and ferry operators, farmers who plow the floodplain soil, are brokers of passage. They do not produce ore or salt. They do not hold political power. They facilitate movement, and that makes them essential and vulnerable in equal measure.

Beneath it all, the Passage Keeper's fragmented corpse defines the river's behavior. The god's skull rests at the highland confluence where the river is born. The ribcage lies at the ford where the river can be crossed. The heart pulses in the delta where the river meets the sea. The fragments are dissolving, slowly, and as they decay, the river is changing. Floods arrive harder. The ford deepens. The pull grows stronger. The Riverlands is a passage built on divine bones, and those bones are turning to silt.

## Geography & Climate

### River System

The Millrun River is the spine of the region, a cold-water river fed by three highland streams and flowing south through fifty miles of gradually descending terrain before reaching the delta.

**Upper Millrun (Millrun Falls to Millford, 20-25 miles):**

The river exits the Stone Heights at Millrun Falls, a series of cascading rapids dropping 1,000 feet over three miles. Below the falls, the river enters the Riverlands proper, wider and slower than the highland torrents but still fast enough to challenge upstream navigation. The riverbanks here are forested, mixed oak and pine, with narrow floodplain fields cleared by farmers who risk spring flooding for fertile soil. The current is strong, the water cold and clear, and barge traffic is one-way only, downstream. Return journeys are overland by cart or on foot.

The river flows past scattered farmsteads, small ferry crossings, and timber camps. No major settlements exist in this stretch. The land is productive but isolated, too far from Millford to sell fresh goods easily, too far from the highlands to benefit from forge trade. The families who farm here are independent and poor, selling grain downriver when the barges run and surviving on their own stores the rest of the year.

**The Ford at Millford (midpoint, sacred site):**

At [[Millford]], the river widens and shallows, spreading across a cobbled bed that allows safe crossing. This is the Passage Keeper's ribcage, divine bones dissolved into the riverbed over seventy-five years, holding the current steady and the crossing passable. The ford is the reason Millford exists, the only reliable crossing point between the highlands and the lowlands. Wagon traffic, trader caravans, and seasonal migrants all pass through Millford, paying tolls to cross and fees to use the docks.

The ford is sacred geography, though few remember why. The cobbles are not mortar-laid, they are fragments of divine bone partially mineralized. The current here is smoother than it should be, the water clearer. Barge captains launching downstream from Millford report a sense of blessing, of safety, that lasts until they reach the delta. The ford is stable now, but it is sinking. The bones are dissolving. In a generation, the crossing may fail entirely.

**Lower Millrun (Millford to Delta, 25-30 miles):**

Below Millford, the river continues south, widening and slowing as the terrain flattens. The banks are lower here, more prone to flooding. Oxbow lakes and abandoned channels scar the floodplain, evidence of the river's wandering course. The forests thin, replaced by marsh willows and reeds as the river approaches the delta. Barge traffic increases. Grain barges from Millford join cargo from smaller upriver landings, all heading toward [[Saltmere]]'s docks.

This stretch is where the pull appears. Near the delta, where the river begins to split into channels, the current becomes unpredictable during autumn low water. Barges are dragged off course, pulled toward the deep channels where the god's heart rests. The phenomenon is worsening. Three barges have been lost in the past year, more than in the previous decade combined.

### Boundaries

The Riverlands is a linear region, defined by river length rather than enclosed territory.

**Northern boundary:** Millrun Falls, where the river descends from the Stone Heights into the lowland corridor. The falls mark both a geographic transition (elevation drop, climate shift) and a political one. Above the falls, highland authority. Below, lowland influence begins.

**Southern boundary:** The delta marshes where the Millrun splits into three channels (Westrun, Centerflow, Saltcurrent) and enters [[The Saltfen]]. The boundary is not a hard line but a gradual transition from river to marsh to tidal bay.

**Eastern and western boundaries:** The settled zone extends only 1-3 miles from the riverbank on each side, limited by rising ground and denser forest. The region is a corridor, not a basin. Beyond the river's immediate floodplain, the land belongs to neighboring (currently undefined) regions.

**Total length:** Approximately 50 miles of navigable river from falls to delta, with a settled corridor 2-6 miles wide.

### Terrain Features

**Floodplain:**
The fertile belt of alluvial soil flanking the river, 0.5-2 miles wide depending on location. The soil is rich, deposited by centuries of spring floods, and supports grain farming, vegetable plots, and orchards. Farmers here accept the flood risk in exchange for high yields. Settlements and permanent structures are built on slight rises or reinforced levees to avoid the worst flooding.

**Oxbow Lakes and Abandoned Channels:**
The Millrun's course has shifted over centuries, leaving crescent-shaped lakes and dry channels scarring the floodplain. Some oxbows are deep enough to support fishing. Others have silted in and become marshland. The presence of so many abandoned channels suggests the river is unstable, prone to jumping its banks and carving new paths. This instability is worsening as the Passage Keeper's bones decay.

**Riverbank Forests:**
Mixed hardwood and pine forests grow along the river's edges, particularly in the upper stretch between the falls and Millford. Timber is harvested for barge construction, bridge pilings, and charcoal. The forests are thinning from overuse, but they regenerate faster than highland forests due to the milder climate and deeper soil.

**Levees and Dikes:**
Around [[Millford]] and the larger farmsteads, artificial levees have been built to protect against spring flooding. These are earthen mounds reinforced with timber pilings and stone. Maintenance is constant. A failed levee means flooded fields, lost grain stores, and ruined homes.

### Trade Corridors

The Millrun River is the region's economic artery, carrying trade between the Stone Heights and coastal markets.

**Barge Routes:**

Downstream traffic (highland to lowland): Grain barges launch from [[Ashcrag]] in the highlands, travel down the Cold Run to Millrun Falls, portage around the falls, relaunch below, and continue downstream to [[Millford]]. At Millford, cargoes are consolidated, grain milled, and barges reloaded for the final run to [[Saltmere]]. The journey from Ashcrag to Saltmere takes 3-5 days depending on water level and delays.

Upstream traffic: There is none. The current is too strong to pole or row against. Return journeys are overland via cart roads paralleling the river. This asymmetry creates economic imbalance. Lowland goods (salt, cloth, tools) must be hauled uphill by wagon at great expense, while highland grain flows downriver cheaply by barge. Lowland merchants exploit this imbalance by lending at interest to highland producers who cannot afford return shipping.

**Seasonal Navigation:**

Spring (March-May): High water from snowmelt. Fast currents, dangerous rapids near the falls, but deep channels allow heavy cargo loads. Flooding can close the ford at Millford for days, bottlenecking trade.

Summer (June-August): Low water. Barge traffic slows. Some stretches become too shallow to navigate. Cargo must be lightened or portaged.

Autumn (September-November): Moderate water from autumn rains, ideal for trade. But this is also when the pull appears in the delta, creating navigation hazards.

Winter (December-February): Ice and freezing rain. The upper Millrun freezes partially. Barge traffic ceases. Overland trade continues but is limited by road conditions.

**Ford Crossing Economics:**

The ford at [[Millford]] is a chokepoint. All wagon traffic between highlands and lowlands must cross here or detour dozens of miles east or west. [[Lyman Corvish]], the Crown-appointed toll collector, charges crossing fees based on cargo weight and wagon axles. The fees generate significant revenue, split between the Crown, the village council, and ford maintenance. Toll evasion is common. Smugglers cross at night or use unmarked upstream fords, undercutting legitimate trade.

### Climate Patterns

The Riverlands enjoys a temperate climate, milder than the harsh Stone Heights and less humid than the coastal Saltfen marshes.

**Temperature:**
Winters are cold but not brutal. Snow falls but does not linger. Rivers freeze partially but rarely solid. Summers are warm but not oppressive. The growing season is 6-7 months, significantly longer than the highlands' 4-month season.

**Flood Cycles:**

Spring floods (March-April): Snowmelt from the Stone Heights sends a surge of cold water downstream. The Millrun rises 3-6 feet above normal, flooding low-lying fields and threatening levees. Historically, spring floods were predictable, arriving in late March and receding by mid-April. In recent years, the floods have arrived earlier, hit harder, and lasted longer. [[Millford]]'s Lower Docks were damaged last spring when the ford overtopped for the first time in living memory.

Autumn rains (September-October): Gentler than spring floods, but still significant. The floodplain absorbs the water, enriching the soil. Barge traffic peaks during autumn as the water level rises enough to float heavy cargo loads.

**Changing Patterns:**

The river's behavior is becoming erratic. Floods are more severe, low water is lower, and the seasonal rhythm is shifting. Older barge captains and farmers notice the changes but attribute them to weather or logging upstream. The truth is divine decay. The Passage Keeper's bones held the river's course and moderated its flow for millennia. As the bones dissolve, the river is returning to its natural, chaotic state.

## Divine Influence

### The Passage Keeper's Remains

The Passage Keeper's fragmented corpse defines the Riverlands' supernatural geography:

**The Skull (Highland Confluence):**
At [[Ashcrag]], where three highland streams converge to form the Millrun, the god's skull dissolved into the streambed. The water here is unnaturally clear, the current smooth. Highlanders do not know the skull is there, but they sense the place is sacred. A small stone shrine sits on the south bank of the confluence, and offerings are left before barge launches and forge blessings.

**The Ribcage and Spine (Millford Ford):**
The divine bones create the cobbled shallows at [[Millford]]. The "stones" are not mortar-laid by mortal hands, they are fragments of divine bone partially mineralized over seventy-five years. The bones hold the current in place, preventing the river from shifting course or deepening. As the bones dissolve, the ford is sinking. The crossing that has been stable for centuries will fail within a generation.

**The Heart (Delta):**
Beneath [[Saltmere]]'s marshes, deep in the silt where the river splits into three channels, the god's heart rests. It is the size of a house, covered in barnacles and river weeds. And it still beats. Once per hour, a slow, grinding pulse that sends ripples through the delta. This is the source of "the pull."

### Supernatural Phenomena

**The Pull:**
The god's heart is trying to reassemble the fragmented body. It pulses, sending divine will through the river, a command to "return, flow back, come together." This manifests as a supernatural downstream current near the delta, appearing unpredictably during autumn low water. Barges caught in the pull are dragged toward the delta's deep channels regardless of how hard the crew rows. Three barges have been lost in the past year. The pull is strengthening.

**Ford Instability:**
The cobbled shallows at [[Millford]] are sinking as the divine bones dissolve. Barge captains who have worked the ford for decades notice the water is deeper now. The ford that has held for seventy-five years is failing. When it collapses, highland-lowland trade will require a bridge (which no faction can afford to build) or a ferry monopoly (which will spark economic war).

**Whispers in the Current:**
Some barge captains report hearing voices in the river at night, especially near the ford or the delta. The voices do not speak words, they sound like water flowing over stones, like grinding silt, like something vast and broken trying to remember its name. These are the god's fragmented consciousness, scattered across the river, trying to reassemble.

### Cultural Legacy

**Ford Blessings:**
Before crossing the ford at [[Millford]], some travelers touch the water and whisper, "Keeper, grant safe passage." It is a rote gesture now, more superstition than prayer, but it descends from the god's teachings. The original prayer was longer, a formal request for the god's blessing and a promise to respect the river. Now it is three words, mumbled quickly, but the intent remains.

**Barge Oaths:**
Serious deals struck on barges or at riverside markets are sworn "by the current." To swear by the current is to invoke the Passage Keeper's authority, saying, "If I lie, may the river refuse me passage." Breaking such an oath is believed to bring divine retribution: capsized barges, failed crossings, cargo lost to flood. [[Dace Torrn]] and other veteran river traders keep their oaths scrupulously.

**Toll Origins:**
The tolls collected at Millford's ford descend from the god's teachings, though their meaning has been corrupted. Originally, travelers paid offerings to the Keeper, not to mortals, symbolic tokens left at shrines on both sides of the crossing. After the god's death, enterprising toll collectors claimed the offerings as revenue. The Crown formalized this as a tax. Now [[Lyman Corvish]] collects crossing fees on behalf of the Crown, and no one remembers the money once belonged to a god.

## River Culture

The Riverlands does not have a unified culture. It is a transitional zone where highland and lowland customs overlap, where traders from a dozen regions pass through but do not stay. What culture exists is defined by the river itself.

**Ford Maintenance Traditions:**

The ford at [[Millford]] requires constant maintenance. Cobbles shift, silt accumulates, and spring floods scour the crossing annually. After each flood season, the village council organizes a communal work detail to clear debris, reset displaced stones, and reinforce the banks. This is not paid labor, it is civic duty. Farmers, millers, and dockworkers work side by side for three days, repairing the crossing that all of them depend on. The work is overseen by [[Halford Quick]], the dock master, who knows the ford's geography better than anyone.

This tradition descends from older practices. Once, the ford was maintained as an act of devotion to the Passage Keeper, a collective offering to the god who made crossing possible. Now it is pragmatic, but the communal nature remains. Millford's ford is not owned by the Crown or the millers or any individual. It belongs to everyone who uses it, and everyone who uses it must maintain it.

**Barge-Building Craft:**

The flat-bottomed barges that carry grain downstream are built at [[Ashcrag]]'s confluence and at smaller timber yards along the Millrun. Barge-building is a specialized craft, passed from master to apprentice. The barges must be light enough to pole through shallow water, sturdy enough to survive spring rapids, and wide enough to carry tons of grain without capsizing. They are built from riverbank timber, oak planks sealed with pine tar, and reinforced with iron fittings from highland forges.

A barge launched from Ashcrag will last 5-10 journeys before the hull weakens and must be broken down for scrap. Barge timber is valuable, reused for dock pilings, bridge repairs, and charcoal. Nothing is wasted. Barge captains treat their vessels as tools, not property. A barge is not named or decorated. It is built, used, and discarded when it fails.

**River Shrines:**

Small shrines dot the riverbank at key crossing points, forgotten relics of the Passage Keeper's worship. Most are simple stone cairns or wooden posts carved with flowing water symbols. Travelers still leave offerings, coins, carved tokens, pieces of bread, not because they believe in the god, but because it is tradition. The shrines are maintained by no one and everyone. A collapsed cairn will be rebuilt by the next traveler who notices. A rotted post will be replaced. The shrines endure through collective neglect and collective care.

**Flood Season Rituals:**

Before the spring floods arrive, farmers who live on the floodplain move valuables to high ground, reinforce levees, and prepare to evacuate if the water rises too high. The night before the first flood surge is expected, families gather on the levees and light bonfires. This is not a religious ceremony, it is a vigil. The fires mark the high-water line from the previous year. If the floods rise higher, the community will see the danger coming. If the water stays below the fires, the season will be safe.

This ritual is practical, but it carries echoes of older devotion. Once, the fires were offerings to the Passage Keeper, prayers for the river to flow gently. Now they are signal fires. But the intent, respect for the river and preparation for its violence, remains the same.

## Settlements & Landmarks

**[[Millford]]** (primary settlement, population ~600):
The only major settlement in the Riverlands, built astride the ford where the Millrun can be crossed. Three water mills process highland grain. Docks handle barge traffic. The village council mediates between highland and lowland interests. Tensions center on water rights, toll collection, and the missing barge mystery.

**[[The Pull Site]]** (landmark, delta hazard zone):
The convergence point near [[Saltmere]] where the three delta channels meet and the Passage Keeper's heart rests. The pull is strongest here. Barges vanish. The missing barge is at the bottom, caught in the supernatural current. Dangerous to approach, impossible to ignore.

**Smaller sites:**
- **Millrun Falls:** Northern boundary, portage point, cascading rapids where highland barges offload and relaunch.
- **Ashcrag Confluence:** Highland headwaters where three streams meet, sacred site (though few know why), the god's skull rests here.
- **Scattered Ferries:** Small ferry crossings upstream and downstream of Millford, serving local farmsteads. Operated by independent ferrymen who charge lower tolls than Millford but offer less reliable service.

## Political Structure

The Riverlands has no unified governance. Political authority is fragmented, contested, and nominal.

**Crown Authority (Toll Collection):**

The Crown claims jurisdiction over the Millrun River as a trade corridor. This authority manifests through toll collection at [[Millford]]'s ford, managed by [[Lyman Corvish]], the Crown-appointed toll collector. Corvish collects crossing fees on behalf of the Crown and remits a portion to the royal treasury. In theory, the Crown uses this revenue to maintain the ford and protect trade. In practice, the Crown has not invested in the Riverlands in decades. The tolls are extraction, not governance.

Corvish is resented but tolerated. He is the only Crown presence for fifty miles. If he were removed, the highland and lowland factions would fight over who controls the crossing. For now, Corvish is neutral, which makes him useful.

**Local Council Governance:**

[[Millford]]'s village council governs day-to-day affairs: ford maintenance, dock fees, dispute mediation, and grain storage regulations. The council is dominated by [[Garrick Thane]] and the millers, but it also includes [[Halford Quick]] (dock master) and two elected farmers. The council's authority extends only to Millford itself. Upstream and downstream communities are ungoverned except by tradition and mutual agreement.

**No Regional Authority:**

There is no lord, mayor, or governor for the Riverlands as a whole. The region is a corridor, not a polity. Highland factions ([[Forge Council]], [[Highland Merchant Cartel]]) exert influence through trade agreements and debt. Lowland factions ([[Saltmere Merchant Assembly]]) control the downstream markets and dictate terms. The Riverlands is caught between them, a hinge with no power of its own.

## Factions

No factions are based in the Riverlands, but several operate through it:

**[[Highland Merchant Cartel]]** (operates from [[Ironhollow]], controls ore and grain shipments):
The cartel uses the Millrun to ship ore and grain downstream. They negotiate barge contracts with [[Millford]] captains and pay tolls under protest. The cartel resents Crown toll collection and has lobbied for reduced rates, unsuccessfully.

**[[Saltmere Merchant Assembly]]** (operates from [[Saltmere]], controls lowland markets):
The Assembly controls the downstream markets where highland grain is sold. They set prices, dictate terms, and lend at interest to highland producers. The Assembly is covering up the missing barge incident to prevent economic panic.

**[[Forge Council]]** (operates from [[Ashcrag]], governs highland smiths):
The council ships tools and processed grain downstream via the Millrun. They depend on the river for market access but have no direct control over it. Ford disruptions hurt highland smiths by cutting off lowland buyers.

**Independent Traders and Barge Captains:**

The largest "faction" is no faction at all: independent traders like [[Dace Torrn]] and [[Lira Fenn]], barge captains who run the river for profit and answer to no guild or council. They negotiate contracts, avoid tolls when possible, and carry news between highlands and lowlands. They are pragmatic, opportunistic, and essential. Without them, the trade corridor collapses.

## Cultural Identity

The Riverlands is liminal space, defined by what it connects rather than what it contains.

**Transitional Identity:**

People here are "river folk," a term that means little beyond "not highland, not lowland." They speak both highland and lowland dialects. They trade with both regions. They have no distinct crafts, no unifying religion, no shared history beyond the river. They are brokers, intermediaries, and facilitators. They survive by enabling passage, not by producing anything themselves.

This creates resentment. Highlanders see river folk as parasites who extract tolls and miller's fees without doing real work. Lowlanders see them as backward provincials who cannot compete in coastal markets. River folk see both groups as arrogant. Highlanders do not understand commerce. Lowlanders do not understand labor. River folk understand both because they live between them.

**Pragmatic, Not Principled:**

The Passage Keeper's philosophy, yield but do not break, flow around obstacles, still shapes river culture even though the god is dead. River folk are adaptable. They negotiate, compromise, and bend to pressure rather than resist. This is not cowardice, it is survival. The highlands and lowlands have armies, councils, and resources. The Riverlands has a river. When highland factions demand lower toll rates, Millford's council negotiates. When lowland merchants demand grain quotas, barge captains comply. Resistance would be crushed. Adaptation keeps the trade flowing.

But this pragmatism has limits. Push too hard, demand too much, and river folk will simply stop facilitating. The ford can be blockaded. Barges can refuse to launch. The river can be closed, and then both highlands and lowlands starve.

**Forgotten Divinity:**

River folk do not worship the Passage Keeper, but they remember the god's teachings in degraded form. Ford blessings, barge oaths, and toll traditions all descend from divine worship. The words have changed, the meaning has faded, but the rituals persist. River folk respect the river as a living force, something with will and mood and temper. This respect is practical, not theological. The river can kill you if you disrespect it. Whether that is divine punishment or natural consequence, the result is the same.

## History

The Riverlands has no grand history, only a long accumulation of small histories.

**Before the Theomachis (unknown antiquity to 75 years ago):**

The Passage Keeper was worshipped along the Millrun for centuries, perhaps millennia. Shrines dotted the riverbank. Ferrymen and barge captains prayed for safe passage. Toll offerings were left at crossings. The river was sacred geography, and the god's presence made it safe. Floods were predictable. Crossings were stable. The pull did not exist.

**The Theomachis and Fragmentation (75 years ago):**

When the gods went to war, the Passage Keeper fell. The god's body shattered, fragments scattering along the river. The skull landed at the confluence. The ribcage created the ford. The heart sank into the delta. In the immediate aftermath, the river's behavior did not change. The divine bones held. Worship faded, but the geography endured.

**The Silent Generations (75 years ago to 20 years ago):**

For two generations after the Theomachis, the Riverlands functioned normally. The ford was stable. Barge trade grew. [[Millford]] expanded. The Crown formalized toll collection. Highland and lowland economies integrated. No one noticed the bones were dissolving because the decay was too slow to measure in a single lifetime.

**Recent Destabilization (last 20 years):**

The river's behavior is changing. Spring floods are more severe. The ford is deepening. The pull is appearing in the delta. Older barge captains notice but do not understand. Younger generations assume the river has always been this way. The truth, that the divine bones are failing, is unknown. The Riverlands is built on a foundation that is dissolving, and no one realizes it yet.

## Connections to Other Regions

**[[The Stone Heights]] (north, connected via Millrun River):**

The highlands produce grain, ore, and tools. The Millrun is their only cheap trade route to lowland markets. If the river fails, the highlands are economically isolated. Highland factions ([[Forge Council]], [[Highland Merchant Cartel]]) depend on the Riverlands but do not control it, creating resentment and vulnerability.

**[[The Saltfen]] (south, connected via delta):**

[[Saltmere]] depends on highland grain flowing downstream. If the river fails, the city starves or must import grain from distant coastal markets at great expense. The [[Saltmere Merchant Assembly]] understands this dependency and is quietly terrified of the pull's escalation.

**Eastern and Western Neighbors (currently undefined):**

The Riverlands is a corridor connecting north and south, but it is bordered by other regions to the east and west. These regions are currently undefined, but they are likely:
- Less dependent on the Millrun (they have their own trade routes)
- Culturally distinct from both highlands and riverlands
- Potential competitors or allies if the river fails and alternative trade routes are needed

## Campaign Role

The Riverlands serves as:

**Geographic Connector:**
The region links [[The Stone Heights]] and [[The Saltfen]], providing a natural travel corridor for campaigns that span multiple regions. PCs moving between highlands and lowlands will pass through [[Millford]] and encounter river culture.

**Economic Chokepoint:**
The ford at Millford is a bottleneck. Control the ford, control highland-lowland trade. Factions will fight over this. PCs can be hired to protect the crossing, blockade it, or investigate threats to it.

**Divine Decay Introduction:**
The missing barge investigation and the pull site are the campaign's introduction to divine decay as a physical, escalating threat. PCs will discover that the god's remains are failing and that mortal economies are built on divine bones.

**Moral Complexity:**
The Riverlands offers no clean answers. Should the ford be repaired or replaced? Should the pull be stopped or managed? Who owns the god's remains? Who has the right to "fix" the river? PCs will face choices with no good outcomes, only trade-offs.

**Tonal Foundation:**
The Riverlands establishes the world's emerging tone: grounded supernatural horror in economic collapse. Beautiful and wrong. Labor against entropy. Things are getting worse, and no one can stop it, only survive it.
