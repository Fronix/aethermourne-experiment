## Overview

The Stone Heights is a highland region defined by cold, stone, and the petrified corpse of a dead god. The land is hard here. Thin soil barely covers the exposed bedrock. Winters stretch for five months, cutting the region off from lowland markets with snow and frozen roads. The growing season is short, four months at most, and farmers plant cold-hardy grain that barely ripens before the first frost. The forests are thinning, logged out to feed the charcoal kilns that keep the forges burning. The iron ore, once rich and plentiful, is running out. And beneath it all, the Stone Warden's remains radiate a supernatural cold that makes the land harsher than geography alone can explain.

This is a region shaped by endurance. The people who live here, farmers and smiths and miners, follow the old god's teachings whether they know it or not: do not yield, outlast the hardship, take pride in your labor. They distrust lowlanders, who do not understand what it means to survive a winter when the southern road is impassable for three months and your grain stores must last until spring thaw. They resent the Crown, which abandoned the highlands after the Theomachis and now ignores them unless lowland creditors demand intervention. They value independence, self-sufficiency, and the dignity of work done with your own hands.

But independence is fragile. The highlands depend on the lowlands for salt, cloth, and capital. Lowland creditors like [[Corwin Marsh]] control highland debt, and when debts come due, highland autonomy crumbles. The [[Highland Merchant Cartel]] seized [[Ironhollow]] ten years ago, turning independent miners into contract laborers trapped in company town squalor. The [[Forge Council]] governs [[Ashcrag]] with guild authority, but farmers and laborers are organizing against it, demanding representation and threatening a strike that could collapse the highland economy. And beneath the political tensions, the Stone Warden's divine decay is accelerating. Winters are getting longer. Ore veins are behaving strangely. Stone-cursed creatures are appearing in the deep forests. Something is wrong with the god's corpse, and it is getting worse.

The Stone Heights is a place where the old world is dying and the new world has not yet arrived. It is a place of harsh beauty, stubborn people, and divine corruption that seeps up from the bones of the earth.

## Geography & Climate

### Boundaries

The Stone Heights encompasses the highland watershed that drains into the Millrun River, a roughly oval-shaped region measuring 16-20 miles north to south and 12-18 miles east to west.

**Northern boundary:** The exposed rocky peaks north of [[Ironhollow]], jagged ridgelines of barren stone that rise 3,000-4,000 feet above sea level. These peaks are the Stone Warden's spine, vertebrae turned to stone and thrust upward by the impact of the god's fall. No trees grow here. Snow lingers into late spring. The peaks mark the headwaters of the three streams that feed the Millrun River.

**Southern boundary:** Millrun Falls, a series of cascading rapids where the Millrun River exits the highlands and descends into the lowland river valley. This is both a geographic transition (elevation drop from 1,500 feet to 500 feet over three miles) and a cultural boundary. Below the falls, the land flattens, the climate softens, and the Crown's authority begins to matter. Above the falls, the highlands remain independent.

**Eastern and western boundaries:** Watershed divides along forested ridgelines, roughly 5-8 miles east and 6-10 miles west of [[Ashcrag]]. Streams beyond these ridges drain into other regions, currently undefined. The ridgelines are not as dramatic as the northern peaks, but they are steep enough and forested enough to discourage travel. The Stone Heights is a natural basin, isolated by terrain.

**Total area:** Approximately 200-300 square miles of highland watershed. Most of this is forested hills, rocky slopes, and thin-soiled valleys. Arable land is concentrated in the Ashcrag valley and a few outlying farm valleys along the tributaries.

### River System

Three cold-water streams converge at [[Ashcrag]] to form the Millrun River:

**Cold Run** (northern stream):
- Source: Snowmelt and springs in the northern peaks
- Course: Flows south through [[Ironhollow]] valley, passes [[Halfway Burn Site]], enters Ashcrag valley from the north
- Length: ~12-14 miles from source to confluence
- Character: Fast, rocky, cold year-round. Freezes solid November-March. Not navigable except by foot traffic on ice in winter.
- Named features: Iron Bend (barge landing at Ironhollow)

**Ashwater** (western stream):
- Source: Highland springs and runoff from western ridgeline escarpment
- Course: Flows east through forested valleys, enters Ashcrag valley from the west
- Length: ~8-10 miles from source to confluence
- Character: Moderate flow, clear water. Partially freezes in winter. Flows through dense pine forest (Ashwood).
- Economic role: Water source for timber operations, seasonal charcoal transport

**Piney Flow** (eastern stream):
- Source: Highland springs in forested ridges east of Ashcrag
- Course: Flows west through agricultural valleys (Harth Estate and tenant farms), enters Ashcrag valley from the east
- Length: ~6-8 miles from source to confluence
- Character: Slowest and widest of the three streams, meandering through fertile valley soil. Rarely freezes completely.
- Economic role: Irrigation for grain farming, water source for outlying farmsteads

**The Confluence:** The point where the three streams meet at [[Ashcrag]], reinforced with stone pilings and wooden platforms. This is the barge-building yard where highland grain, tools, and ore are loaded onto flat-bottomed barges for the downstream journey to [[Millford]] and beyond. A small stone shrine sits on the south bank, dedicated to the Stone Warden. Offerings are left here before barge launches and forge blessings.

**Millrun River (downstream from Ashcrag):**
- Formation: Three streams become one river at The Confluence
- Course: Flows south 4-6 miles through narrowing valley, exits highlands at Millrun Falls, continues 15-20 miles to [[Millford]]
- Navigation: Seasonal. Spring (March-May) and autumn (September-October) are barge seasons when snowmelt or autumn rains raise water level enough for downstream traffic. Summer flow is too low. Winter is frozen. Barges cannot travel upstream; return journey is overland.
- Economic role: The Millrun River is the economic lifeline of the Stone Heights. Grain, tools, and ore flow downstream to lowland markets. Salt, cloth, and manufactured goods flow back upstream via overland cart during trade seasons.

### Terrain Features

**Ashcrag Valley** (primary settlement valley):
- Wide, fertile valley at the confluence of the three streams
- Gentle slopes, bedrock showing through thin highland soil
- Valley width: 2-3 miles at widest point
- Elevation: 1,500-2,000 feet
- Land use: Farmland along Piney Flow, timber operations along Ashwater, forge quarter and market ground on slopes above The Confluence
- Notable features: Stone Hearth (council meeting hall built into valley wall), Harth Estate (largest farm), timber yards (south of confluence)

**Ironhollow Valley** (northern mining valley):
- Narrow, rocky valley carved by Cold Run
- Steep slopes, thin soil, exposed mineral veins in hillsides
- Valley width: Less than 1 mile at settlement, narrowing to gorge upstream
- Elevation: 2,200-2,800 feet
- Land use: Mining pits scattered across slopes within half-day's walk of settlement
- Notable features: Iron Ridge (escarpment with richest ore veins), ore yard (central staging area), slag heaps visible for miles

**Outlying farm valleys:**
- Small tributary valleys along Piney Flow and Ashwater
- Fertile pockets where tenant farms grow grain, raise livestock
- Isolated during winter, connected to Ashcrag market during trade seasons
- Population: 5-20 per valley (extended family farmsteads)

**Forests:**
- **Piney Reach** (eastern forest): Dense pine and birch along Piney Flow watershed. Primary source of current timber operations. Logged selectively for 40+ years, showing signs of depletion near settlement. Deeper forest (5+ miles from Ashcrag) still has old-growth stands.
- **Ashwood** (western forest): Mixed pine, birch, and ash along Ashwater watershed. Primary source of charcoal for forges. Heavily logged, many areas reduced to stumps and saplings. [[Halfway Burn Site]] is an abandoned charcoal operation within this depleted zone.
- **Ironhollow Scrubland** (northern deforested zone): Once forested, now reduced to thin saplings, scrub, and exposed rock. Logged out 15+ years ago to fuel Ironhollow smelting operations. Soil erosion, poor regrowth, ecological collapse. Locals call it "The Stumps."

**Rocky features:**
- **Northern Peaks** (The Warden's Spine): Exposed rocky peaks, 3,000-4,000 feet elevation, barren and windswept. The Stone Warden's vertebrae turned to stone. Avoided by miners (cursed stone, tools break, sounds of grinding bones at night).
- **Western Ridgeline**: Rocky escarpment, 2,500-3,000 feet elevation, forested on eastern side, barren on western side (rain shadow). Watershed divide.
- **Eastern Ridgeline**: Forested ridgeline, 2,000-2,500 feet elevation, gentler slopes than western ridgeline. Watershed divide.
- **Highland Bedrock** (The Warden's Flesh): Thin soil sits atop petrified divine remains. Farmers strike bedrock after plowing 6-12 inches deep. The god's body is just beneath the surface, preventing deep-rooted crops or forests.

### Climate Patterns

**Winter (December-February):** The defining season. Temperature well below freezing (0-20°F) for weeks at a time. Heavy snowfall, 3-6 feet accumulated in valleys, deeper in mountains. Southern road to [[Millford]] impassable for 3-5 months depending on severity. River frozen solid at Ashcrag. No barge trade, no overland trade, settlements isolated. Cultural impact: Stockpiling season, survival mode, community cohesion tested. The harsh winter 20 years ago (5-month road closure, starvation) is a formative cultural memory that shaped the [[Forge Council]]'s centralization.

**Spring (March-May):** Snowmelt, thaw, flooding risk along river valleys. Temperature 30-50°F, highly variable. Road conditions muddy until late April. River at peak flow, primary barge season begins. Frantic economic activity: barges loaded and shipped, contracts negotiated, debts settled. Planting begins late April/early May. Cultural mood: Relief, renewal, optimism after winter isolation.

**Summer (June-August):** Short, intense growing season. Temperature 60-80°F, warm days, cool nights. Moderate rainfall, afternoon thunderstorms common. River flow drops, limited barge traffic. Steady forge work, timber cutting, ore extraction, grain tending. Grain grows rapidly, harvest begins late August. Long work days, preparation for autumn trade and winter stockpiling.

**Autumn (September-November):** Harvest, secondary trade season, preparation for winter. Temperature 40-60°F, first frosts in October. Autumn rains raise river level (September-October barge season, smaller than spring). Grain harvest, final tool shipments, charcoal stockpiling for winter forge work. Cultural mood: Anxiety, urgency, stockpiling, watching weather for signs of early freeze. First snow usually November, sometimes late October (early freeze is disaster).

**Weather hazards:**
- Summer thunderstorms: Intense, sudden, dangerous in exposed terrain. Flash flooding in narrow valleys.
- Autumn windstorms: Cold winds from the north, early snow, freezing rain. Can close roads weeks earlier than expected.
- Winter blizzards: Multi-day snowfall, whiteout conditions, deadly to travelers.

**Supernatural climate:**
The winters are harsher than geography alone can explain. The Stone Warden's divine essence, the god's will to endure and never yield, persists in the stone and manifests as cold. Blizzards appear without warning. Frost comes early and lingers late. Highlanders who camp on bare stone in winter report feeling the cold seep upward through their bedrolls, as if the earth itself is trying to freeze them. This is not natural cold, it is divine, radiating from the god's remains like inverted heat.

## Divine Influence

### The Stone Warden's Remains

Seventy-five years ago, during the Theomachis, the god of stone, mountains, metalworking, and endurance fell in the highlands. The Stone Warden's body crashed into the ridgeline with such force that the impact reshaped the terrain. Mountains buckled. Valleys split. Rivers changed course. And when the dust settled, the Warden's corpse had turned to stone.

The god's petrified remains define the geography of the Stone Heights:

**The Spine (Northern Peaks):** The exposed rocky peaks north of [[Ironhollow]] are the Warden's vertebrae, thrust upward by the god's fall. These peaks are barren, windswept, and supernaturally cold. Miners avoid the highest peaks, claiming the stone is cursed, that tools break against it, and that those who camp overnight hear grinding sounds like bones shifting beneath the earth.

**The Flesh (Highland Bedrock):** The thin soil of the highland valleys sits atop the Warden's petrified flesh. Farmers know that plowing deeper than a foot risks striking bedrock. The god's body is just beneath the surface, too close to allow deep-rooted crops or forests. This is why the highlands produce short-season grain and thin timber, why the soil is poor compared to the lowlands.

**The Blood (Iron Ore Veins):** The iron ore extracted from [[Ironhollow]]'s mines is the Warden's mineralized blood, or perhaps bone marrow. The ore is rich but finite. Surface veins that were easy to extract forty years ago are now nearly exhausted. Miners must dig deeper, and the deeper they go, the stranger the ore becomes. Some veins pulse faintly when struck. Some bleed rust-red water. Some resist tools as if the metal itself does not want to be taken.

**The Will (Supernatural Cold):** The Warden's divine essence, the god's will to endure and never yield, persists in the stone. It manifests as cold. Winters in the Stone Heights are harsher than they should be for the latitude. The cold is not natural, it is divine, radiating from the god's remains.

### Supernatural Ecology

The Warden's death was not clean. Divine decay is slow, and seventy-five years is only a moment in the lifespan of a god's corpse. The remains are still dying, still leaking divine essence into the land, and this creates supernatural phenomena:

**Stone-Cursed Creatures:** Wildlife exposed to the god's remains over generations develops petrifying mutations. Deer with stone-encrusted antlers that cannot shed them. Wolves with stone hides resistant to blades. Boars with tusks that grow inward, piercing their own skulls, turning them mad with pain. These creatures are rare, found only in the deep forests near the northern peaks or in abandoned mine shafts where divine decay concentrates. Highlanders call them "the Warden's children" and consider them omens of bad luck.

**Ore Madness:** Miners who work deep in the [[Ironhollow]] escarpment, cutting ore from veins close to the god's bones, sometimes develop obsessive behaviors. They hear whispers in the rock, voices that sound like grinding stone. They become convinced that one more strike, one more foot deeper, will reveal a vein of pure divine ore that will make them rich. They neglect food, sleep, and safety, chasing the whispers until they collapse from exhaustion or die in cave-ins. The [[Highland Merchant Cartel]] calls it exhaustion psychosis. Miners call it the Warden's Curse.

**Cold Sickness:** In certain places, pockets where the god's coldness concentrates, the supernatural chill is deadly. Travelers who camp in these zones develop frostbite in minutes, even in summer. The cold bypasses clothing, sinks into bones, and refuses to leave. Victims report feeling as if their blood is turning to ice, their joints stiffening like stone. If not moved to warmth immediately, they die, and their bodies are found frozen solid. Highlanders mark these zones with cairns and avoid them.

**Living Ore Veins:** Some of the deepest ore veins in [[Ironhollow]] are not inert metal. They shift when miners approach, retreating into the rock face. They bleed rust-red water when struck, as if the stone itself feels pain. Miners have reported veins that "close" after being mined, the ore healing like wounded flesh. Experienced miners know which veins to avoid. Cutting into a living vein brings cave-ins, equipment failure, and bad luck that follows the crew for months.

### Cultural Legacy

The Stone Warden is dead, but the god's teachings and worship survive in highland culture:

**Stone Shrine at The Confluence:** At the point where the three highland streams converge in [[Ashcrag]], a small stone shrine sits on the south bank. It is older than the town, built by the first settlers who recognized the Warden's presence in the bedrock. Offerings are left here before barge launches, before forge blessings, and before dangerous mine work. The offerings are simple: a nail, a piece of ore, a stone taken from the god's peaks and returned with thanks. The shrine is maintained by no one and everyone. To damage it would be unthinkable.

**Forge Blessing Rituals:** Guild masters in [[Ashcrag]] invoke the Warden's name when lighting a new forge or beginning a major commission. The ritual is brief: a piece of iron ore is heated until it glows, then quenched in water while the smith recites, "Stone yields to fire, fire yields to water, water yields to stone. We forge in your name, Warden, and honor the metal." The quenched ore is kept in the forge as a charm against bad luck. [[Myra Cadros]] and Hal Brennan both maintain this tradition, though they argue about the proper wording.

**Miner Prayers:** Before entering the [[Ironhollow]] mines, some miners touch the rock face and whisper, "Warden, grant passage. We take only what we need." It is not a prayer for safety, it is a request for permission, an acknowledgment that the ore belongs to the god and miners are borrowing it. Miners who skip this ritual are considered reckless. When a cave-in kills someone, other miners will quietly ask, "Did they pray?"

**Stone Oaths:** Serious vows in the Stone Heights are sworn "on stone" or "on the Warden's bones." To swear on stone is to invoke the god's unyielding nature, to say, "I will not break this promise, as stone does not break." Breaking a stone oath is believed to bring divine curse: bad luck, failed ventures, early death. [[Orrin Pike]] has sworn his coalition oath on stone. [[Aldric Harth]] swore his council oath on stone. Both men are now trapped by their promises, unable to compromise without invoking the Warden's wrath.

## Settlements & Landmarks

**[[Ashcrag]]** - Guild town, population ~350, seat of the [[Forge Council]]. Built at the confluence of the three streams where highland grain, tools, and ore are loaded onto barges for lowland markets. The town smells of charcoal smoke and forge heat. Two master forges (Cadros Ironworks and Brennan's Forge) dominate the economy. Farmers resent the council's control over grain prices and barge priority. The Stone Hearth, a two-story timber tavern, serves as the council's meeting hall. The Confluence barge yard and stone shrine are the town's economic and spiritual center.

**[[Ironhollow]]** - Company town, population ~175 permanent + 50-75 seasonal workers, controlled by the [[Highland Merchant Cartel]]. A collection of rough timber buildings clustered around an ore yard where iron ore is weighed, recorded, and loaded for transport to [[Ashcrag]]. The air smells of ore dust and sweat. Miners work under contract, bound by debt they can never quite clear. The cartel owns everything: mines, company store, boarding houses, ore itself. There is no council, no guild, no voice for the workers. [[Senna Kade]], contract miner and labor organizer, is secretly coordinating with [[Orrin Pike]] to build a cross-settlement strike that could reshape highland power dynamics.

**[[Halfway Burn Site]]** - Abandoned charcoal operation between Ironhollow and Ashcrag, 4 miles from each settlement along Cold Run. Stone kiln foundation, collapsed timber shed, forest depleted and overgrown. Abandoned 15 years ago when the forest was logged out. Now used as neutral meeting ground for the [[Highland Workers Coalition]]. Far enough from both settlements that cartel patrols and council eyes do not reach. Isolated enough that no one questions why someone might be walking this stretch of Cold Run.

**The Confluence** - Barge-building yard at [[Ashcrag]] where the three streams meet and become the Millrun River. Stone pilings, wooden platforms, chaotic activity during trade seasons. The stone shrine on the south bank is a pilgrimage site, offerings left before dangerous work or major ventures.

**Millrun Falls** - Cascading rapids where the Millrun River exits the highlands and descends into the lowland river valley. This is the geographic and cultural boundary between highlands and lowlands. Barges must navigate carefully in spring when flow is fast. Some barge traffic ends here, goods transferred overland around the falls.

**Iron Ridge** - The escarpment above [[Ironhollow]] where the richest ore veins are exposed. Mineral-rich rock face, dangerous terrain, finite resource. [[Jorin Velk]] works an independent claim here, bypassing the cartel. [[Caden Wroth]], the cartel's Mining Factor, is looking for an excuse to seize it.

**The Stumps** - Local name for the deforested Ironhollow scrubland north of the settlement. Once dense forest, now barren hills dotted with slag heaps and mine tailings. Visible for miles, a monument to resource depletion and ecological collapse.

## Political Structure

### Forge Council Jurisdiction

The [[Forge Council]] is the ruling body of [[Ashcrag]], a guild-dominated governance structure that controls the highland economy through market fees, barge loading priorities, and timber licensing. The council governs Ashcrag and claims authority over "all settlements and holdings within the Ashcrag valley watershed."

In practice, this means:
- Ashcrag town and surrounding farms (direct governance)
- Outlying farm valleys along Piney Flow and Ashwater tributaries (taxation, market access)
- Timber operations within Ashwood and Piney Reach forests (licensing, fee collection)

The council does NOT govern [[Ironhollow]], which lies beyond the ridgeline in the Cold Run watershed. When the [[Highland Merchant Cartel]] seized Ironhollow ten years ago, the council protested but lacked the leverage to intervene. The watershed serves as a de facto political boundary: control the water, control the economy.

The council operates independently of Crown authority. Once per year, a representative travels to the regional Crown seat to renew fealty in a ceremonial ritual. The Crown does not send tax collectors, officials, soldiers, or judges to the highlands. This de facto independence emerged after the Theomachis, when the Crown's reach collapsed and highland regions were too remote and poor to be worth administering.

### Highland Merchant Cartel Control

The [[Highland Merchant Cartel]] controls [[Ironhollow]] as a company town. Ten years ago, the cartel used debt leverage to seize the settlement from independent miners and impose a monopoly on iron ore extraction. Miners work under contract, bound by debt to the company store. The cartel's Mining Factor, [[Caden Wroth]], enforces quotas, collects debts, and ensures the ore flows to [[Ashcrag]] forges.

The cartel's economic stranglehold extends beyond Ironhollow:
- Monopoly on iron ore supply (if they cut off ore, Ashcrag forges go dark)
- Control of ore prices (cartel sets rates, council must accept or lose tool production)
- Debt leverage over highland traders (barge financing, equipment loans)

[[Myra Cadros]] and the [[Forge Council]] want to wrest control of Ironhollow from the cartel, but they lack the leverage. The cartel is prepared to use violence to maintain their monopoly.

### Crown Relationship

The Stone Heights governs itself independently of Crown authority, a situation born of neglect rather than rebellion. After the Theomachis, the Crown focused on rebuilding core territories (lowlands, coastal cities) and abandoned marginal regions like the highlands. Over seventy-five years, neglect became independence.

The arrangement is fragile. If highland debt defaults threaten lowland merchant stability, the Crown might reassert authority to protect Assembly interests. [[Myra Cadros]] fears this scenario: lowland creditors using Crown legitimacy to annex highland resources. She frames the council's independence as "highland self-governance resisting Crown interference," but she knows that independence is contingent on the Crown continuing to ignore the highlands.

## Factions

**[[Forge Council]]** - Guild-dominated governance structure ruling [[Ashcrag]]. Five-member body: two master smiths (permanent seats), three landowner representatives (elected, three-year staggered terms). Controls market fees (10% of all transactions), barge loading priority, grain price influence, timber licensing, and emergency grain reserve. Led by [[Myra Cadros]], master smith and council head. Opposes the [[Highland Workers Coalition]] but has not cracked down yet, waiting for the coalition to collapse on its own.

**[[Highland Workers Coalition]]** - Nascent labor organizing network coordinating between tenant farmers in [[Ashcrag]] and contract miners in [[Ironhollow]]. Demands council representation for farmers, improved safety and reduced debt terms for miners. Led by [[Orrin Pike]] (Ashcrag, charcoal producer and farmer organizer) and [[Senna Kade]] (Ironhollow, contract miner). Planning a coordinated strike to force both the [[Forge Council]] and [[Highland Merchant Cartel]] to negotiate. If the strike succeeds, it reshapes highland power dynamics. If it fails, organizers will be blacklisted, imprisoned, or worse.

**[[Highland Merchant Cartel]]** - Lowland-backed economic consortium controlling [[Ironhollow]] as a company town. Monopolizes iron ore extraction, operates company store and boarding houses, enforces debt contracts. Represented by [[Caden Wroth]] (Mining Factor) and [[Tallow Breck]] (cartel factor and river trader). Economic rivalry with [[Forge Council]] over tool market and ore pricing. Prepared to use violence to maintain monopoly.

**Saltmere Merchant Assembly** (external faction, lowland creditors) - Merchant coalition based in [[Saltmere]], controls lowland river trade and finances highland barge operations. [[Corwin Marsh]], merchant house head, holds significant debt from [[Aldric Harth]] (Ashcrag grain lord). Debt due in two months. If Marsh calls the debt and seizes the Harth Estate, lowland creditors control highland grain exports, threatening highland independence. The Assembly sees the highlands as a resource extraction zone, not a partner.

## Cultural Identity

### Highland vs Lowland

The divide between "highland" and "lowland" is economic, political, and cultural:

**Economic:** Highlands produce (grain, tools, iron ore). Lowlands finance (capital, loans, barge contracts, imported goods). Highland economy depends on lowland markets, but lowland creditors control the terms. This creates resentment: highlanders do the hard work, lowlanders profit.

**Political:** Highlands are independent of Crown authority. Lowlands are Crown-governed. Highlanders see themselves as free, self-governing communities. Lowlanders see highlanders as provincial, backwards, and economically dependent.

**Cultural:** Highlanders endure harsh winters, work with their hands, value self-sufficiency. Lowlanders are "soft," do not understand hardship, profit from others' labor. To be called a lowlander in the highlands is an insult. To be called a highlander in the lowlands implies stubbornness and provincialism.

The boundary is not just elevation, it is the point where the river becomes navigable (Millrun Falls), winters become shorter, and Crown authority begins to matter.

### Guild Culture

The Stone Heights has a status hierarchy rooted in guild traditions descended from the Stone Warden's teachings:

**Master smiths** (highest status): [[Myra Cadros]], Hal Brennan. Control forges, employ journeymen and apprentices, sit on [[Forge Council]]. Forge work is sacred labor, echo of the Warden's domain. Mastery is earned through skill, endurance, and guild approval.

**Landowners** (high status): [[Aldric Harth]], tenant farm operators. Own productive land, sell grain through Ashcrag market. Sit on council as farmer representatives, though tenant farmers see them as too close to guild interests.

**Journeymen and independent artisans** (middle status): Skilled workers who have completed apprenticeships but do not yet own forges or land. Aspire to mastery.

**Tenant farmers and timber workers** (low status): Rent land from estate owners, work charcoal kilns, sell labor. Resent guild control over market access and prices. [[Orrin Pike]] and the [[Highland Workers Coalition]] represent this group.

**Company miners** (lowest status): Contract laborers in [[Ironhollow]], bound by debt to [[Highland Merchant Cartel]]. No land, no guild, no voice. Despised by guild workers as cautionary examples of what happens when you lose independence.

Guild traditions include apprenticeship (seven years learning the trade), mastery trials (forge a masterwork to earn guild approval), and forge blessing rituals invoking the Stone Warden's name.

### Seasonal Rhythms

Highland life is dictated by the seasons:

**Winter (survival mode):** Settlements isolated by frozen roads and impassable snow. Stockpiles must last 3-5 months. Forge work slows to conserve charcoal. Families huddle by hearths. Community cohesion tested. No trade, no travel, no income. Debt accumulates. The harsh winter 20 years ago (5-month road closure, starvation, Forge Council restructuring) is a cultural touchstone.

**Spring (frantic trade):** Snowmelt, thaw, relief. Roads open, river rises, barge season begins. Grain loaded and shipped. Tools sold. Ore transported. Contracts negotiated, debts settled (or extended). Planting begins. Optimism after winter isolation.

**Summer (steady work):** Long days. Grain grows. Timber cut. Ore extracted. Forge work steady. Preparation for autumn harvest and winter stockpiling.

**Autumn (anxious urgency):** Harvest, secondary barge season, final shipments before freeze. Watching weather for signs of early snow. Charcoal stockpiling. Anxiety about whether stockpiles will be enough.

Failure to prepare for winter is deadly. Stockpiling is survival. Self-sufficiency is prized because you cannot rely on imports for 3-5 months.

## History

**Pre-Theomachis (unknown to 75 years ago):** Early highland settlement under Crown authority. Small farming and mining communities. The Stone Warden worshipped as patron of miners, smiths, and mountain folk. Trade with lowlands limited by terrain and seasonal isolation.

**Theomachis (75 years ago, Year -75):** The gods went to war. The Stone Warden fell in the highlands, body crashing into the ridgeline. Divine impact reshaped terrain: mountains buckled, valleys split, rivers changed course. The Warden's corpse petrified, becoming the bedrock and peaks. Crown authority collapsed. Highland regions abandoned by central governance.

**Post-Theomachis Recovery (75-50 years ago):** Highlanders survived on their own, following the Warden's teachings: endure, do not yield, outlast the chaos. First settlers built [[Ashcrag]] at the confluence, recognizing the strategic value of the barge-loading site. Forges established atop the god's remains. Iron ore mining began in what would become [[Ironhollow]]. Guild culture developed from Warden's domain teachings. Highland independence emerged from Crown neglect.

**Pre-Council Era (50-22 years ago):** Informal governance. Extended families controlled farms and timber operations. Harth estate established. Ironhollow mining expanded under independent prospectors. Lowland creditors began financing highland barge operations, creating debt dependency. Crown continued to ignore highlands, focusing on core territories.

**Council Formation (22 years ago):** Guild masters and landowners formed defensive alliance against lowland creditor expansion. The [[Forge Council]] created as formal governance structure to coordinate grain sales, barge contracts, and resist debt foreclosures. Early council weak, decentralized, representing guild and landowner interests equally.

**Harsh Winter Crisis (20 years ago):** Catastrophic winter, 5-month road closure. Ashcrag ran out of imported salt, grain stores spoiled, families starved. Weak council could not coordinate response. [[Myra Cadros]] took control, organized rationing, negotiated emergency barge runs when ice broke. Restructured council to centralize authority under guild dominance. Emergency grain reserve established. Harsh winter is formative cultural memory.

**Cartel Displacement (10 years ago):** [[Highland Merchant Cartel]] used debt leverage to seize [[Ironhollow]] from independent miners. Company town imposed. Miners became contract laborers. [[Forge Council]] protested but lacked leverage. Ironhollow became monopoly ore supplier, giving cartel economic stranglehold over highland forges.

**Coalition Emergence (2 years ago):** [[Orrin Pike]] (Ashcrag tenant farmers) and [[Senna Kade]] (Ironhollow contract miners) began coordinating, recognizing shared interests against guild and cartel control. [[Highland Workers Coalition]] formed. Organizing quietly, building strike capacity.

**Campaign Present (Year 0):** Multiple converging crises. [[Aldric Harth]]'s debt to [[Corwin Marsh]] due in two months (lowland threat to highland grain control). [[Highland Workers Coalition]] planning strike (internal threat to council authority). Charcoal shortage threatening forge operations (resource depletion). Divine decay accelerating (supernatural threat from Stone Warden's remains). Highland independence fragile, pressured from below (coalition), above (creditors), and beneath (divine corruption).

## Connections to Other Regions

**South: The Riverlands** - Lowland river valley region, primary connection via Millrun River barge trade. [[Millford]] is the first lowland town, 15-20 miles downstream from Ashcrag. Grain, tools, and ore shipped downstream during barge seasons. Salt, cloth, and manufactured goods shipped back upstream via overland cart. Economic dependency: highlands produce, lowlands finance and distribute.

**Further South: The Saltfen** - Delta region where Millrun River meets the coast. [[Saltmere]] is the major port controlling lowland grain distribution. [[Saltmere Merchant Assembly]] (led by [[Corwin Marsh]]) finances highland trade, holds highland debt, and threatens to use Crown legitimacy to seize highland assets if debts default.

**North: Barren Peaks** - Beyond the northern peaks (the Stone Warden's spine), the terrain becomes inhospitable. No settlements, no resources worth extracting. Windswept rock, permanent snowpack, dangerous terrain. Occasionally explored by desperate prospectors or adventurers seeking lost divine artifacts. Stone-cursed creatures more common in this zone.

**East and West: Undefined Neighboring Regions** - Beyond the watershed divides lie other regions, currently unexplored and unnamed. Streams beyond these ridges drain into other watersheds. Future expansion possibilities.

## Campaign Role

The Stone Heights is a setting for highland-focused adventures and campaigns, particularly stories about class struggle, resource depletion, economic exploitation, and divine corruption:

**Highland Workers Coalition Strike:** Central conflict. PCs may be hired to mediate, spy, protect organizers, break the strike, or stay neutral. Outcome reshapes highland power dynamics. If strike succeeds, workers gain representation and improved conditions. If it fails, organizers are destroyed and cartel/council authority solidifies. If it escalates to violence, the region descends into chaos and lowland creditors intervene.

**Forge Council vs Cartel Economic Rivalry:** Competition over tool market, ore pricing, and control of Ironhollow. PCs may be drawn in as mercenaries, negotiators, or investigators. [[Myra Cadros]] wants to reclaim Ironhollow. [[Caden Wroth]] wants to expand cartel control. Conflict could escalate to sabotage, violence, or cartel-backed mercenaries.

**Lowland Debt Crisis:** [[Aldric Harth]]'s debt to [[Corwin Marsh]] is due in two months. If Marsh seizes the Harth Estate, lowland creditors control highland grain exports. PCs may help Aldric raise funds, negotiate terms, smuggle grain to pay debt, or expose his illegal smuggling to the council. Outcome determines whether highlands remain independent or fall under lowland economic control.

**Divine Decay and Supernatural Threats:** The Stone Warden's remains are destabilizing. Winters getting longer, ore behaving strangely, stone-cursed creatures appearing more frequently. PCs may investigate mine collapses caused by living ore veins, rescue travelers from cold sickness zones, hunt stone-cursed creatures in deep forests, or explore the northern peaks to discover the source of accelerating divine corruption. Campaign-scale threat: divine decay reaching critical point, triggering supernatural blizzard that lasts months or awakening something buried in the god's skull.

**Resource Depletion:** Forests thinning, ore veins exhausted, soil thin over bedrock. The highlands are running out of resources. PCs may be hired to scout new timber stands, explore deeper mines (risking ore madness and living veins), negotiate imports with lowland merchants, or help communities transition to sustainable practices before collapse.

> [!warning]- GM Notes: Secrets & Campaign Hooks

**Key Secrets:**

1. **Emergency grain reserve nearly depleted:** [[Myra Cadros]] is secretly rationing fuel and hiding the fact that the council's emergency grain reserve (established after the harsh winter 20 years ago) is down to 30% capacity. If this becomes public, it shatters her credibility and validates [[Orrin Pike]]'s accusations that the council prioritizes guild profit over community survival.

2. **Aldric smuggling grain to pay debt:** [[Aldric Harth]] is secretly smuggling grain to [[Saltmere]] via alternative routes, bypassing council market fees and barge contracts. He is breaking council law to raise funds to pay [[Corwin Marsh]]. If caught, he loses his council seat and possibly his estate. If successful, he survives but deepens lowland control over highland grain.

3. **Hal Brennan funding coalition anonymously:** Hal Brennan, [[Myra Cadros]]'s rival on the [[Forge Council]], is quietly funneling money and supplies to the [[Highland Workers Coalition]] through intermediaries. He sees the coalition as a way to undermine Myra's authority and position himself as a reformer. If exposed, it is a guild betrayal and political scandal.

4. **Mira Voss copying council ledgers:** [[Mira Voss]], one of the coalition's intelligence contacts in [[Ashcrag]], has been secretly copying [[Forge Council]] ledgers and passing financial data to [[Orrin Pike]]. The coalition knows exactly how much the council collects in fees, how much is stockpiled, and where the money goes. This information is leverage for strike negotiations.

5. **Surface ore depleting faster than cartel admits:** The [[Highland Merchant Cartel]] knows the surface ore veins in [[Ironhollow]] are nearly exhausted. Within 5-10 years, ore extraction will require deep mining, which is more dangerous, expensive, and exposes miners to ore madness and living veins. The cartel is pressuring [[Jorin Velk]] to sell his independent claim (richest remaining surface vein) because they need it to maintain production levels. If the cartel seizes Jorin's claim and the deep veins prove unworkable, the highland tool economy collapses.

6. **Divine decay accelerating:** The Stone Warden's remains are not stable. Winters are measurably longer (average 3.5 months isolation 50 years ago, 4.5 months now). Ore veins are developing living properties at increasing rates. Stone-cursed creatures are appearing closer to settlements. Something is wrong with the god's corpse. If divine decay continues at current rate, the Stone Heights may become uninhabitable within 50-75 years.

**Campaign Hooks:**

1. **Mine collapse at Ironhollow:** Six miners trapped in collapsed pit mine. [[Caden Wroth]] refuses to authorize rescue (too dangerous, not worth cost). Survivors and families beg PCs for help. Investigation reveals collapse caused by living ore vein that "closed" when miners struck it. PCs must decide whether to rescue (defying cartel) or walk away (miners die, resentment deepens).

2. **Barge escort through Millrun Falls:** [[Myra Cadros]] hires PCs to escort critical spring barge shipment through Millrun Falls rapids. High water, dangerous navigation, valuable cargo (tools, grain, emergency medical supplies). Sabotage risk: [[Highland Workers Coalition]] may attempt to block shipment to pressure council, or [[Highland Merchant Cartel]] may sabotage to damage Myra's reputation.

3. **Council-coalition mediation:** Both sides secretly contact PCs to mediate strike negotiations. [[Orrin Pike]] wants council seat for tenant farmers and land reform. [[Myra Cadros]] refuses but might accept compromise if pressured. PCs must navigate competing interests, prevent violence, and broker deal that both sides can accept. Failure results in strike collapse (coalition destroyed) or violent confrontation (region destabilizes).

4. **Cartel spy contract:** [[Caden Wroth]] or [[Tallow Breck]] hires PCs to infiltrate [[Highland Workers Coalition]], identify leaders and meeting sites, and report back. Moral test: help cartel crush coalition (profitable but evil) or refuse/double-cross (risky but principled). If PCs accept and succeed, coalition leaders arrested or killed, strike broken. If PCs refuse or betray cartel, they make powerful enemy.

5. **Northern peaks exploration:** Rumors of strange sounds, lights, and activity in the northern peaks (Stone Warden's spine). [[Myra Cadros]] or independent scholars hire PCs to investigate. PCs discover one of: (a) Stone-cursed creatures migrating closer to settlements, (b) Living ore veins spreading beyond Ironhollow, (c) Cult attempting to resurrect the Stone Warden, (d) The god's skull partially exposed, something inside still "alive." Campaign-scale threat revealed.

6. **Aldric's debt crisis:** [[Aldric Harth]] secretly approaches PCs for help. Debt to [[Corwin Marsh]] due in two months, cannot pay without smuggling grain (illegal) or selling estate (family ruin). PCs may help smuggle grain (risky, breaking council law), negotiate with Marsh (difficult, Assembly wants control not compromise), or expose Aldric to council (political scandal, Marsh seizes estate). Outcome determines whether lowland creditors gain foothold in highland grain control.
