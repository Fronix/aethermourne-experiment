## Overview

Millford sits astride the Millrun River where the current slows enough for a reliable ford crossing. The river flows south from the northern highlands, carrying grain barges downstream to lowland markets and bringing trade goods back upstream. The town exists because of the ford, but it thrives because of the mills.

Three water mills stand along a half-mile stretch of river just upstream of the ford. The steady gradient and reliable flow power massive grinding wheels that process grain from highland farms. The mills are the heart of Millford's economy, and the millers, who take a generous cut of every bushel processed, are the heart of its politics. The rest of the town, dockworkers and traders, farmers and ferrymen, orbits their decisions.

It's a working town. The smell of river water and grain dust. The rhythmic thump of mill wheels. Barges tied up at the docks waiting their turn to cross. The ford itself is wide and shallow, cobbled in the center where generations of wagon wheels have worn ruts into the stones. In summer, you can walk across. In spring floods, you wait.

> [!warning]- GM Only
> **The mill rivalry is the key tension.** [[Garrick Thane]] owns Ashfeld's Mill and Southgrain Mill, controlling two of the three. [[Brenna Cray]] owns Kess Mill, upstream. Water rights disputes and accusations of upstream damming create natural conflict. The village council is packed with Garrick's allies. Brenna's supporters include resentful farmers who see the millers as parasites.
>
> **The river itself is changing.** Over the past two seasons, the spring floods have been more severe, and the autumn flow has been weaker. Older residents don't remember it being this unpredictable. There's no supernatural cause yet established, but this is a thread for future development.

## Layout & Landmarks

### The Ford

The town's reason for existing. A wide, shallow stretch of river where the Millrun's flow spreads across a cobbled bed. In low water, the crossing is knee-deep. In flood season, it's impassable for days. A wooden toll house sits on the northern bank, staffed by [[Lyman Corvish]], the crown-appointed toll collector who manages crossing fees from traders. The south bank has a small waystation with stables and a feed store.

On both sides of the ford, wagon ruts have carved deep grooves into the approach roads. Traders heading south to lowland markets congregate on the north bank, waiting for their turn or for the water to drop. Heading north, highland-bound traders bringing salt, cloth, and tools wait on the south side.

### The Three Mills

**Kess Mill** (upstream, northern edge of town). The oldest of the three, built forty years ago by [[Brenna Cray]]'s grandfather. A sturdy stone structure with a single large wheel. Brenna runs it herself, living in the attached mill house. She's considered the finest miller in town for quality, but she processes less volume than the Ashfeld operation. Her position upstream gives her first access to the current, a fact Garrick resents.

**Ashfeld's Mill** (mid-river, town center). [[Garrick Thane]]'s primary operation. A larger building with two grinding wheels, rebuilt and expanded ten years ago when Garrick bought out a failing competitor. Ashfeld's takes the bulk of commercial grain contracts. The building doubles as Garrick's home, a two-story structure that looms over the docks.

**Southgrain Mill** (downstream, southern edge). [[Garrick Thane]]'s second mill, smaller and older. He bought it five years ago from a widow who couldn't keep up with repairs. It's staffed by his younger brother, Kellan Thane, who handles overflow work and undercuts Kess Mill on price. Some farmers say Southgrain's flour tastes of river mud, but it's cheap.

The three mills stand like rival keeps along the riverbank. The sound of their wheels, out of sync with each other, creates a constant rolling thunder across the town.

### The Docks

Two sets of docks bracket the ford. The **Upper Docks**, just downstream of Southgrain Mill, handle northbound barges waiting to unload cargo before the crossing. The **Lower Docks**, on the south bank past the ford, handle southbound grain barges loading up for the lowland run.

The docks are rough-built wooden platforms with mooring posts and covered storage sheds. The dock master, [[Halford Quick]], keeps a small office on the Upper Docks and collects mooring fees on behalf of the village council. He's also the town's unofficial peacekeeper when traders get into disputes. [[Dace Torrn]], a well-known barge captain and river trader, moors here frequently and serves as an informal liaison between the trading community and the council.

### Mill Street

The main road through town runs parallel to the river, connecting the three mills. It's lined with grain storage buildings, a smithy, a cooper's shop (barrel makers are essential for trade), a tavern called **The Crossing House**, and a general goods store. Most of the town's 600 residents live in cottages and farmsteads spreading back from the river into surrounding fields.

### The Council Hall

A modest two-story timber building on Mill Street where the village council meets monthly. The council is made up of five members: both millers ([[Garrick Thane]] and [[Brenna Cray]], who rarely agree on anything), [[Halford Quick]] (the dock master), and two farmers elected annually. [[Osmund Vey]], a senior farmer and respected community voice, frequently speaks for the farming interests at meetings. Meetings are open, frequently loud, and heavily attended by farmers with grievances about miller's cuts.

## People & Culture

Millford is a town of workers and merchants. Most residents are farmers who bring their grain to the mills, dockworkers who handle the barge trade, or craftspeople who support the milling economy, coopers, smiths, cart-wrights. The rhythm of the town follows the river: busy in spring and autumn when the water is high and the barges run frequently, slower in winter when the highland roads freeze and trade drops off.

There's little room for sentiment here. Farmers resent the millers for taking a fifth of every bushel processed, but they need the mills, the nearest alternative is two days away. Traders resent the toll collectors, but they need the ford. The millers resent each other's success, but they need the council to maintain the ford and keep the law. It's a web of resentment and necessity, held together by mutual dependence.

People in Millford respect hard work, distrust outsiders who don't bring trade, and have strong opinions about water rights. Religious practice is minimal, though there's a small shrine to river spirits near the Upper Docks, maintained by older dockworkers who leave offerings before flood season.

### Notable Residents

**[[Garrick Thane]]**, owner of Ashfeld's Mill and Southgrain Mill. Mid-40s, broad-shouldered, with the confident bearing of a man who controls the town's economy. He sits on the village council and owns enough political influence to control most votes. Wants to buy out Kess Mill and consolidate all milling under his operation.

**[[Brenna Cray]]**, owner of Kess Mill. Late 30s, sharp-eyed, and deeply stubborn. She inherited the mill from her grandfather and runs it with a reputation for quality and fairness, she charges lower miller's fees than Garrick. Fights every water rights dispute and votes against Garrick on every council matter. Farmers trust her more, but Garrick has more power.

**[[Halford Quick]]**, dock master and council member. Older man, maybe 60, with a weathered face and a talent for keeping the peace between traders. Neutral in the mill rivalry, which makes him valuable as a tiebreaker on the council. Knows every barge captain who runs the Millrun and keeps mental records of who owes favors to whom.

**Kellan Thane**, [[Garrick Thane]]'s younger brother, runs Southgrain Mill. Less ambitious than his brother, more interested in drinking at The Crossing House than managing the mill. Some say he's embezzling from the mill profits, others say Garrick keeps him underpaid on purpose. Either way, Southgrain's reputation for poor quality reflects on Kellan's work.

## Tensions & Hooks

1. **Water rights war.** [[Brenna Cray]] accuses [[Garrick Thane]] of building an illegal weir upstream of Ashfeld's Mill to divert more current to his wheels, reducing flow to Southgrain and hurting her mill's power. Garrick denies it, but the council (which he controls) won't investigate. Brenna is considering sabotage. Farmers are choosing sides, and the next council meeting could erupt into violence.

2. **Toll rebellion.** A group of traders has started refusing to pay ford crossing tolls to [[Lyman Corvish]], claiming the council does nothing to maintain the cobbles and the ford is degrading. [[Halford Quick]] has threatened to bar them from the docks, but the traders have support from farmers (led by [[Osmund Vey]]) who resent council spending on mill subsidies instead of ford repairs. If the traders organize a blockade, Millford's economy collapses.

3. **The missing barge.** A grain barge heading downstream three weeks ago never arrived at the lowland markets. The barge captain's family is demanding the council send a search party. [[Halford Quick]] suspects pirates or a sinking in rough water, but some dockworkers (and [[Dace Torrn]], who knows the river better than most) whisper about "the pull", a strange current that appears during autumn low water and drags vessels off course. No one wants to talk about it openly.

4. **Kellan's debt.** [[Garrick Thane]]'s brother Kellan owes money to a trader from the lowlands, a man named Ossian Greave who's come to Millford to collect. Kellan claims he can't pay. Ossian is threatening to take Southgrain Mill as collateral. Garrick refuses to bail his brother out, but if Ossian seizes the mill, it disrupts Garrick's consolidation plans. Ossian is staying at The Crossing House and making increasingly public threats.

5. **Flood season fears.** The Millrun's spring floods have been growing more severe each year. Last spring, the ford was impassable for two weeks, and the Lower Docks were damaged. Older residents say the river's changing, getting angrier. Some blame the mills for disrupting the current. Others say it's just weather. The next flood season is two months away, and no one has a plan if it's worse than last year.

> **Hook 1 (Water rights)** can lead to: sabotage, council corruption investigation, farmer uprising, or PC mediation.
>
> **Hook 3 (Missing barge)** is the river mystery thread. "The pull" is real, a supernatural current tied to something downstream (undefined for now, but a clear implication for future building). Can lead to river exploration, lowland market investigation, or supernatural discovery.
>
> **Hook 5 (Floods)** connects to the river's changing behavior. This could be a natural cycle, or it could be the first sign of something larger in the world. Keep this vague for now, it's a slow-burn thread.
